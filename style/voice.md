# Voice Bible

**North star:** A studio-rat friend who reads Tape Op and Sound on Sound, hates marketing copy, and will not suffer the word *journey*.

Reference cadence: Ira Glass deadpan × Steve Albini interview dryness × a pinch of BBC-radio-engineer matter-of-factness. Technical, gear-specific, lightly sardonic. Zero marketing copy. Zero hype. Zero emojis.

---

## The three rules

1. **Concrete before abstract.** "Fostex M80 into a Tascam MSR16 at 7½ IPS" beats "analog warmth." If you're about to write a mood word, name a model number instead.
2. **Deflate before inflating.** If you're about to praise a technique, name its limitation in the same paragraph. Never end a section on a pure cheer.
3. **Gear is named; feelings are not.** Prefer model numbers to mood words. The reader is an adult who can decide whether it sounds good.

---

## Approved structural patterns

- **Cold open with a confession or contradiction.** "Aphex's drums feel wrong because they're *right* in tune." Not "Welcome to Week 5!"
- **Receipt-first.** Cite before claiming. "Tape Op 89, page 34 — Jenkinson says…" NOT "Jenkinson famously said…"
- **Close with an exercise or a shrug.** No triumphant wrap-up. "Tune a kick to root + a snare to the fifth. Thirty seconds. Go."
- **Caveats inline, not at the end.** Flag uncertainty where it lives. If the forum says he used a QY700 but nobody can confirm, write "almost certainly a QY700 — this is forum consensus, not a confirmed interview" in the same sentence, not a footnote.

---

## Tone calibration examples

Read these. Write in this voice.

> "The thing about Squarepusher is he does not like you — or anyone — watching the screen while he works. That's the whole plot."

> "Henke put a granulator on the market and then personally mixed a record on laptop speakers. Pick a lane, Robert."

> "This week we steal from Four Tet. Specifically, the fifteen-percent randomization trick he admits to on Tape Notes 140 and then downplays for the next forty minutes."

> "Everyone tells you Autechre used Max. Nobody tells you Booth had to turn the screen off to finish *Confield*."

> "Before we touch Roar: an apology. The marketing calls it a saturator. It is three distortions taped together with a feedback loop. That is better."

> "The Fridmann bass is not a plugin. It is a Dorrough 610 abused in ways its broadcast-engineer designers did not authorize."

---

## Voice DOs

- Use em-dashes for asides (`—`). Prefer them to parentheses.
- Use first-person plural sparingly ("we"), first-person singular almost never. The narrator is a guide, not a protagonist.
- Specify brand + model + year whenever a piece of gear is first named. After that, model only.
- Pull quotes from interviews when you can. Lift them verbatim where length allows, citing source.
- Describe processes as *choices* — "Fridmann chose the Dorrough because the distortion characteristic was musical" — not as magic.

## Voice DON'Ts

- Do not use exclamation points. Ever.
- Do not use emojis. Ever.
- Do not write "AI-powered," "AI-enhanced," or any AI-marketing phrasing in student-facing copy.
- Do not use the word "journey." Do not use "sonic journey." Do not use "take you on a journey."
- Do not over-caveat. One honest "unconfirmed" beats three "it is said that."
- Do not end lessons with a motivational kicker. End with the exercise, then stop talking.
- Do not repeat teaser lore across weeks. Each teaser lands once. The calendar enforces.

---

## Scoping the "slightly irreverent"

Irreverent means: willing to point out that a device's marketing name is silly, willing to admit that a famous producer's signature move is sometimes just stubbornness, willing to name when a technique has a real cost.

Irreverent does NOT mean: cheap jokes, self-deprecation as filler, winking at the reader, cool-guy posturing, insulting other producers. The reader is a peer. The narrator is not trying to be liked — they're trying to be useful.

---

## Reading for Ableton concepts specifically

Ableton device names get weird. Roar. Meld. Operator. Wavetable. Simpler. Treat the names as the engineers who shipped them intended — as tools with verbs attached. "Run the signal through Roar." "Set Simpler to Slicing mode." The device is a noun that does a thing. Never mythologize.
