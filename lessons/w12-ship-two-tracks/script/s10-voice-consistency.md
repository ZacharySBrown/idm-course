Twelve weeks in, you have twelve pieces. Listen back-to-back and some of them will sound like they were made by twelve different producers. [pause 400ms]

That is the point of the course, and also the problem you now have.

[pause 400ms]

Voice, in this context — and I am using the word *voice* deliberately over *style* or *sound* — is not the one clever thing that was unique to one track. Voice is what *recurs*. Across tracks. Without you noticing.

[pause 500ms]

Concrete examples, from this twelve-week curriculum. [pause 200ms] If you used the kick-to-the-root move from Week five, and it showed up again in Week seven when you were chopping the Amen break, and it showed up a third time in Week ten when you were generating variations — that is voice. Three times is a pattern. Two is a coincidence. One is an experiment.

[pause 400ms]

If you left two full bars of silence before the drop in your Week eight Autechre-inspired piece, and then you found yourself doing it again in Week eleven's beat switch — that is voice. If the Fridmann-bass-distortion setting you discovered in Week four became the plugin you reached for every time the low end felt thin across the remaining weeks — that is voice.

[pause 500ms]

What is *not* voice. [pause 200ms] The Week nine vocal-chop experiment you never did again. The Week two subtraction exercise that produced a track you cannot listen to. The Week eleven beat switch that only worked because the stem happened to cooperate on that particular source.

[pause 400ms]

Voice is what you would do *without thinking*, if you had to make a record in two days and could not afford to experiment. [pause 300ms] The twelve-week archive tells you what those defaults actually are — which is frequently not what you told yourself your defaults were. Rubin makes this point, in different language, repeatedly across *The Creative Act* — the act of looking at what you already made is how you learn what you are going to make next.

[pause 400ms]

~The archive tells on you.~ That is the next exercise.
