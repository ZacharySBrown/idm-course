LUFS. Four letters, one unit, most of the modern loudness conversation. [pause 300ms]

Loudness Units relative to Full Scale. ITU recommendation BS.1770 is the standard — the spec that every LUFS meter, on every platform, obeys. I am flagging BS.1770 as a needed bibliography entry because it is worth citing for students who want to see the math under the hood.

[pause 400ms]

Two practical targets.

First. Minus fourteen LUFS integrated. That is the normalization target Spotify and Apple Music both use as of this writing. Here I have to be honest about the receipt. I could not resolve a first-party Spotify or Apple engineering page in the bibliography — treat the minus fourteen as industry consensus pending those citations, which are on the `needs_bib` list. The number is reliable. The source discipline around it is not, in this lesson, yet.

[pause 300ms]

What does minus fourteen mean in practice? It means if you master to, say, minus seven LUFS — which is loud, which is radio-era loud — Spotify will turn you down by seven decibels before playback. Your master is not *louder* on Spotify. It is just more squashed relative to the reference tracks around it on a playlist. That is the loudness-war wager, and post-normalization it is mostly a losing wager.

[pause 400ms]

Second. Minus nine to minus eleven LUFS integrated, for club and loud-listening contexts. This is not normalized. A DJ loads your track into a controller, hears it at the level you printed it, and your dynamic range is what they work with. [pause 200ms] Under these conditions, louder is a real choice, not a vanity one.

[pause 300ms]

If your two finished tracks are headed for streaming, master to minus fourteen. If one is a DJ tool, master *that one* to minus ten and keep a separate streaming master. Two tracks, potentially two targets.
