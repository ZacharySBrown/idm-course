Self-master or send it to an engineer. [pause 300ms] The decision is not about skill. It is about *finishing*.

A line circulates in producer forums, attributed to Dave Fridmann — *"if it's not finished, it's not mastered."* [pause 300ms] I want to be honest about the source, because this is the course's last lesson and the receipt discipline matters more here than anywhere else. I could not find a verbatim published Fridmann quote with that wording in either of the research specs I worked from, or in the current bibliography. The sentiment is consistent with Fridmann's public interviews and with davefridmann dot com's archival posture, but treat the wording as *community paraphrase*, flagged on the `needs_bib` list for a future bibliography pass.

[pause 400ms]

The meaning, regardless of wording, holds. Mastering is not a rescue operation. Mastering is a *translation* pass applied to a mix that has already been decided. [pause 200ms] If the low end is not committed — if you are still deciding whether the sub goes under the kick or off to the side — a mastering engineer cannot fix that. If you have three different arrangement options in muted tracks and have not picked one, no amount of Pro-L 2 will pick for you.

[pause 500ms]

The honest test is one question. *Do you know what the track is supposed to sound like.* [pause 300ms] If you do — if the mix is decided, the arrangement is decided, the low end is committed, the vocal is the vocal — then self-master this week. Use the tools we just walked through. Translate. Move on.

[pause 300ms]

If you do not — if the track still has open decisions — do not send it out. An engineer charges money. An unfinished mix stays unfinished on their desk too. They can polish; they cannot compose. Finish the mix first. Then decide.

[pause 400ms]

For the twelve-week course, self-master both tracks. Next year, with the four-track EP candidates we are about to identify, you can make that call per track. [pause 300ms] Most of the ones you finish will not need an outside engineer. The one or two that are meant for vinyl, or for a commercial release, or for a context that demands a neutral ear — send those.
