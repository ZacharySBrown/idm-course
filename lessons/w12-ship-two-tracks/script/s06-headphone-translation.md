Your mastering job is not to make the track sound great on *your* headphones. [pause 300ms]

It is to make the track sound acceptable on *everyone else's* headphones, and their phone speakers, and their car, and the cheap Bluetooth speaker on their bathroom counter. That is *translation*. The word is load-bearing.

[pause 400ms]

Two headphone pairs worth owning. Neither is expensive. Both have been measured, independently, many times.

First. The AKG K371. Closed-back, studio reference, originally marketed as a broadcast headphone. Frequency response sits close to the Harman target curve within plus or minus three decibels from forty hertz up to ten kilohertz, per independent measurements by the Crinacle and oratory1990 communities. [pause 200ms] I could not find a canonical first-party measurement URL for the bibliography — flagged as needs-bib — but the data is consistent across multiple independent rigs. Good low-end translation down to about forty-five hertz. Isolates well because it is closed. The cable is ugly. That does not affect the frequency response.

[pause 400ms]

Second. The Sennheiser HD600. Open-back. Thirty-plus years old as a reference. Honest midrange, flat through the vocal range, detail in the upper mids that reveals sibilance and reverb tails. [pause 200ms] The weakness is well-known — the HD600 rolls off below about fifty hertz. You cannot trust it for kick fundamentals. Pair it with the K371, or with a single check in a car or on a studio monitor with proper sub extension.

[pause 400ms]

Protocol. Mix on your main monitors. A/B the master on the K371 closed-back. Spot-check on a phone speaker — literally the built-in speaker on an iPhone or a Pixel. If the kick disappears on one check-point and booms on another, something is wrong in your low-mid balance. [pause 300ms]

~Your reference tracks go through the same three check-points.~ If their master translates and yours does not, the difference is in your master, not in the room.
