Open the Sample tab on any warped clip and you get a dropdown. Six items. Before we do any A/B-ing, let's just name them. [pause 200ms]

**Beats.** A slicer pretending to be a warp mode. It detects transients, slices the audio at every one, plays each slice at its original speed, and truncates or loops the tail to hit the next grid mark. Punch stays intact. Anything tonal — held chords, vocals, pads — turns into a stuttering mess.

**Tones.** A monophonic granular engine with pitch tracking. Built for vocals, solo basslines, lead synths. Anything where there is one note at a time and you care about the pitch being stable. Falls apart on chords.

**Texture.** A *polyphonic* granular engine. Chords welcome. Drones welcome. It has two creative controls — Grain Size and Flux — that we'll spend a whole slide on later. Short version: Texture is the mode you reach for when the goal is not preservation but *mutation*.

**Re-Pitch.** No time-stretching. None. Pitch and tempo locked together. This is the software version of a tape varispeed. Pull the pitch up, the clip gets shorter and higher; push it down, longer and lower. This is the mode the Roc-A-Fella production camp used to build a subgenre. We will get there.

**Complex.** The legacy phase-vocoder. Ableton left it in for backward compatibility. Do not use it. Use the next one.

**Complex Pro.** Phase-vocoder with two added controls — Formants and Envelope. Full mixes, polyphonic sources, anything Texture makes too weird. This is your workhorse mode.

[pause 250ms] Rule of thumb — and write this one down.

~If the audio is mostly transient, Beats.~ [pause 150ms] Mostly tonal — Tones or Complex Pro. Mostly drone — Texture. Want a tape deck — Re-Pitch.
