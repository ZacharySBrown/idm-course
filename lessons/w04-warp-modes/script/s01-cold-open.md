Confession to open the week. [pause 300ms]

Ableton's Warp function sits in the Sample tab next to Fade, Gain, and a tempo field. The Live 12 manual — chapter on *Clip View*, sub-head *Warp Modes* — files it under time-stretching. Every tutorial you will ever watch on YouTube, every blog post, every Discord explainer will tell you the same thing: Warp is for making samples line up with your project tempo.

[pause 200ms] That is correct. It is also a waste of the feature.

Consider what actually happens when you flip a clip from Beats mode to Complex Pro. You did not *tempo-match* the clip — tempo-matching is trivial, it happens automatically the moment you draw a warp marker. What you did was pick a *resynthesis algorithm*. A phase vocoder. And the phase vocoder has a personality. It smears transients. It preserves harmonic content. It gently softens everything that was sharp.

Flip to Texture mode and you replaced the phase vocoder with a granular engine — different personality again. Different artifacts. Different failure modes.

[pause 250ms] So the framing we're going to hold for the next forty-odd minutes is this: *Warp mode is a sound-design surface.* Not a utility. Not a convenience. ~An instrument choice.~ Six of them, each with its own cost.

We will work exclusively on one stem today. The drum bed from NEU's "Hallogallo," isolated via stemforge. I will tell you in a minute why that stem in particular. [pause 200ms] But the thing I want you attending to, across every demo: transient shape, formant content, noise floor. Those are the three dimensions that *change*. If you cannot hear one of them shifting, listen again. Something shifted.
