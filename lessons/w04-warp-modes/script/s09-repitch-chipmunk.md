Re-Pitch. [pause 200ms] The simplest warp mode in Live and, arguably, the most historically important.

It does not time-stretch. That is the whole description. Speed up the clip and the pitch goes up with it. Slow it down and the pitch drops. Pitch and time are *locked*. One semitone up equals roughly a six-percent reduction in length. An octave up halves the clip; an octave down doubles it.

If you grew up on tape, you already know this behavior — it is the varispeed control on a reel-to-reel, or the pitch fader on a Technics 1200. [pause 200ms] Re-Pitch is those devices, in software.

Here is why it matters. [pause 250ms]

In 1999, a producer named Just Blaze, working in New York with a young Kanye West and a handful of cracked copies of Pro Tools, was looking for a way to take a soul record — Chuck Jackson, The Persuaders, whoever — and turn a four-bar sample into something that sounded *wrong enough* to be a hook. Kanye's move was to pitch it up four, five, six, sometimes seven semitones. Not time-stretched. Pitched. Coupled with the tempo. [pause 200ms] The result — baritone soul singers turned into yelping choirboys — became a signature. **Chipmunk soul**. The compass notes quote Just Blaze on the RBMA lecture archive: quote — "The way me, Kanye and Just chopped it, it was: we'll get seven pieces of a record, put it back together, make the sample do the hook." End quote.

The critical detail — *coupled* pitch and tempo. A modern time-stretcher can pitch audio up without changing its duration. Re-Pitch refuses. That refusal *is* the sound. Split the two and you lose the character; the formants no longer move with the pitch, the transients no longer speed up, the hi-hats no longer get brittle. [pause 200ms]
