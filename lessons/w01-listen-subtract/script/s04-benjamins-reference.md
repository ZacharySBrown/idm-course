Benjamins. *benjamins dot wav.* A track that lives in the stemforge root on Zak's drive, and that the course is going to return to four times over twelve weeks.

[pause 400ms]

Why this track. Two reasons. One, it has the kind of mix where muting the drums actively changes the song — the bass is load-bearing, the vocal has enough air around it to hear the reverb, the *other* stem — which is what the separator calls "everything melodic and textural" — carries a surprising amount of the track's identity. Two, the stems separate cleanly. Some tracks do not. This one does.

The pipeline — *default, no-slice* — is a conservative Demucs-style separation. Four outputs: drums, bass, vocals, other. The *other* bucket is where the separator dumps anything it cannot confidently classify, which in practice means keys, synths, guitars, pads, sometimes backing vocals. Treating "other" as a legitimate stem rather than a bug is one of the quiet wins of the current generation of source separators.

[pause 400ms]

This week, Benjamins is the thing you listen to. In Week 4, you are going to re-open *benjamins_drums.wav* and warp it through Complex Pro versus Beats mode and hear how Ableton's Complex Pro softens transients on a kick in a way that Beats mode does not — that is a warp-modes lesson, not a listening lesson, and it assumes you already know how the drum stem sounds dry. In Week 8, we will use *benjamins_other.wav* as feedstock for a Roar feedback-loop resampling experiment. In Week 12, Benjamins is the A/B reference against which you are going to compare your two shipped tracks, to see whether the mixes you have made hold up next to a track that was — and this is stated without a lot of romance — made by someone who knew what they were doing.
