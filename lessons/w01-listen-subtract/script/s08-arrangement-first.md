A session template. Not aspirational. Load-for-every-session.

Locators at zero-zero intro, forty-five-seconds A section, two minutes B section, three minutes breakdown, three-forty-five peak, five minutes outro, five-forty-five end. These times are suggestions, not contracts — but the act of placing them, before you have any music, converts the session from open-ended into *finite.* You are committing to a shape before you know what fills it. That is the single rule that most reliably breaks the loop trap.

[pause 400ms]

A reference track on a muted audio lane, located at bar one. One Aphex, one Autechre, one Dilla, one Kendrick. A/B against the reference every twenty minutes. This is the OMPRD-382 protocol we discussed a few slides ago, operationalized as a template. You do not re-load the reference; it is already in the session.

Tracks pre-grouped. Drums Group with kick, snare, hats, perc inside. Bass Group, sub and mid-bass. Melody Group, lead and pad and arp. Texture Group, two field-recording or ambient slots. Vocal Group, two chop slots. Return A: a plate reverb, infinite setting. Return B: a tape-style delay. Return C: a granular send. Return D: a feedback-style saturator — Roar with a feedback shaper set low, if you are on Live 12.2 or later. None of these are running this week. They are there so that in Week 3, when you need them, you do not spend the next forty-five minutes building the template instead of the track.

Then, and this is the part that is going to feel strange: close the laptop lid. Route the monitors. Play the stems. Listen.

Sean Booth, Sound on Sound April 2004 — *the screen has to go off. It's an illusion that totally pollutes what you're thinking and what you're listening to.* Booth is describing a specific Autechre-era move — running Max patches and refusing to look at the waveforms — but the principle is portable. Your eyes are louder than your ears. Your ears are the ones you are trying to train.
