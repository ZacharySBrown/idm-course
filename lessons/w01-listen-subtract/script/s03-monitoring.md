The monitoring question. Let us handle it once, here, so it does not come up every week.

Four pieces of gear. You do not need all four. You need to pick two — one pair of speakers and one pair of headphones — and know them.

[pause 300ms]

*Yamaha HS5*, 2014, a five-inch near-field. The charitable way to describe the HS5 is "honest." The accurate way is "dull." This is a feature. The HS5 translates to laptop speakers because it *is* almost a laptop speaker with a tweeter bolted on. If a mix holds up on an HS5 it holds up in a Honda Civic. That is what it is for.

*Neumann KH80 DSP*, 2016. A better near-field. Room-corrected. Costs roughly twice the HS5. The tradeoff: if you do not have a treated room, the KH80's transparency will show you every flutter echo in your walls, and you will start believing that your mix has problems when your *room* has problems. Buy the KH80 when you have a room worth trusting.

*AKG K371*, 2019. Closed-back. Harman-target-ish. The cheap headphone that most publications agree on, which is rare. Good for tracking. Good for the late-night pass when the monitors would wake somebody up.

*Sennheiser HD600*, 1997. Open-back. Mid-forward. Still the reference your mastering engineer has sitting on the shelf in the hallway. The HD600 will not give you sub information — it literally cannot move that much air — but everything between two hundred hertz and eight kilohertz is unambiguous.

[pause 500ms]

Pick one pair of speakers and one pair of headphones. A/B between them every session. The goal is not *flat* — flat is a marketing word. The goal is *known*. You cannot mix against a surface whose lies you have not memorized. ~Sorry, every Gearspace thread.~

[pause 300ms]

The fifth option, which is not a piece of gear — your car. The car is where the mix has to survive. If you finish a session on the HS5 and then drive around the block listening on the car stereo, you are doing the same thing the mastering engineer at Tarbox Road does, with worse speakers and a better commute.
