Subtract like a producer.

This course will teach five arrangement methods — Subtraction, Variation Generation, Skeleton, Resample-and-Mutate, and Bounce-and-Arrange. You will use all five eventually. Subtraction gets introduced in Week 1 because it is the most *finish-friendly* of the five. The others require you to have ideas. Subtraction requires you to have one idea and the willingness to remove parts of it.

[pause 500ms]

The move, formally. Build your loop to its densest version first. The densest bar — the climactic one, the drop, the chorus, whatever you want to call it — is the *master state.* You then duplicate that master state across a full six-minute timeline, and you *delete or mute* elements to form every other section.

So: intro becomes everything muted except the texture layer. A section is texture plus drums. B section is texture plus drums plus bass. Breakdown drops the drums back out. Peak is the unmuted master. Outro is the inverse of the intro — back to texture only.

[pause 400ms]

This guarantees cohesion because every section is derived from the same master state, meaning every sound in the track shares its EQ, its reverb, its saturation, its *room.* It also sidesteps the anxiety of *what goes next,* because the answer is always "less of what you already have." That is a feature.

Next week you will actually do this with a loop of your own. This week, you hear it in someone else's finished track. Play Benjamins and try to identify which version of the loop the producer treated as the master state. It is almost always the chorus. Rarely the drop. Occasionally the second verse, in songs where the second verse is where the arrangement finally blooms.

[pause 300ms]

Rick Rubin has a line about this that both of our specs cite — *"Perfection is obtained when there's no longer anything to take away."* Rubin is paraphrasing Saint-Exupéry, and the attribution is a little overused in producer circles, but the working principle is right. Finish by removing. You do not need another layer. You need to hear what the track already has.
