The course begins with silence.

[pause 600ms]

Specifically — the silence after you mute the drums on *Benjamins* and realize the bass was doing most of the work. [pause 400ms] You will do this exercise in about twenty minutes and it will embarrass you a little, which is the point.

This is not, yet, a production course. It is a listening course that happens to use Ableton. The distinction matters because almost every intermediate-producer mistake a student makes in weeks three through nine of a twelve-week program traces back to a Week 1 that did not happen. They open synths too fast. They build sounds before they know what a mix *is*. They spend six hours on a kick in isolation and wonder why the kick does not sit in the mix when the mix does not yet exist.

[pause 400ms]

So we are going to spend Week 1 not opening any synths.

You are going to load four stems of a track called Benjamins — the stemforge pipeline already produced them, you do not need to run anything — you are going to mute one stem, then another, then another. You are going to write down, on paper, one sentence per stem. What it is doing rhythmically. What it is doing harmonically. And then you are going to bounce a thirty-second clip in which the drums are muted for the first fifteen seconds and un-muted for the second fifteen, and that clip is the deliverable.

[pause 300ms]

Twelve weeks from now you will have shipped twelve tracks. This week you ship a listening exercise. ~Exciting.~ It is, actually, if you treat it seriously.

The rest of the course — Week 2's Subtraction method, Week 5's microtuning, Week 7's Amen-chopping, Week 12's mastering — all of it assumes you finished Week 1. Do not skip Week 1.
