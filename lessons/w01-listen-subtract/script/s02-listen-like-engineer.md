Listen like an engineer. The phrase is overused, but there is an actual protocol underneath it, and it is less romantic than the phrase suggests.

Berklee Online, course code OMPRD-382, Critical Listening and Analysis. One paragraph in the syllabus. *Load reference and work-in-progress on adjacent tracks in your DAW. Level-match via LUFS meter. Listen in thirty-second chunks. Switch.* That is the method. It is taught identically at Point Blank, at EDMProd, in every intermediate-producer curriculum that has ever bothered to teach listening as a separate skill from mixing.

[pause 400ms]

The five axes you write notes on — *low-end energy, stereo width, vocal-and-lead clarity, transient sharpness, reverb depth.* Not *mood.* Not *vibe.* Not *energy.* Those words are useful in retrospect; they are useless while you are listening.

Low-end energy means: is the sub doing active work, or is it implied by the body of the kick? If you mute the sub and the track still has weight, the kick body is doing the job. If you mute the sub and the track feels anemic, the sub was load-bearing.

Stereo width means: mono at eighty hertz because physics, usually widening through the low mids, collapsing again at the vocal because the vocal is center-panned and loud. You listen for where the stereo field pinches.

Transient sharpness is the difference between a click and a thump, and reverb depth is the difference between a dry snare, a fifteen-millisecond slap, and a two-second plate tail.

[pause 400ms]

Notes get written on *paper*. This is not a nostalgia move. Sean Booth said it plainly in Sound on Sound April 2004 — and I am reading this quote, from the interview — *"the screen has to go off. It's an illusion that totally pollutes what you're thinking and what you're listening to."*

Booth is talking about Autechre's Max patches, but the principle generalizes. Your eyes will tell you that the waveform looks loud. Your ears know whether it *is* loud. Trust the ears.

[pause 400ms]

The protocol is cheap. Load, level-match, switch, write. Five axes, thirty seconds each. Nobody's going to grade your handwriting.
