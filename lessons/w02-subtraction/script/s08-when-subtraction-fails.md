[pause 250ms] Here are three situations where Subtraction is the wrong method.

One. *Your Peak is thin.* If the densest state of your track is already sparse, every section derived from it will be sparser. Muting a thin Peak gives you an inaudible A section. Either go back and fix the Peak — which is a Writing session, not an Arrangement session, and therefore a separate day — or switch methods. Skeleton, where you work line-by-line across sections, handles sparseness better. Handbook 5.5.

[pause 200ms] Two. *The track wants a new idea at bar 80.* Subtraction forbids new elements. That is its rule. If the track is asking for a second motif to show up after the Reduction — a counter-melody, a new pad, a vocal line you hadn't planned — Subtraction will prevent you from writing it. That is a Variation Generation session. Five mutations of the loop, arranged as distinct sections. Handbook 5.4. We do that in Week 10.

Three. *You cannot commit to a densest state.* If your Peak keeps changing every time you open the session, you are still in the Writing mentality, not the Arrangement mentality. [pause 150ms] Close the session. Write on a different day. Come back with a bounced loop and a reference you will not renegotiate.

[pause 300ms] *The two-mentality problem.* Handbook section 1 and section 6.1 both lean on this. Writing, Arrangement, Sound Design, and Mixing are four different sessions. Treat them as four. Subtraction is an Arrangement-mentality method. If you are still writing, Subtraction is going to feel like a straitjacket — because it is one. That's the whole point.
