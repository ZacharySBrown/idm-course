[pause 300ms] The assignment. Read carefully; it is exact.

[pause 200ms] Step one. Take your most recent sixteen-bar loop. *Not a new one.* The last one you made, even if you don't love it. Especially if you don't love it. The point of this exercise is to demonstrate that Subtraction converts an adequate loop into a track, not that Subtraction requires a great loop. Use what you have.

[pause 250ms] Step two. Write the Subtraction arrangement **on paper.** Index card, notebook, napkin — analog surface. Six sections: Intro, A, B, Reduction, Peak, Outro. For each section, list which elements drop. This takes five minutes. Do not open the DAW until the paper plan exists. [pause 150ms] ~The paper step is the one most students skip, and it is the one that saves the most time downstream.~

[pause 200ms] Step three. Open Ableton. Drop the seven locators from slide 3 at the correct timecodes. Drag the loop into Arrangement View and duplicate it across six minutes. Peak state everywhere.

[pause 200ms] Step four. Start a sixty-minute timer. One hour. Mute backward. Do not add elements. Do not open a synth. Do not sound-design. If the hour ends and you're not done, stop anyway. You'll ship what you have.

[pause 150ms] Step five. Insert one bar of true silence somewhere between bar thirty-two and bar eighty. Your choice where. No reverb tail on the outgoing bar — a hard cut.

[pause 250ms] Step six. Bounce to mp3 at 192 kilobits per second. Select a three-minute window from the middle of the six — usually around bar sixteen to bar forty-eight — and export. Three minutes. Not four. Not two fifty-five. Three.

[pause 200ms] Step seven. Upload the mp3 before next session. Paper plan goes in the thread next to the file, photographed if you drew it.

[pause 300ms] *What we are grading.* That the file exists. That is the entire criterion. Not tone, not groove, not mix translation. File exists, or file does not exist. Handbook 6.1. [pause 200ms] Go.
