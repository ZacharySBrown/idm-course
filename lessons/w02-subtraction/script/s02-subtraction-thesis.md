[pause 300ms] Subtraction, stated plainly.

You build the single densest loop your track will ever contain. Call it the Peak. Everything on, every layer audible, everything you wanted in the song, all happening at once, for sixteen bars. [pause 150ms] Then you duplicate that loop across a six-minute timeline, end-to-end. For about thirty seconds the session sounds exhausting — because it is six straight minutes of climax, with no relief. [pause 200ms] That is the starting position.

From there you *mute backward*. Every other section — the Intro, the A section, the B section, the Reduction, the Outro — is made by switching tracks **off**, not by writing new material. The A section becomes "the Peak with the lead and the vocal chop muted." The Intro becomes "the Peak with everything muted except the texture and one bass note." The Reduction becomes "the Peak with the drums and bass dropped."

[pause 200ms] *Two things this buys you.* ~One~ — cohesion is free. Every section of the track is derived from the same master state, which means every section shares DNA. You cannot accidentally write a chorus that does not belong to the verse, because there is no chorus; there is only the Peak, partially muted. [pause 150ms] ~Two~ — the "what goes next" question, which is the question that kills most tracks at bar 32, now has exactly one answer. *Less of what you already have.*

Source on this. Zak Handbook, section 5.3. The method is not mine — it comes out of the Finish More Music school and matches the way Four Tet and Boards of Canada describe their own linear-accretion approach. Hebden, again on `Tape Notes` 140, describes it as "decide which things ride through the track in a simple way." That is the Peak, plus a constant anchor, which we will hit in about three slides.

[pause 250ms] So. Step one is *build the Peak*. Step two is *place the locators*. Step three is *mute backward*. We're going to do them in that order, starting with the locators, because the locators come before you touch a single sound.
