Last week we talked about listening — about taking a finished record apart with your ears before you took one apart with a stem splitter. That was diagnostic. This week is the opposite. This week is construction. Specifically, the first of the five arrangement methods the course will give you — the Subtraction method.

~Why lead with Subtraction?~ [pause 150ms] Because the single most common failure mode for intermediate producers is the loop trap. You make a great eight-bar loop. You add a hat. You add another hat. You *EQ* the second hat. Three hours pass. You still have an eight-bar loop. The track is not failing because the loop is bad. The track is failing because nothing has been *arranged*.

Subtraction is the method that fixes this the fastest, because it inverts the usual move. You do not build up. You build the densest state first — call it the Peak — and then you *mute backward* into the quieter sections. The A section is not something you write; the A section is the Peak with three things turned off.

[pause 200ms] By the end of this lesson you will have placed seven locators on an Arrangement View timeline, built a sixteen-bar Peak loop, and muted your way to a six-minute arrangement. [pause 100ms] Deliverable at the end: a three-minute mp3, built in one hour. Not a good three-minute mp3. A *shipped* three-minute mp3. That distinction is load-bearing.

[pause 300ms] Prereq for this lesson is Week 1 — "Listen like an engineer, subtract like a producer." If you skipped it, go back. The ear-training there is the ear-training this method depends on. [pause 200ms] Okay. Let's go.
