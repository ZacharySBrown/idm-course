[pause 300ms] The eight-bar rule. [pause 200ms]

Standard dance music changes something every eight bars. That is the baseline. A hat comes in, a filter opens, a clap joins — some signal that the section has advanced. IDM changes something every two to four bars, but it keeps a constant anchor underneath so the listener does not fall off the track. We just covered the anchor. This is the other half.

[pause 250ms] *The rule, compressed.* If you have enjoyed a loop for eight bars, break it. Rick Rubin, via every producer who ever shipped a record, via Zak Handbook section 5.9. The moment you catch yourself wanting to hear it again is the cue to cut — not to tweak further. Your pleasure with the loop is the alarm. ~It is a surprisingly hard alarm to obey.~

[pause 200ms] *How to break it inside Subtraction, where you are not allowed to add new elements.* Three moves.

One. Mute one layer for bars nine through sixteen. Re-enable it for bars seventeen through twenty-four. Now the same loop has a variation without any new content.

Two. Duplicate the drum clip in place and nudge it back one sixteenth-note for bars twenty-five through thirty-two. Same notes, new feel. Works best on hats and perc, not on kick.

Three. Insert one bar of true silence at bar forty-eight. No reverb tail. No tape stop. Just a gap. [pause 150ms] *Silence is compositional.* Rubin, paraphrasing Saint-Exupéry — and I am flagging this one in `needs_bib:` because the attribution in the handbook is third-hand — "Perfection is obtained when there is no longer anything to take away."

[pause 250ms] *A single bar of silence at bar forty-eight will do more than any fill you could write.* Autechre's *Tri Repetae* leans on two-plus bars of near-silence as structural punctuation. Aphex's track simply titled *#3* does the same. Most producers are scared of silence because they read it as "nothing happening." In Subtraction, silence is an element — and frankly, the most dramatic one you have. Handbook 5.9.
