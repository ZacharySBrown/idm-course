Mantra. "Good is good enough. Finished is better than perfect. Ship the track." [pause 300ms]

This is philosophy, but it is also operational. The reason it belongs in a week on Subtraction is that Subtraction without completion discipline is just another way to get stuck. You can happily spend eight hours fine-tuning the mute map, A/B'ing bar thirty-two against bar ninety-six, refusing to bounce. You will end the session with no mp3. That is the failure mode. [pause 250ms]

*Four operational rules that make this method actually ship.*

[pause 150ms] One. Forty-five-minute time-box on any single element. Kitchen timer on the desk. Not the laptop — a physical timer. When it rings, you commit. You bounce. You do not negotiate with the timer. Handbook 6.1.

Two. Andrew Huang's difference threshold. Quote from the handbook: "When I start making volume adjustments of less than half a decibel, I know I'm finished." [pause 150ms] Adjustments below your perceptual threshold are perfectionism pretending to be progress. Stop the session.

Three. Eno's Oblique Strategies. Physical card deck on the desk. `bib:oblique_strategies_eno`. [pause 100ms] Five cards that matter for this week: "Repetition is a form of change." "Cut a vital connection." "What mistakes did you make last time?" "Honor thy error as a hidden intention." "Just carry on." When you are thirty minutes into tweaking a kick and hate the kick, pull one. Eno was religious about these; you can afford to be too.

Four. Bounce in Place the Peak loop *before* you start muting. Ableton Live 12 reference manual, Arrangement View, Consolidate and Bounce in Place. Frozen audio is psychologically expensive to un-freeze, which means you will not un-freeze it, which means the Peak is locked, which means your session has a *bottom*.

[pause 300ms] The goal this week is not a great track. The goal is a *finished* track. Great is what happens when you do this fifty-two times.
