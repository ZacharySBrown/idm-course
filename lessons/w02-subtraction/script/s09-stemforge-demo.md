[pause 300ms] Ear-training drill. This is the Subtraction method applied to someone else's finished record, so you can hear what muting actually does to a mix before you attempt it on your own.

[pause 200ms] Stemforge — the tool lives at `/Users/zak/zacharysbrown/stemforge` in this repository, recipe `w02-little-by-little-stems` — splits the track `little_by_little.wav` into four stems: drums, bass, vocals, and other. Same splitter we used in Week 1. [pause 150ms] If you are remembering Week 1, stemforge is not magic. Bleeding happens. The "other" bucket is a catch-all. You already know the caveats.

[pause 250ms] Three A/B listens for this slide. *Do them with headphones on, not laptop speakers.* Monitor to your ears.

[pause 150ms] First A/B. Full mix, then the mix with drums muted. *What you are listening for:* how much of the perceived low-end loudness was actually the kick's transient, versus the bass line. Usually more than you'd guess. The bass alone sounds deflated — that is a fact about human hearing, not about the bass patch.

[pause 200ms] Second A/B. Full mix, then bass-alone — inverse mute. Everything else gone. *What you're listening for:* how much of the *vibe* of the record was one bass line doing most of the emotional work. Almost always a surprising amount. This is the argument for the constant anchor — pick an element that is doing real work.

[pause 250ms] Third A/B. Full mix, then vocal-only. *What you're listening for:* where the anchor actually lives. In a lot of records with vocals, the anchor *is* the vocal — but quieter than you think. It is almost never the loudest element in the mix.

[pause 200ms] *Write one sentence in your notebook during these listens.* Which stem could you mute for the Reduction section of your own track and still keep the listener interested? [pause 150ms] That question, compressed, is the entire Subtraction method.
