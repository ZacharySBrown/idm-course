`Tape Notes` episode 140. Kieran Hebden. [pause 200ms] Quote: "Decide which things ride through the track in a simple way, and which the detail is in." [pause 300ms]

That is the whole concept, in Hebden's words. He is describing a compositional division of labor. Some elements in a track are *riders* — they sit there, unchanging, for the entire runtime. Other elements are *detail* — they mutate every two or four bars. The riders give the listener an orientation. The details give the listener something to notice. Take away the riders, and the detail sounds like random noise. Take away the detail, and the riders sound like a drone. You need both.

[pause 250ms] Inside Subtraction, this translates very cleanly. Pick one track in your Peak that will never be muted. Candidates: a single pad note, held. A hat pattern running sixteenths. A sub on the root note. A field recording bed. Something that is not interesting on its own.

[pause 200ms] *That last part matters.* The anchor is not supposed to be interesting. It is supposed to be *constant*. If your anchor is interesting, it fights with the detail elements and turns into another voice competing for attention. A good anchor is almost boring by itself. Listen to ten minutes of a Four Tet track and try to articulate what the pad is doing — you usually can't, because it has been sitting there unchanged for ten minutes and your brain has stopped tracking it. That is success.
