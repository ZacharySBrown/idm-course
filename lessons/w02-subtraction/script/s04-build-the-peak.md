[pause 300ms] The Peak. Sixteen bars of climax. Everything on.

You are going to write the *densest state* of your track, right now, in Session View — before the A section, before the Intro, before any of the quieter material. This is the inversion that most producers find uncomfortable. You are writing the chorus first. You are writing the moment the whole song is building toward, at minute zero, with no context.

[pause 200ms] *Why.* Because the rest of the track is going to be made by muting elements out of the Peak. If the Peak is thin, the A section will be thinner. The Peak is the ceiling of the entire track's energy. You want that ceiling high.

[pause 250ms] *Checklist for the Peak loop.* Kick, snare, hats, one perc layer. Sub bass and a mid bass — two tracks, two bands, separated. One melodic element — a pad or a lead, pick one, do not try to have both at this stage. One texture layer — a field recording, a noise bed, a reverb wash sitting on a return. One vocal chop or a one-shot. And one detail element — a ratchet, a click, a stutter. That is seven or eight tracks. ~Not a hundred.~

[pause 200ms] The constraint that makes Subtraction work is this. *If it is not in the Peak, it does not exist in the track.* You cannot add a new element at bar 96 because the section feels thin. Thin is the point. Thin is Subtraction doing its job. When you feel the urge to add something new at bar 96, the correct response is to *unmute* something from the Peak that was previously hidden. Not to write a new part.

[pause 250ms] Loop the sixteen bars. A/B against a reference track — one you picked in Week 1. When you catch yourself enjoying it for eight full bars, that is your cue. Stop writing. Consolidate the loop. Bounce it in place if you want to be extra committal about it. Handbook section 6.1 — *commit and move on.* [pause 200ms] Now we have a Peak. Next move: mute backward.
