[pause 400ms] The dance-music drop is a pile-up. More kick, more bass, more riser, more side-chain pump until the whole mix is gasping. You know the shape. You have probably written one.

[pause 200ms] The *IDM* drop is the opposite. The kick walks out of the room.

[pause 300ms] That is the thesis for this week in one sentence. Three moves, all in the same family — they each say *something is different now* without also saying *here is more of it.*

[pause 200ms] Move one — the *TDE beat switch.* Replace the entire drum pattern at bar sixteen or thirty-two. No crossfade. No filter sweep. No crash cymbal. The lurch is the hook.

[pause 150ms] Move two — *the rub.* The phrase belongs to Bob Power, the engineer behind D'Angelo's *Voodoo* and most of the Roots catalog. *The rub* is his term for the friction between a sample and the track the sample sits in. A bassline in the Aeolian pulling against a sample that implies Dorian. Both voices musical. Neither voice willing to yield. That unresolved pull is the hook. ~Primary-source citation for Power using the phrase specifically — we have not found it yet. Forum consensus, Roots-fan lore, and Dan Charnas's *Dilla Time* on the Dilla side of the same technique.~

[pause 150ms] Move three — the *inverse drop.* Burial's *Archangel*, around one-thirty. The EDM producer would add a kick here. Burial removes one. Pitched vocal stutters take the weight. Vinyl crackle takes the weight. Foley takes the weight. Textural density replaces rhythmic density.

[pause 300ms] There is no Stemforge recipe for this lesson. This is a *student-driven* week. You are bringing your own in-progress track. The dullest eight-bar section you wrote last week — find it. We are cutting into those eight bars with *one* of the three moves. Not all three. One.

[pause 200ms] Before we pick, we are going to look at where these moves came from and why they work. Then the Ableton surface. Then the thirty-minute exercise. Then you ship.
