[pause 300ms] *The rub.* The phrase belongs to Bob Power — the engineer behind D'Angelo's *Voodoo,* A Tribe Called Quest, Erykah Badu, most of the Okayplayer records of the late nineties.

[pause 200ms] Caveat upfront — we have not located the primary-source interview where Power defines the term in print. The phrase is cited repeatedly in Roots-adjacent engineering lore and in several Bob Power lectures, but the specific article we want — Tape Op, RBMA, Sound on Sound — we have not surfaced yet. It is in `needs_bib:` on this lesson. Treat it as attributed, not yet confirmed.

[pause 300ms] Here is the definition anyway — *the rub* is the compositional friction between a sample and the track the sample sits in. When the sample implies one harmonic universe and the bassline pulls against it, the ear cannot fully reconcile the two. That unresolved pull is the hook.

[pause 300ms] Canonical example — J Dilla, *Get Dis Money,* from *Welcome 2 Detroit,* 2001. The vocal sample is Herbie Hancock's *Come Running to Me* — you can cross-check on `bib:whosampled_db`. The sample sits in one mode. Dilla's synth bassline underneath is in the Aeolian. Different mode from the sample. Both voices musical. Neither voice willing to blend.

[pause 200ms] Dan Charnas, *Dilla Time* — `bib:charnas_dilla_time`, chapter fourteen — argues that Dilla's approach to sampling was never strictly loop-based. He was writing *counterpoint* against the sample. The bassline is a second compositional voice, deliberately not in the sample's key center.

[pause 300ms] Critical distinction. *The rub is not dissonance.* It is not a clashing semitone interval. The rub is *modal friction* — two legitimate harmonic readings pulling against each other. Both voices inside a musical relationship. The ear hears two keys that are related but not the same, and holds both at once.

[pause 200ms] If you put a bassline a tritone from your sample's root, that is not the rub. That is a wrong note. The rub sits inside the harmony. It just sits in a different room of the same house.
