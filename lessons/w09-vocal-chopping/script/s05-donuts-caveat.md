[pause 400ms] A correction, because this one matters and because the myth is load-bearing for a lot of producers' bad assumptions about gear.

The story you have probably heard: *Donuts* was made entirely on a Boss SP-303 from a hospital bed. Dilla, dying, with one small sampler. The album as a last testament composed on tiny machine.

It is not true. Dan Charnas documents this in *Dilla Time*, the 2022 biography, and the Smithsonian's own archival note on the MPC3000 in their permanent collection confirms the gear of record. [pause 200ms] *Donuts* was primarily Pro Tools plus the Akai MPC3000. The SP-303, and later the SP-404, arrived at the hospital late in the process. Most of the album was already tracked. Some of the late material, maybe; the bulk, no.

[pause 300ms] Why does this matter for our lesson. Because if you believe the SP-303 story, you will go chase the SP-303 sound in order to get Dilla's sound. And the SP-303 is not what gave *Donuts* its character. The per-pad swing — *Dilla time* — was an MPC3000 feature. The one-thirty-second chops were an MPC3000 feature. The twelve-bit summing character everyone attributes to the 303 is, for most of the record, actually the MPC's own bus and the vinyl source material Dilla was pulling from.

The correct Ableton analogue is Simpler in Slicing mode, with Ornament at one-sixty-fourth for the flams, and nudged note starts for the per-pad drift. Not a bitcrusher pretending to be an SP-303. The bitcrusher gives you crunch. *Dilla time* gives you feel. Those are different problems. [pause 300ms]

~The hospital-bed-303 story is the kind of mythology that confuses circumstances with method.~ Dilla was in the hospital. He was dying. He also owned one of the defining sampling workstations in hip-hop history, and he used it. Respect the gear that did the work. [pause 200ms] Preserve this caveat if you cite the lesson elsewhere.
