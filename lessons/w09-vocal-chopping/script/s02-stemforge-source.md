[pause 200ms] The source. *Hot Pants*, James Brown, 1971. The full title is longer than the song's intro. We are not using the drum break — the rest of the world has used the drum break — we are using the vocal. The stemforge recipe is `w09-hot-pants-vocal-stem`. It calls the LALAL backend rather than the default Demucs pipeline, because LALAL is cleaner on dense brass-plus-vocal material.

[pause 200ms] Here is the footnote you need to internalize. LALAL is non-deterministic. Run it twice, get two different isolations. The bounce committed to `build/audio/stemforge-renders/w09/hp_vocals.wav` is the canonical one. Do not re-render to get a cleaner result. That is a different file. That is a different lesson. Commit the bounce, treat it as source-of-truth, move on.

Expect bleed. Reverb tails under the vocal. A ghost of the horn stab in between the phrases. Consonant smear around the plosives. None of it is a flaw. Prefuse loves this material. Dilla would have loved this material. The texture is the point. [pause 300ms]

WhoSampled lists over six hundred uses of the *Hot Pants* breakdown. None of those are ours, and we are not clearing this — we are transforming it. Prefuse's ethic, from the same XLR8R interview: ~"I'll find a way to make it sound like you haven't heard it before."~ That is the permission slip for the whole week.

[pause 200ms] Before you touch Simpler: drop the bounce on an audio track, freeze the track, flatten it. The chopping happens on the flattened bounce. Not on the original file. Not on a warped clip. A committed audio region. If you do not commit, every subsequent chop is referencing a warp engine that may re-interpret the transients when you change tempo. Freeze and flatten. [pause 200ms] Then we start cutting.
