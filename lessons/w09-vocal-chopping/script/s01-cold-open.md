[pause 300ms] Here is the confession. The phrase *"vocal chops"* is a genre label now — a producer-pack folder name, a YouTube-tutorial keyword — and it has flattened four completely different methods into one generic gesture. We are going to un-flatten them.

Four producers. Four machines. Four attitudes toward the same problem — what do you do with a human voice you did not record. [pause 200ms]

Prefuse 73, real name Scott Herren, chopped acapellas into phoneme-length fragments and finger-drummed them back across sixteen pads. The machine was an Akai MPC2000XL patched into Pro Tools. The receipt is his XLR8R interview, where he says, on the record, ~"I don't try to match bpms and shit."~ That is a method statement. Off-grid is the point.

J Dilla sliced vocals at one-thirty-second-notes — twice the subdivision most producers stop at — and rearranged the fragments until the source was gone. Per Dan Charnas in *Dilla Time*, the machine was the MPC3000, now in the Smithsonian. Not the SP-303. We'll get to that.

Kanye West and Just Blaze pitched soul records up four to seven semitones, using varispeed, not time-stretch. Just Blaze said it plainly at Red Bull Music Academy — *"we'll get seven pieces of a record, put it back together, add a breakdown part and make the sample do the hook."* Seven pieces. Literal.

DJ Premier did something stranger than all three. On *Ten Crack Commandments* he recorded himself *scratching a record* and then triggered that performance back into the beat as if it were a drum sample. [pause 200ms] Not a loop. A drum.
