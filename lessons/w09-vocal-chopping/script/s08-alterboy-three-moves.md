[pause 200ms] Little AlterBoy. One plugin. Three settings. Memorize them.

SoundToys built Little AlterBoy as a pitch-and-formant stripped-down tool with three modes — Transpose, Quantize, Robot — and four knobs. The device rewards memorization because the same three settings recur across multiple production styles.

[pause 300ms]

First setting. **Chipmunk soul double.** Transpose mode. Pitch plus seven. Formant plus seven. Link on. What this gives you is a second voice that sits an octave-minus-five higher than the source, with the formants shifted up to match so it doesn't sound like a bitcrusher. Run this as a parallel layer under your Re-Pitch chipmunk track, tucked at minus ten dB in the group. That is the doubled sped-up-soul sound Kanye tended to deploy before the Mike Dean era took over.

[pause 300ms] Second setting. **Robot lead from MIDI.** Switch to Robot mode. Route a MIDI track into Little AlterBoy's sidechain input — the device accepts MIDI as a pitch source. Drive at five. Now the vocal becomes a MIDI-controlled vocoder. Play a two-bar phrase on the MIDI track and the chopped acapella follows the notes. Instant talk-box lead. Good for filling the one-thirty-second rests in your Dilla pattern — you are not adding more vocal chop, you are adding a melodic vocal line built from the *same* chop, retriggered by MIDI.

[pause 300ms] Third setting. **Bass from vowels.** Back to Transpose mode. Formant minus eight. Pitch zero. Link off — this is the crucial part, because by default Little AlterBoy links formant and pitch. Unlink them.

What this does is strip the vocal into a low, slow, throaty texture with no pitch shift, just formant distortion toward the low end. Run Hot Pants through this, low-pass at two hundred hertz, and you have a sub-bass layer that started life as James Brown's backing singers. A Richard Devine move. Feed it to Granulator III or Roar if you want to push it further.

[pause 200ms] Three plugins' worth of effect from one instance. Memorize the three settings and stop shopping.
