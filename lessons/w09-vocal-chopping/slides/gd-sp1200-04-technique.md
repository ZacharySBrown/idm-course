# How the RZA / Premier / Pete Rock method worked

The canonical SP-1200 sampling move:

1. Pull record on Technics 1200. Cue to a 1–2-second break.
2. **Sample at 33 rpm or 45 rpm** — 45 rpm shortens the sample by 1.35×, freeing pad RAM. Pitch down in the SP-1200 to recover the original key. The <term key="sample_at_45">sample-at-45</term> trick is universal among SP-1200 producers (`bib:charnas_dilla_time`).
3. Truncate to the break. The 2.5-second limit is hard. If you can't fit it, you chop.
4. Assign to a pad. Set pitch. Play.
5. For the Premier method: sample your own scratching as a pad hit, trigger as percussion (`bib:premier_ten_crack_scratches`). Covered in s09.

Receipts:
- **Pete Rock**: *"I only sample stuff that gives me a good feel"* — filtered full-mix stereo records through a studio EQ at Greene Street to extract basslines where no stem existed (`bib:charnas_dilla_time` via spec). "T.R.O.Y." is two different sections of Tom Scott's "Today" pitched to match, drums from James Brown "Say It Loud."
- **RZA** used the SP-1200 through *"Bring the Pain"* and *"Method Man,"* then moved to the Ensoniq EPS-16 Plus for longer loops (`bib:rbma_rza_lecture`). The 2.5-second limit is why he switched.
- **DJ Premier** on *Illmatic* and *Ten Crack Commandments* — MPC60 plus Technics plus SP-1200 grit; the drums were often SP-processed, the basses not.
