Second case study, same episode. `Tape Notes #140`, the track "Skater."

[pause 300ms]

Hebden's bass voice on "Skater" is the Omnisphere preset "Plectra Sub." It plays a simple bass figure. Nothing unusual on paper.

[pause 400ms]

He doubles that bass with an identical MIDI clip — same notes, transposed up — on a *bell* patch from the same library. The bell plays the exact bass melody, pitched up into the top octaves. He levels the bell quietly. In his own words — *"you can't really hear the bell too much… without it, it's burying the bass more in the mix."*

[pause 500ms]

Read that phrasing again. Without the bell, the bass *buries* itself. The bell is not there to add melodic content. It is there to keep the bass *from sinking*. The brightness halo around the low fundamental is what lets the bass read as clear.

[pause 400ms]

The same principle applies to the lead on the track — Hebden notes that the main lead is the same melody played on three different instruments, layered in sequence across the arrangement. Not in parallel. In sequence. Each voicing takes a turn.

[pause 400ms]

The lesson, generalized. When a bass line disappears in the low-mids, the default move is to automate a high-shelf or a transient shaper. Hebden's move is cheaper and more musical — double the bass with a bright voice playing the same notes, level the bright voice at minus eighteen to minus twenty-four decibels, center-panned. No EQ move. No transient surgery. Just a second instrument on the same MIDI clip.

[pause 400ms]

On a decent pair of headphones you cannot consciously hear the bell. Mute it. The bass shrinks. Unmute. The bass reads bigger. The parameter you changed is *clarity*, not *level*.

[pause 300ms]

This is the subliminal layer as mixing tool, not just as atmosphere.
