Subliminal layering. The phrase is Hebden's. On `Tape Notes #140`, verbatim — *"there's little quiet echo-y bits of vocals and noises and little things happening all the time combined with everything else… tons of subliminal little sounds and textures all the time."*

[pause 400ms]

The precedent is not *Three*. The precedent is Fridge, Hebden's old band with Adem Ilhan and Sam Jeffers. In the Fridge live tracking sessions, the room mics picked up the players talking to each other between takes and playing video games during breaks. Hebden kept those mics open and summed them, *quietly*, with delay, under the finished mix. The conversations are on the record. You cannot consciously hear them. If you solo the bus you hear voices through a tape delay, muttering about whose turn it is on the PlayStation.

[pause 500ms]

He describes the function, also `Tape Notes #140`, as — *"a way for people to connect to it without them really even understanding why they're connecting to it."*

[pause 400ms]

The mechanism is not magic. It is simple. A layer sitting fifteen to twenty-five decibels below the lead elements. Often band-limited. Often drenched in a short delay. It does not compete for headroom. It does not fight the kick. It adds *spatial depth* — the impression that the mix extends outside the stereo field you are consciously tracking.

[pause 400ms]

The mix-bus implication is why the record works on laptop speakers. Because the subliminal layers are *additive depth*, not rivals for the primary dynamic range. A laptop cannot reproduce the depth anyway — but it will not *hurt* the mix. When the mix plays back on something bigger, the depth surfaces.

[pause 400ms]

For the rest of this lesson, when we say "subliminal layer," we mean this specific thing. A parallel track at minus eighteen dB or lower. Band-limited. Often delayed. Functionally invisible. Structurally load-bearing.

[pause 300ms]

The kick stack uses it. The bell under the bass uses it. The MIDI randomization uses it. Same philosophy, three different implementations.
