The laptop-speaker thing. `Tape Notes #140`, direct quote — *"ninety percent of the listening and mixing and everything for this album, all the music I make these days is just the laptop speakers."*

[pause 400ms]

He supplements with Audio-Technica reference headphones and Sennheiser HD25s on trains and planes. And he says, later in the episode, that *Three* was released "pretty much without any mastering on it."

[pause 400ms]

Upside first. A laptop speaker cannot reproduce sub. If you can feel weight on a MacBook, the mix is either very hot in the low-mids — which tends to not survive a club — or it has a sub layer so cleanly separated from the body that the midrange alone carries the impression of weight. The "Daydream Repeat" kick stack exists *because* Hebden forced himself to solve sub on a system that has no sub.

[pause 500ms]

The four-layer architecture is the solution to that constraint. Layer one carries character. Layer two is sub for the club. Layer three is click for the laptop. Layer four is body for the headphones. Each speaker class gets its own layer. That is laptop-speaker discipline *producing* good club mixing, not the other way around.

[pause 400ms]

Downside, inline. Producers who adopt laptop-speaker-only mixing without the supporting infrastructure — without the touring rig to calibrate against, without the reference headphones, without the preset discipline upstream — end up with mixes that read fine on a laptop and shrill on anything bigger. Hebden survives it because his arrangements are sparse and his palette is pre-voiced by Omnisphere. You may not have those supports.

[pause 500ms]

The rule for this course. One laptop-speaker pass per session. At least once before you bounce, close your interface, play the track through the laptop speakers, and note the problems you hear.

[pause 300ms]

Do not adopt laptop speakers as your only reference. That is Hebden's discipline, not a universal best practice. ~He has the touring income to find out what failed.~
