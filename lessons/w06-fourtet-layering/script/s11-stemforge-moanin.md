The input is "Moanin'," the 1958 Art Blakey and the Jazz Messengers recording. Bobby Timmons wrote it. Lee Morgan on trumpet. Benny Golson on tenor. We run it through the Stemforge glitch pipeline, which slices, repitches transients, and leaves ghost tails on the detritus.

[pause 400ms]

Two renders land in the build directory for week six. One is `moanin_dry.wav` — the unprocessed stem split, as a reference. The other is `moanin_glitch.wav` — the wet glitch pipeline.

[pause 400ms]

What to listen for on the wet render. First — the sub-audible ghost tails. Where the glitch pipeline re-slices a transient, it leaves a quiet tail behind. Normally you would reject those tails as artifacts. For our purposes today they are *exactly* the Hebden subliminal-layer material. Quiet. Band-limited. Already contextual — they were born from the same source as the primary content.

[pause 500ms]

Second — pipeline artifacts as texture. The glitch pass will produce detritus a more polished mix session would filter out. Treat it as source material, not as a bug. This is the "dirty intake" posture Hebden applies to his own sample folder — he specifically does *not* clean up stems too hard before loading them into Ableton, because the bleed is often musical.

[pause 500ms]

For today's exercise — keep the full Moanin' glitch stem on a parallel return or a muted channel. Mute it before you render the final loop. We are not building a track over Moanin'. We are building a kick stack that fits *against* Moanin'.

[pause 400ms]

That means the kick stack has to know what key Moanin' is in. For our purposes, Bobby Timmons's composition is in F minor — you can verify by ear or by dropping Ableton's Tuner on the walking bass. Tune your sub sine to F-one, the click stays unpitched, the sub body stays on F-zero or F-one, your choice by ear.

[pause 400ms]

Do not, under any condition, mix the Moanin' stem up to equal the kick stack. It is a subliminal bed for the exercise. That is its entire job.
