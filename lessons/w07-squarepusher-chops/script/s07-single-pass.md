# s07 — Single-pass

Jenkinson, on *Big Loada*, Tape Op eighty-nine, verbatim: *"To this day, nobody believes that the tracks on Big Loada were a single pass of me sequencing my Akai S950 from my DR-660."* [pause 500ms]

Read that again. Nobody believes him. ~He is still a little pissed off about it fifteen years later.~ The tracks are hyperactive, cut up, ghost-reversed, aliased — they sound like hours of surgical editing. They are not. They are one take of him programming patterns into the DR-660 while it triggers the S950, recorded live to tape.

And on *Hard Normal Daddy*: *"It's just something I was trying to do — program like a player, make it have that swing, with a bionic aspect."*

The discipline is the point. One pass. No take-compiling. The sequencer runs. You perform into it. Whatever happens is the record. [pause 400ms]

Three reasons it works.

One. **Commit forces identity.** A take that cannot be undone develops a signature. If you know you cannot fix it, you stop trying to make it perfect and start trying to make it *yours*. Jenkinson's aliased S950 repitch is not a choice he agonized over. It is what the box did. He committed. It became his.

Two. **Errors become features.** The S950's aliased pitch-shift is brittle and digital and kind of gross. Jenkinson kept it. Because it was there. Because fixing it would have taken the session off the rails. Because the take was the thing.

Three. **The session ends on time.** Single-pass is the forty-five-minute rabbit hole with the lid screwed on. You cannot burn a day on one loop if you have already committed to one pass.

The Ableton rule for this week. [pause 300ms] When you run the exercise at the end of this lesson, **disable Edit-and-Undo in your head**. Technically the menu item is still there — Ableton cannot enforce this — but you do not touch it. Capture MIDI the performance in one take. Bounce in place. Delete the source MIDI clip. Keep only the printed audio.

If the result is ugly, the result is ugly. Ship it anyway. [pause 200ms]

Week 10 teaches how to mutate ugly results into finished music. That is the variation-generation method. It is a whole chapter. You cannot run it on nothing. You need raw material to mutate, and single-pass is how you generate raw material.

So this week we make. Next week we think about how Autechre think. Week ten we learn what to do with this week's output. [pause 300ms] Commit the pass.
