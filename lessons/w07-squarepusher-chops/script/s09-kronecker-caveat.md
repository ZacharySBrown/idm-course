# s09 — Kronecker King caveat

*Hello Everything*, twenty-oh-six. [pause 300ms] The track "Kronecker King" has a snare sound nobody on the forums agrees how to rebuild.

The best-guess reconstruction, from KVR and Jenkinson's own archived site notes: the snare sample is trimmed to a one-to-two-millisecond envelope — a Kronecker-delta impulse, mathematically speaking, which is a unit spike with zero duration. Everything after the initial click is removed. The snare "body" is then synthesized underneath, typically in Reaktor Core.

Caveat, inline, where it lives. [pause 300ms] The one-to-two millisecond figure is community consensus, *not* a direct Tape Op quote. What *is* confirmed, same Tape Op interview, is that from *Go Plastic* onward Jenkinson abandoned breakbeat chops for drums *"synthesized inside Reaktor"* — an explicit rebellion against sample-loops. So the *principle* is confirmed: trim the transient off a real recording, synthesize the body. The *exact millisecond count* is reconstruction. I have flagged it in `needs_bib`.

~So we teach the technique and the attribution caveat together.~ That is the standing rule in this course. Do not launder unverified gear lore as interview fact.

Ableton translation. [pause 250ms]

Step one. Load a snare sample into **Simpler** in One-Shot mode. Any snare. A 909 hit, an acoustic snare, the top snare slice from your Funky Fanfare Drum Rack.

Step two. Set Fade-In to one millisecond. Set Length to two milliseconds. Only the attack transient survives. If you play the Simpler now, you will hear a click. That is the Kronecker impulse.

Step three. On the same Drum Rack chain — or a layered chain triggered from the same MIDI note — drop an **Ableton Meld** or **Operator**. Tune it to the pitch of the original snare body. Shape the amp envelope with a short attack, fifty-to-eighty-millisecond decay, no sustain, short release. This is the synthesized body.

Step four. Play both from the same MIDI note. The click fires from Simpler. The body fires from the synth. The snare is now a two-device sandwich. [pause 300ms]

Why this matters pedagogically. Once you start synthesizing the body of a drum, the drum stops being a *sample* and starts being a *voice*. The transient stays real — the click carries the recording's acoustic signature. Everything else is yours to shape. You can tune the body to the root of the track. You can modulate the body's pitch across the song. You can make a snare that sits in the chord.

This is the doorway into the Week 8 Autechre material. Autechre synthesize almost all their drums. They are not sampling. They are *building*. Jenkinson is midway between — half-sampler, half-synthesist. Both postures produce drums that sound nothing like a sample library. [pause 300ms]
