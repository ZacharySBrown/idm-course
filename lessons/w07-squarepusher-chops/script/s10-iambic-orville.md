# s10 — Iambic 9 Poetry

*Iambic 9 Poetry* is on *Ultravisitor*, twenty-oh-four. It is not a drum-and-bass track. It is roughly seventy-eight BPM, in D major feel — D to B-minor to G to F-sharp-minor — and the bass is played live, fretless-style, by Jenkinson himself. The first half appears to be live drums. The primary processor across the track is the Eventide Orville. [pause 400ms]

This is Jenkinson as a musician in the ordinary sense of the word. Someone with a bass in his hands, playing songs.

Two facts for the record. [pause 200ms]

Fact one. Jenkinson is self-taught in DSP via Curtis Roads' *The Computer Music Tutorial*, MIT Press, 1996. That book is eleven hundred pages, covers granular synthesis, physical modeling, formant synthesis, all the primitives. It is the reason Jenkinson can write his own Orville patches. It is also, not coincidentally, on the shelf of nearly every serious IDM producer.

Fact two. Those Orville patches are written in VSIG, Eventide's modular patch editor, which ran on a PC and transferred patches to the Orville hardware by SysEx. A patch was a graph of DSP primitives. Jenkinson's compositional work included building the effects he then ran audio through. [pause 400ms]

Why this track matters for the lesson. Two reasons.

One. **Live instrumentation is not forbidden in Squarepusher's catalog.** The popular image is the chopped-break guy, the bionic bassist, the no-computer purist. Real Jenkinson is more pluralist than that. Iambic 9 Poetry is mostly him playing an instrument through a custom effect chain, recording the performance, making small edits. That is a normal musician workflow. The signature is not the break-chopping. The signature is the *signal path*.

Two. **Your Max for Live patches are your VSIG.** [pause 300ms] The thing a good producer does in twenty-twenty-six that Jenkinson did in twenty-oh-four is build the instrument before composing with it. Next week — Autechre week — we sit with this idea for real. Booth has Max patches dating to twenty-eleven that still run on the NTS Sessions rig from twenty-eighteen. The instrument is older than the track.

For us, this week, stay on the breaks. Iambic 9 Poetry is a reminder for later. Once you can chop an Amen break, you can *also* play over one. The catalog is bigger than the single technique.

One more slide. The exercise. [pause 200ms] Ship the chop.
