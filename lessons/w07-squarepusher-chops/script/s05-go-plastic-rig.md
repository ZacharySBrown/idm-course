# s05 — Go Plastic rig

*Go Plastic*, twenty-oh-one. Warp. [pause 300ms]

Jenkinson on the rig, Tape Op eighty-nine, verbatim: *"I didn't use a computer on Go Plastic. It was made with a Yamaha QY700, TX81Z and FS1R, an Eventide DSP4000 and Orville, an Akai S6000 and a Mackie 16-channel desk."* [pause 500ms]

Let's name every piece.

Yamaha QY700. We already covered it — the brain. Sequencer and MIDI clock for everything downstream. [pause 200ms]

Yamaha TX81Z. Released 1987. A rack version of the DX21. Four-operator FM synthesis, eight voices, no user interface worth the name. Cost four hundred dollars new. The metallic bell and plucked-string sounds on early Squarepusher are this box, often. [pause 200ms]

Yamaha FS1R. Released 1998. Eight-operator FM plus formant synthesis, the most complex FM synth Yamaha ever made, considered roughly unplayable because the front-panel editing is brutal. Jenkinson loves it for exactly that reason — you program it from the outside and accept what it gives back.

Akai S6000. Late-era 19-inch rack sampler, 128 megabytes maximum RAM. This is where the Amen break lives on Go Plastic-era material. Earlier tracks — *Come On My Selector*, *Big Loada* cuts — lived in the Akai S950, a twelve-bit sampler from 1988 with a specific aliased-pitch-shift character that the S6000 does not reproduce.

Eventide DSP4000 and Orville. The effect hub. [pause 300ms] These are not off-the-shelf reverb boxes. Jenkinson writes his own algorithms for them in an editor called VSIG — Eventide's modular patch environment, PC-based, transferred to the hardware by SysEx. He builds the effect and then runs audio through it.

Mackie 16-channel desk. ~A five-hundred-dollar summing box.~ Nothing exotic. The character is all upstream of it.

One specific patch, same interview, verbatim: *"The bass distortion on 'Megazine' was done with an old-school one-ten-volt Morley Wah pedal and an Orville distortion patch based on a curve, X-Y mapping module."* [pause 400ms]

So. Read that rig again. QY700, two FM synths, one sampler, two Eventides, one cheap console. That is the entire *Go Plastic* studio. Nothing named Pro Tools. Nothing named Max. Nothing with a screen bigger than a pack of cigarettes.

We do not need the hardware. That is important. You can reproduce the *signal path* in Ableton. What you cannot reproduce from a plugin list is the *mental model*. The point of enumerating the rig is not to buy it. The point is to notice how small it is. Six things. Six signal destinations. A brain that fits in your head. [pause 300ms]

That is what you are trying to build.
