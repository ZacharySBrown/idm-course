# s02 — QY700

The Yamaha QY700. [pause 300ms] Released 1997. Six tracks of MIDI, sixteen patterns per song, 77-note polyphony, 32-channel output over a pair of MIDI ports. General MIDI tone generator on board that nobody uses. Step entry via a numeric keypad.

The LCD is 320 by 240 pixels and monochrome. It shows you pattern numbers, step numbers, note values. It does not show you a piano roll in any meaningful sense. This is the *point*.

Jenkinson, Tape Op eighty-nine, the core principle, verbatim: *"In order to really work fluently in a studio you need to have a model, or virtual image, of all those pathways in your head. If I'm running bits of data through it, I run it through the same pathway in my mental image, and then I know exactly what the results are going to be."* [pause 400ms]

Read that carefully. The mental model is the thing. The screen pollutes the mental model. Therefore the screen has to go.

Two consequences for us. [pause 200ms]

One. **Program like a player.** The hardware sequencer is a musical instrument. You perform into it. You do not sculpt it in post. When Jenkinson enters a bassline into the QY700, he is making performance choices in real time about step, velocity, length. He is not auditioning ten variations and comping.

Two. **Numeric entry is a feature, not a bug.** Typing note values into a keypad slows you down and makes you commit. The low-resolution screen is a constraint against second-guessing.

Ableton translation. [pause 250ms] Use **Capture MIDI**. Set a 16-bar clip length. Play your part on a controller against the metronome. Stop. Drag the captured buffer into the clip. Do not open the piano roll to clean up. Do not quantize. If the take is terrible, play it again from scratch. Do not edit notes.

This is a gentle version of the QY700 posture in software. The stronger version — we get to in slide seven, when we talk single-pass.

One caveat inline. Some producers argue this kind of discipline is fetish. They have a point. If your playing is worse than your editing, the Jenkinson method loses you music. ~It is not universal.~ But it is the fastest known route to having a *sound* rather than a catalog of fixes.
