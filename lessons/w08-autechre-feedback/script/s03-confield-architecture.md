*Confield* came out in 2001. Booth and Brown had been living inside Max/MSP since 1997, which at that point was about four years of daily practice. The *SOS* April 2004 feature — which is really a post-mortem on the *Confield*-to-*Untilted* period — has Booth saying the quiet part out loud. [pause 300ms] *Most of Confield came out of experiments with Max that weren't really applicable in a club environment.*

Translation: the record is the *lab notebook*. [pause 400ms]

There are three technical ingredients in the *Confield* patch family that you should know by name, because we are going to approximate each of them in Ableton this week.

First — [pause 200ms] Navier-Stokes fluid-dynamics simulations used as slow continuous modulators. Navier-Stokes is the partial-differential-equations math that describes how fluid flows through a pipe. You do not need to know the math. You need to know the shape. The output is a smooth, non-periodic, non-repeating curve. They mapped it to filter cutoff, pan position, pitch offset. An LFO replacement, except the LFO has *memory*. It never draws the same wave twice, but it is not random — it is a physical simulation.

Second — nested analogue sequencers. One sequencer's control voltage decides what step another sequencer plays. A third sequencer drives the second at a different tempo. [pause 300ms] Booth on WATMM: *immediately you have something that some people would call random, but I would say is quantifiable.* The distinction is whether you can derive the output from the inputs. You can. So it is not random.

Third — twenty-plus-minute takes. They would run the patch for twenty, twenty-five minutes, record the stream, and spend the evening editing it down. The composer is a *curator of a rule-bound output stream*. [pause 400ms]

Your Ableton version: build the generators, use `Capture MIDI` to record the run, then chop and rearrange in Arrangement view. Your job is not to write bars — it is to select from the stream.
