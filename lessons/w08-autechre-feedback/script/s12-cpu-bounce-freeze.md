The unglamorous part of this week. [pause 400ms] Both the handbook and the deep-research spec skip this, so it lives here.

Generative chains are CPU-heavy. Three Max for Live devices, a Roar on the drum bus, a Drum Rack with eight sample chains, Macro Variations firing across a Chain Selector — your laptop fan will come on within four bars. Max for Live devices in particular are interpreted-Max inside a plugin wrapper; they are not efficient.

Three tools, in this order, per the Live 12 manual. [pause 300ms]

First — Freeze Track. Right-click any track header, select Freeze. Ableton renders the entire track, including all devices, down to a temporary audio file and suspends the device processing. CPU returns. The frozen track can still be moved, chopped at clip boundaries, and arranged — but you cannot edit the device parameters until you Unfreeze.

This is your *thinking pause*. Freeze a generative chain while you work on the arrangement around it.

Second — Flatten. After Freeze, right-click the track header again and select Flatten. [pause 400ms] The frozen audio becomes a real audio clip in a real audio track. The MIDI and the devices and the sidechains and the rack configurations — all gone.

This is **the commit.** This is the point of no return. If you flatten a track and the printed audio turns out to be wrong, you are re-doing the work. That is a feature, not a bug. It is the same discipline as printing to tape — you commit, and then you move on.

Third — Bounce in Place. Live 12.3 extended this to what the manual calls Bounce Groups. [pause 300ms] For subgroups of tracks, you can commit the whole group to one audio track, preserving the group routing. Use this on your generative drum bus once the four-commit MIDI chain is done.

Rule: once a chain sounds right, Flatten within the same session. Do not come back tomorrow to unfreeze and tweak. You are buying *decision closure*. Closure is what gets tracks shipped.
