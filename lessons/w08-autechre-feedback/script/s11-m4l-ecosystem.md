Three Max for Live devices. [pause 300ms] Each one replaces a hand-built Max/MSP structure that Booth and Brown would have patched themselves — and that the rest of us, reasonably, would rather not.

Device one — K-Devices ESQ and its percussive sibling HEXO. Per-step probability step sequencers. Each step in the grid carries its own chance value between zero and one hundred percent. [pause 400ms] This is the direct descendant of the technique Booth described in *SOS* April 2004, the one I keep quoting because it is so precise — *we may have one fader that determines how often a snare does a little roll or skip.* ESQ is melodic. HEXO is drum-focused. Both let you set probability per step without touching a single `random` object. The system is bounded. The variation is constrained.

Device two — Dillon Bastan's Strange Mod. Three-dimensional chaotic attractors. [pause 300ms] Lorenz. Rössler. Thomas. If you do not know what those words mean — they are deterministic mathematical systems that produce non-repeating trajectories. They are Navier-Stokes-adjacent. Mathematically, chaos is not randomness; it is extreme sensitivity to initial conditions in an otherwise deterministic system. You map the X, Y, and Z outputs to filter cutoff, pan position, pitch offset. You are running a 2026-edition version of the *Confield* fluid-dynamics modulator.

Device three — Markov Variations. [pause 400ms] Markov-chain note sequencer. You feed it a source melody, it learns the transition probabilities between notes, it generates statistically consistent derivatives. This is the same family as the voice-writing patches Booth has described on the WATMM *Oversteps* threads — conditionals forcing scales and harmonic relationships between independent voices.
