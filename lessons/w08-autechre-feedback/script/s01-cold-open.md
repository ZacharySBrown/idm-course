Everyone tells you Autechre used Max. [pause 300ms] That is true, and it is also the least interesting thing you can say about them.

The part that actually makes the music work — the part that distinguishes *Confield* from every college-student Max patch that has ever sounded like *Confield* — is that Sean Booth and Rob Brown ~refuse to use random operators~. [pause 400ms]

Read that again. They build generative systems for a living. They write patches that spit out drum patterns for twenty minutes at a stretch. And they *refuse* to use the one object in Max that every beginner reaches for first. The `random` object. They do not use it.

Booth on the WATMM forums, paraphrased from what he said in *Sound on Sound* April 2004 — [pause 200ms] *we don't use random operators because they're irritating to work with. Every time you run the process it sounds different. How we play the system dictates how the system responds.*

That sentence is the whole week. [pause 500ms]

Not "generative music." Not "algorithmic." *Procedural.* [pause 300ms] Rule-based. Repeatable. Composed. A process you can run three times and get three identical outputs, where the *interesting* variation comes from you changing a seed or a fader position, not from the machine rolling dice.

We are going to build the Ableton-native version of that discipline. Rhythm generator. Recombine, in Shuffle mode. Time Warp. Velocity Shaper. Ornament. Five tools, four *commits* — you are going to print the MIDI to audio between every step, which forces the decisions to stick.

Then we run the whole thing through Roar's multiband feedback, which is the closest Ableton ships to the Autechre Shure Auxpander patchbay that ran signal back into itself until it stopped being a drum sound and started being a texture.
