The core exercise of the week. The *four-commit procedural MIDI chain*. [pause 400ms] Built entirely out of Ableton 12 MIDI Transform and Generate tools that ship with Live — no Max for Live required for this part.

Open a new MIDI track. Drop a Drum Rack on it. We will load samples in the exercise slide. For now, assume the rack is loaded.

Step one — Rhythm generator. Right-click an empty MIDI clip, Generate → Rhythm. Settings: [pause 300ms] Steps sixteen. Density fifty-five percent. Division one-sixteenth. Shift zero. Hit Generate. You have a raw grid.

**Commit.** Right-click the clip. Duplicate it into a second, scratch track. Rename the original `rhythm_v01`. You now have a backup of the generator output.

Step two — Recombine, Shuffle mode. Select the clip, Transform → Recombine → Shuffle. This re-orders the card deck. Same notes, new positions. [pause 300ms] Commit — duplicate into a scratch track, rename.

Step three — Time Warp. Transform → Time Warp. Draw a breakpoint speed curve. Start at two-point-zero times, drop to zero-point-two-five across two bars. The clip now decelerates. A snare roll that falls apart. [pause 400ms] Commit.

Step four — Velocity Shaper, then Ornament. Velocity Shaper first: draw a curve by hand. Do not use a preset. Kill uniformity. Then Ornament: Length one-sixty-fourth, Density twenty percent. Flams and mordents appear between the notes.

Commit the final version.

The reason for the four commits is not paranoia — it is *compositional visibility*. You have performed four discrete acts. If you stacked all four transforms and applied them at once, you cannot A/B between them. You cannot say "the Time Warp killed it, roll back to step two." You are flying blind.

With four commits, you have four versions of the clip sitting in the project. You can swap them back in. You can compare. You can *compose* — which is a series of reversible decisions, not one forward-only press of a button.

This is procedural, not random. [pause 300ms] Every step is undoable. Every step is named.
