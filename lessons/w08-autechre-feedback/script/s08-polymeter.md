Two tricks. Stacked. Both documented in the Live 12 manual. Both free. Both ignored by most producers. [pause 400ms]

Trick one — the Euclidean generator. Live 12 ships it natively; it was in the original Rhythm generator family. The Euclidean algorithm distributes N pulses as evenly as possible across M steps, with an optional rotation R. Three integers define an entire rhythm.

Ali Jamieson — who has written the clearest English-language documentation of this that exists — suggests a four-track starting point. Kick at sixteen steps, four pulses, rotation zero. Snare at sixteen steps, three pulses, rotation two. Hat at sixteen steps, seven pulses, rotation one. Clave at *twelve* steps, five pulses, rotation zero. [pause 300ms]

Four clips. *Different lengths.* One Drum Rack on the receiving end.

The pattern repeats only at the least common multiple of the clip lengths — which, with a twelve-step clave against sixteen-step drums, is forty-eight steps. Three bars before the pattern literally recurs. Push the clave to eleven steps and you are at a hundred seventy-six step LCM. Eleven bars before a literal repeat.

Trick two — clip envelopes unlinked from loop length. [pause 400ms]

In any audio or MIDI clip, open the envelope editor at the bottom. Choose a parameter — filter cutoff, for example. There is a checkbox that says Linked. Uncheck it. Now set the envelope length to seven bars. Set the audio clip itself to four bars.

The clip plays in a four-bar loop. The filter sweep cycles in a seven-bar loop. They do not re-align until bar twenty-eight.

Three-bar pan envelope against a five-bar amp envelope on a pad? Fully non-repeating motion for fifteen bars before anything lines up the same way twice.

No random operator touched. Every single moment is procedurally derivable from the two cycle lengths. [pause 400ms] This is the Autechre polymeter posture in a form you already own.
