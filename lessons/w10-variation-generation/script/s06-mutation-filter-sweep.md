[pause 300ms] Mutation three. Filter sweep.

The rule: one Auto Filter, mapped to one macro, sweeping across the entire 16-bar duplicate. Nothing else changes. This is the most constrained mutation in the set and it is the one most producers over-cook.

[pause 300ms]

Setup. You group the whole loop into a group track. You insert Auto Filter on the group. Low-pass, 24 dB slope, Resonance around fifteen percent. Resonance over twenty-five percent will ring; fifteen is safe.

You map the Frequency knob to Macro 1 on the group's rack. In Arrangement, you draw a clip envelope on Macro 1 that ramps from fully closed at bar 1 to fully open at bar 16. A straight line. Not a curve. Not a stair-step. A straight line.

[pause 400ms]

Now — the trick that makes this mutation actually musical. It is a Spec A section 5.7 move.

The clip envelope for the filter macro has its own loop length, independent of the audio. Unlink the envelope from the clip loop. Set the audio loop to 4 bars. Set the envelope loop to 7 bars.

What happens: the filter sweep never lands in the same place twice against the groove. The downbeat hits with a fully-open filter on one pass, three-quarters-open on the next, half-open on the third. Autechre have been doing the polymeter-through-envelope move since *Confield* — same idea, a different entry point. Live 12 gives you a checkbox in the clip detail view and a number field for bars.

[pause 400ms]

The limitation. A filter sweep alone, by itself, is 2012 festival-tent music. It earns its keep this week because the section underneath is *otherwise identical* to the original. You are not selling the sweep. You are selling the return to full bandwidth on the downbeat of the next section. That return is where the listener's ear perks up, because the next mutation arrives with its bandwidth restored.

[pause 300ms] If you catch yourself automating more than one parameter — stop. Filter sweep means filter sweep. Save the other parameter for another mutation.
