[pause 300ms] The thesis, named explicitly.

Constraint is the generator. Not inspiration. Not tone. Not the right preset. *Constraint.*

Five weeks back, in the subtraction lesson, we built the loop at its densest and peak, and then muted backward to derive the other sections. That method works because the densest loop is the constraint — the full version is fixed, and every earlier section is a reduction from it.

This week is the inverse. One loop. Five duplicates. Five prescribed mutations. You do not get to pick the mutations. You apply them in order, or in the order that creates an arc, and you ship.

[pause 300ms]

The method has lineage. Finish More Music calls it "spawn from the same seed" — their phrasing, not ours. Spec A, section 5.4, calls it Variation Generation. Brian Eno, on a card printed in 1975 — *Oblique Strategies*, the pack he keeps on his desk — writes: *"Honor thy error as a hidden intention."* Same idea. Different packaging.

[pause 400ms]

Mechanically, in Ableton, it looks like this.

Duplicate your 16-bar loop five times along the Arrangement timeline. Apply one mutation per duplicate. Drum swap. Bass seed. Filter sweep. Reverse. New layer. Each mutation has a specific Ableton surface and we will walk through all five.

Then — and this is the part most producers miss — reorder them. Often 3, 1, 2, 5, 4 reads better as an arc than 1, 2, 3, 4, 5. The mutations are meant to live in any order. Test two orderings before you commit.

[pause 300ms]

Why this works: five sections share DNA because they *started identical*. The coherence is baked in at the duplicate step. Everything after is differentiation. You cannot accidentally write five unrelated sections because the source is the same loop.

That is the whole engine. We spend the next eight slides on the parameters.
