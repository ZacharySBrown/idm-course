[pause 300ms] A caveat slide, because both specs missed this one.

Neither Spec A nor Spec B names versioning discipline explicitly. They should have. Filling the gap now.

[pause 400ms]

The rule, stated once, so it is on tape.

Before every bounce, save a timestamped copy of the Live set. The path convention: tilde-slash `IDM_COURSE_versions` slash year-dash-month-dash-day underscore `wNN` underscore `track_N` dot `als`.

Example. Today is April 20th, 2026. The file: `2026-04-20_w10_track_01.als`. If you edit for two hours and re-save, append underscore `02`, underscore `03`. Never overwrite a previous version. Disk is cheap. Your time is not.

[pause 300ms]

Why this matters, specifically in this week's method. The five-mutations workflow and the Resample-and-Mutate engine both *delete the source*. That is the whole point — commitment forcing. But if you delete the source MIDI, and the printed audio sits in the arrangement, and an hour later you realize Mutation 3 was better with the filter sweep inverted, and the MIDI is gone — you are stuck.

The versioned `.als` is your one-way-back. It is not an undo button. It is a fossil record. You can load `2026-04-20_w10_track_01.als`, find the MIDI, export it as a file, drop it into the current set, and you have recovered the source without undoing your committed audio.

[pause 400ms]

Autechre have been doing this forever. Sean Booth, in the *Sound on Sound* April 2004 feature, describes persistent Max patches that survive across albums. The NTS Sessions — eight hours of material released in 2018 — reportedly used patches first built in 2011. Forum consensus on WATMM, not a confirmed interview, so cite it with the asterisk. But the claim is consistent with everything Booth has said in print about how they work.

The archive is the instrument. The archive is also — and this is the less-glamorous part — the backup.

[pause 300ms]

The pattern, in one line, for the rest of your producer life. Save a timestamped `.als`. Consolidate. Delete the source. Bounce. Move on.

You are versioned. You are committed. You can still recover. Those are the three things you want, and saving a file gives you all three.
