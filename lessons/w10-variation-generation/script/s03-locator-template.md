Spec A, section 5.2, is explicit about this. Before the first audio plays, before you touch a synth, you open Arrangement View and drop locators at six timecodes.

Zero zero, Intro. Zero forty-five, the A section. Two zero zero, the B section. Three fifteen, the Reduction — that is the breakdown. Three forty-five, the Peak. Five zero zero, the Outro. The track ends around five forty-five.

[pause 300ms]

In Ableton — Cmd-drop on the timeline, type the label, hit return. Six locators. That is a minute of work.

The locators are suggestions. They are not contracts. You can move them. But the act of placing them — that is the thing. It converts the session from open-ended into finite. Open-ended sessions are where loops live. Finite sessions are where tracks ship.

Kieran Hebden, on Tape Notes episode 140, roughly: *"Arrangement is just everything. There's no point in having a good sound if it's not arranged into anything."*

Hebden says this calmly, like a weather report. He is right. Arrangement is not the last pass. It is the first pass. The locators are the skeleton; the sounds are the meat; and you can put meat on a skeleton, but you cannot put a skeleton inside meat after the fact.

Five sections. Five mutations. Two bookends that are both reductions of the source loop. Done. You have a six-minute track before you have written a second loop.
