[pause 300ms] Mutation five. New layer.

The rule: introduce *one* sound that was not in the original loop. One. [pause 300ms] One.

The temptation is to add three. You will not. Spec A section 5.4 says one layer, and it means one. Here is why the rule is firm — every new layer you add costs a mix decision. One layer at 16 bars is a variation. Three layers at 16 bars is a new song you did not have time to write, and now the mix is a problem, and now you are in a rabbit hole, and now the track ships in nine months instead of this week.

[pause 400ms]

Good candidates, ranked by effort to payoff.

One — a single held sub-bass note on the root, tuned to your track's key. Sixteen bars, no movement. Weight without activity. This is the single highest-value new layer in the catalog, and it costs you one MIDI note and a Simpler.

Two — a two-note ostinato on a new patch. Meld plate resonator. Simpler loading a field recording. Operator with a short FM pluck. Two notes, repeated. That is it.

Three — a reversed texture pad from your sample folder. Warp mode Texture. Flux at eighty percent. Let it drift.

Four — a single vocal syllable chopped from Week 9's vocal stem. Pitch it up seven semitones. Place it on beat 1 and beat 9 only. The restraint is doing the work.

[pause 300ms]

Caveat — and this is the one producers get wrong. The layer must have been *absent* from sections one through four of your arrangement. If it appeared earlier, and it is reappearing here, it is not a new layer. It is a re-entry. Re-entries belong in the Peak section, not inside a variation slot. You have the Peak section for that. Use it there.

[pause 400ms]

Closing rule. Bounce the new layer to audio inside the 45-minute time-box. Rick Rubin's version of this, in *The Creative Act*, is Andrew Huang's: if you are making volume adjustments smaller than half a decibel, you are done. If you are still programming the new layer at minute 46, you are in a rabbit hole, and the rule was clear. Bounce and move on.
