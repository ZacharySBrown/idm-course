[pause 300ms] Mutation one. Drum swap.

The rule: replace the drum pattern wholesale. Keep everything else identical. Bass line, same. Pad, same. Vocals, same. Only the drums change.

There are two clean paths in Live 12.

[pause 300ms]

Path A is the MIDI recombine. You select the drum MIDI clip, right-click, Transform, then Recombine → Shuffle. Live rotates and reshuffles the hits inside the clip — same kit, same velocities, new order. The kick lands where the hat was. The snare displaces by a sixteenth. It is a deterministic scramble with musical outputs.

Path B is the Drum Rack hot-swap. You keep the MIDI intact and swap the entire rack. The pattern plays back through a different kit. Same notes, unfamiliar voices. A boom-bap pattern becomes an industrial pattern becomes a footwork pattern, because the timing is fixed and only the voices move.

Spec A section 5.4 permits either interpretation. Path B generates more contrast per minute of effort, so if you are on a clock, start there.

[pause 400ms]

The limitation. Drum-swap variations can feel like the drummer quit mid-song if you do not bridge them. The edit point is where this mutation lives or dies. Insert a one-bar crash or a reversed cymbal swell on the bar before the swap. Or drop the bass for two beats. Or pitch a single hi-hat up an octave across the transition. Anything. The point is that the ear needs a signal that *something* is about to change, otherwise the new kit sounds like a studio accident.

[pause 300ms]

And — important — do not tune the new kit to the track. That was Week 5. This week you are moving fast. If the new kit is a semitone out of key, that is fine. You bounce anyway. You can re-tune in mixing. You cannot mix a track that is not arranged.

Drum swap. Five minutes, fifteen minutes tops. Move on.
