[pause 300ms] Before we touch a mixer, a parable.

Summer of 1997. Wayne Coyne of the Flaming Lips walks into a studio in Cassadaga, New York, with a producer named Dave Fridmann, and the two of them decide they are going to make an album that plays on four compact discs *simultaneously*. Not a mixtape. Not a remix project. Four separate CDs, four separate CD players, four friends pressing *play* on a count-off in a living room.

This was not a gimmick. *~This was not a gimmick.~* It was an arrangement constraint. Each CD had to carry a partial version of the song that only made sense in combination with the other three. Take one disc away — the vocal disappears. Take another — the drums leave.

[pause 400ms]

Halfway through tracking, they discovered that you cannot split a normal song across four CDs. They threw out months of work.

The record that shipped — *Zaireeka* — was the first project ever tracked at Tarbox Road Studios. The studio we will namecheck again when we get to the Fridmann bass in Week 4 and the MGMT compressors in Week 8. *Zaireeka* was what built the room.

[pause 500ms]

The lesson for this week's five-mutations framework: **the constraint is the generator.**

If you are stuck, it is not because you have run out of ideas. It is because your constraint is not strong enough.

Flaming Lips' constraint was absurd — four CDs, four stereos, four human beings in sync. Your constraint this week is tamer. One loop, five duplicates, five prescribed mutations. But the logic is the same. You do not get to pick the mutation. You apply the rule and you ship.

[pause 400ms]

That is the whole week in one sentence. Everything after this is receipts and knob positions.
