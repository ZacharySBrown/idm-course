[pause 300ms] Mutation four. Reverse.

The rule: reverse *one* element. Not the whole loop. Not the drums. *One* element.

[pause 300ms]

Here are your candidates, ranked by impact per minute of effort.

First — a reversed cymbal swell landing into the downbeat. Grab a crash. Cmd+J to Consolidate. Right-click the consolidated clip, choose Reverse. Slide the tail so the hit lands exactly on bar 1 of the next section. This is the highest-payoff reverse in the catalog. It does more for a transition than any plugin.

Second — a reversed vocal chop. If you brought a vocal through from Week 9, consolidate a one-bar chunk, reverse it, place it on beat 3 of bar 15. Free transition, no mixing.

Third — a reversed snare tail. Consolidate the snare bus for two bars, reverse, and layer it underneath the un-reversed snare. The pull is subtle. The listener feels it before they hear it.

Fourth — a reversed pad. Consolidate the pad across all 16 bars, reverse the whole thing, then crossfade the reversed pad against the forward pad at the midpoint. This is the most time-consuming of the four and the least rewarding for effort, so — save it for when you are out of easier moves.

[pause 400ms]

A receipt, because we name receipts. DJ Dahi, on "Money Trees" off Kendrick Lamar's *good kid, m.A.A.d city*, reversed a Beach House sample and hung the whole chorus on the backwards loop. The move is old. It keeps working because the human ear marks backwards audio as *coming from somewhere else*, which is precisely what a mutation needs to telegraph to the listener.

[pause 300ms]

What you should *not* do this week: reverse the drum loop. Reversed drums are next week's lesson — the beat switch — and they are their own category of pain, with their own transition problems. Do not combine.

One element, reversed, in one section. That is the mutation. Five minutes. Move on.
