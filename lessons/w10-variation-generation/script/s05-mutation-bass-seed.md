[pause 300ms] Mutation two. Bass seed.

The rule: rerun the bassline with a new seed. Keep the drums unchanged. Keep everything above the bass unchanged.

Live 12 ships a generator called Seed. You drop it on a MIDI track. By default it deals acid-style basslines within a ±7 semitone range. Every time you roll the seed value, Live produces a new sequence inside that range, constrained to your key and scale.

[pause 300ms]

Here is the procedure.

One — duplicate the bass MIDI track. Mute the original. Do not delete it. We keep the original around for reference until we bounce.

Two — drop a Seed generator on the duplicate. Set the pitch range to ±7 semitones. That is the default and it is also correct for this exercise. Set density to fifty or seventy percent.

Three — hit the Roll icon. Audition. Roll again. Budget ten rolls total. On the eleventh, you are procrastinating.

Four — when you land one you like, hit Cmd-Shift-C to Capture MIDI. That freezes the seed output as editable notes. You now have a specific bassline you can edit, velocity-ramp, or leave alone.

[pause 400ms]

Caveat. Seed is a dice-roller inside key constraints. It is not a composer. Half the rolls will be forgettable — they will sound like a generator, because they *are* a generator. Budget the ten rolls. Pick one. Move on. This mutation is a 45-minute slot, not a career.

[pause 300ms]

An aside, because we mentioned keys. In Week 5 we tuned kicks to the root and used the pitched kick itself as a bassline — the Aphex R-8 move, circa 1992. If your track is a *Selected Ambient Works* tribute and the kick is already doing bass duty, the bass seed mutation can still work as a counter-line. Tune Seed to an octave up. Let it dance over the kick-as-bass. Two voices, one pitched source.

Otherwise — ten rolls, one pick, capture, bounce, move on.
