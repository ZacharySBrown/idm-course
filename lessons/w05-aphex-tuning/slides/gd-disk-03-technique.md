# The Drukqs technique — solenoid click as rhythmic layer

How Aphex made "Avril 14th" sound like a music box that knows about percussion: <Citation bib="aphex_bleep_qa_2017" />

1. **Write the sequence in a tracker** — Drukqs was "mostly written in PlayerPro for the mac, my fave tracker" per James' July 2017 Bleep Q&A. Step-time; no real-time performance. This is why the note density is physically impossible. <Citation bib="aphex_bleep_qa_2017" />
2. **Send the tracker MIDI to the Disklavier** — via a dedicated MIDI-to-SMF converter. The Disklavier plays the notes at the velocities written.
3. **Mic INSIDE the piano body** — not above the soundboard. Two condensers under the lid, aimed at the action/hammers, not the strings. This is the key decision. Regular piano recording places mics 20–40cm above the strings. Aphex placed them at the mechanism.
4. **Record to Nagra IVS 5 at 15 IPS** — two-track, reel-to-reel. The tape saturation folds solenoid clicks into the noise floor just enough to integrate them with the piano tone.
5. **Prepare the strings, John Cage style** — screws, bolts, felt strips between strings on some Drukqs tracks. Changes timbre without affecting pitch tracking.

The rhythmic layer that results: **solenoid click on every note-on, louder pedal click on every sustain-engage**. Against the piano melody, that click pattern has its own rhythm. Same notes, two voices, one instrument. <Citation bib="wikipedia_disklavier" />

Community note, worth flagging: the "Aphex built MIDI solenoid drum machines himself" claim comes from forum lore not confirmed in a primary interview. The Disklavier technique is confirmed by his own SoundCloud notes.
