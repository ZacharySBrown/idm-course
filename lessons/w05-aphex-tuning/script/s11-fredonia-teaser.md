For further listening — if the microtuning bug has bitten, there's a program at SUNY Fredonia called Sound Recording Technology that takes forty students every two years and turns out working engineers. They run a mastering lab where precision is treated as craft, not afterthought. One of the professors, Bernd Gottinger, has a pile of GRAMMY nominations for classical and orchestral work. Another alumnus, Matt Estep, is Fridmann's full-time assistant at Tarbox Road. The program does not advertise the pipeline. But it exists. <Citation bib="fredonia_srt_program" /> <Citation bib="fredonia_grammy_announcement" />

Why this belongs in the microtuning lesson. [pause 200ms]

Gottinger masters orchestras — which is to say, he listens to ensembles where the players are already in tune, and the mastering job is to hear which instruments are *slightly* off in which passages, and decide what to do about it. That is precision tuning as a professional discipline. The ear trained to hear two cents of drift across a sixty-piece ensemble.

Aphex's move this week is the inverse. Start with percussion that has no tuning at all — a drum is a transient, a thump, a smack — and then *impose* tuning. Load the kick in Simpler, move Transpose, land the fundamental on the root. The ear trained to hear when the forty-one-hertz line locks in, and to not tweak it past that.
