IDM production, week five. Aphex, part one. Tuning drums to the track.

This is the lesson the whole course pivots on. Not because microtuning is rare, or hard, or mystical — it is none of those things — but because it is the first time we ask you to make a production decision at the sampler, before the mix, before the arrangement, before you even commit to a key. [pause 200ms]

The move is simple enough to describe in one sentence. Load a kick in Simpler. Transpose it until the fundamental sits at the root of your track. Do the same for a snare, tuned to the fifth. The drum bus now carries harmony.

If that sounds trivial, good — that's the framing. ~The mythology gets in the way.~ Aphex has been doing this since Selected Ambient Works, 1992. The Roland R-8 had per-pad tuning. He used it. That is most of what "the Aphex drum sound" is.

This week is the MVP lesson of the course, which means every tool we've built gets exercised. You'll hear a dry-versus-tuned A/B on "Express Yourself." You'll see the spectrogram where a bassline appears without a bass track. You'll load a stock Ableton session and move three knobs. And you'll ship a thirty-second bounce at the end — no exceptions, no extensions.

The lesson is forty-five minutes. The exercise is thirty seconds of audio and forty-five minutes of studio time. [pause 200ms] If it takes longer than that, you're not doing the exercise — you're avoiding it.

Let's get into it.
