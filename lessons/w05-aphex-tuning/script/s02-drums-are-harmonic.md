The technique is older than the term. *Microtuning* as vocabulary didn't stick to Aphex's name until the Korg Monologue AFX edition dropped in 2017. But the move was already twenty-five years old on records. [pause 200ms]

Per Discogs and the Reverb Machine gear reconstruction, RDJ's early London rig was four boxes doing most of the work. A Roland R-8 drum machine with the 808 expansion card. A Casio FZ-10M sampler handling roughly eighty percent of the sampled material. A Roland SH-101, butchered beyond recognition — his words. And a Roland TB-303. <Citation bib="discogs_syro" /> <Citation bib="reverbmachine_rdj_gear" />

The relevant box here is the R-8. Not the 808 itself — which he also owned — but the R-8 with the 808 card loaded. The R-8 had something the 808 didn't: individual audio outs, individual tuning per pad, and MIDI control of those parameters. Which meant you could sequence a kick, send it out its own output, and tune it to whatever pitch the track wanted, without touching the other drums.

The 808 kick is, structurally, a sine wave with a pitched decay envelope. It's an oscillator with a specific frequency. If you detune that oscillator low enough, the ear stops hearing percussion and starts hearing a note. [pause 200ms] You have crossed from the drum bus into the harmonic layer of the track without adding a single voice.

On "Xtal" and "Ageispolis," that is the bassline. Listen to them after this lesson. There is no additional bass instrument in the mix. The kick is doing double duty — impact on the downbeat, and sustained tonic between hits, because it's tuned so low its decay *is* the bass note.

The snare and hats, meanwhile, sit on other scale degrees. The drum bus is composed. That is the receipt.
