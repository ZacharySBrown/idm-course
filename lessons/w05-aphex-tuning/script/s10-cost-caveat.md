Every technique has a cost. Name this one before you commit to the signature, so you don't get surprised on the mix pass. [pause 200ms]

Cost one — modulation gets louder. If your track changes key halfway through, your tuned drums are now *out of* tune for the second half. There are two fixes. Either re-bounce a tuned drum stem per key change, or drop a Scale device on the MIDI track and automate its root parameter at the key-change bar. Both are work. Aphex mostly doesn't modulate — his tracks sit in one key — which is partly why the tuned-drum move works so cleanly on his records.

Cost two — the snare body thins. Pitching a snare up five to nine semitones via Simpler Classic mode shortens the sample and raises the formants. The chest thins, the top gets brittle. The partial fix is to layer an untuned version of the same snare underneath the tuned one, at roughly minus six dB, so the body comes back while the tuned layer carries the pitch. It's a two-pad compromise.

Cost three — the low end gets crowded fast. A kick at E1 and a bass synth at E1 are both fighting for around forty-one hertz. If you have both, you need to carve. Either sidechain the bass to the kick — Ableton Compressor with external sidechain, four to six dB of duck — or drop the kick an octave so it sits at E0 while the bass holds E1. The E0 kick is how "Xtal" sits underneath a melodic layer without stepping on it.

Cost four — the one that kills tracks. Over-tuning.

If every drum in the kit is tuned to a scale degree, the ear reads the entire bus as melodic. The groove evaporates. You've built a glockenspiel. [pause 300ms] Stop at kick and snare most of the time. Maybe one hat. The contrast between *tuned* elements and *un-tuned* elements is where the harmonic signature reads — if everything is tuned, nothing is.

Aphex's records work because the rest of the arrangement earns the move. Tuned drums on a weak arrangement sound like tuned drums on a weak arrangement.
