Let's get the units straight, because the vocabulary gets sloppy fast. [pause 200ms]

One semitone is one hundred cents. One octave is twelve hundred. Cents are a linear measure; frequency is logarithmic. Which means detuning something by a hundred cents doubles the pitch distance you've moved in perceived terms, but the frequency ratio is the same whether you started at E1 or E5. Cents scale. Hertz don't.

Three regimes are worth naming, because they do different jobs.

First — plus or minus one to seven cents. This is animation territory. It's what happens when two oscillators on a saw lead are slightly detuned, and the result is shimmer. You can sit here forever and nobody will call the sound "out of tune." Aphex uses this range on sustained pad notes — random-quantize the pitch by plus or minus seven cents per sustained note, the pad shimmers instead of sitting statically. <Citation bib="aphex_pitchfork_microtuning" />

Second — plus or minus fifteen to thirty cents. This is non-12-TET territory. Quarter-tones, just intonation, meantone, Bohlen-Pierce, the historical tunings Bach and Wendy Carlos argued about for different reasons. Ableton Live 12 reads Scala files directly — the .scl format — and applies them globally to every synth that supports tuning messages. <Citation bib="ableton_live_12_manual" /> This is where you go if you want the whole track in a non-standard scale.

Third — plus or minus one hundred cents. One semitone. That is *transposition*. Not microtuning. Regular tuning. Pitching a kick down twelve semitones to land at E1 is transposition, not microtuning, and that's fine. [pause 200ms] The point is that *the same knob does both*, and producers tend to conflate them.

The Korg Monologue AFX edition has all three regimes built in — per-key cent offsets, user-programmable scales, six RDJ-designed microtunings shipped as presets. <Citation bib="korg_monologue_afx_manual" /> You do not need the hardware for this lesson. Simpler has Transpose and Detune. That's the whole surface we'll use.
