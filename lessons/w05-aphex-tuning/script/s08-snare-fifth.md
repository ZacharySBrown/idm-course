Kick to root is the headline move. Snare to the fifth is what turns the drum bus from *tuned* into *composed*. [pause 200ms]

Here's the mechanic. You already pitched your kick to the root — E1 if you're in E minor, C1 if you're in C major, whatever the track's tonic happens to be. Now take your snare one-shot. Load it into a new pad on the same Drum Rack. Simpler, Classic mode.

The fifth of E minor is B. Two frets up on a bass guitar, seven semitones above the root, the most consonant interval you can hit without being the root itself. You want the snare's fundamental to land on B. That is usually a transpose move in the positive five to positive nine semitone range, depending on where the snare sample's original pitch lives. Most commercial snare samples are natural somewhere around A2 to C3 — there is no standard — so just solo the pad, play it against a B held on a synth, and move Transpose until they agree.

Then ten to twenty cents of Detune to sit it exactly in tune. [pause 200ms]

Play the loop. Listen to the two and the four. The backbeat is not just impact anymore — it's *suggesting the chord*. You have drums carrying the root in the kick and the fifth in the snare. That is a power chord, played entirely by percussion. <Citation bib="aphex_pitchfork_microtuning" /> The drum bus is now a harmonic instrument.

Three extensions once the base move is working.

Hats to the octave. Transpose the hi-hat sample up to E2 or E3, then high-pass at three kilohertz to narrow the body — you want the ring, not the chest.

Tom fills to scale degrees. If you're writing a tom descent, tune each tom to a step of the scale. E, D, C, B, A down — the fill now plays a melody.

Second kick layer at the root minus twelve semitones. Pure sub reinforcement.

Then stop. [pause 200ms] Don't tune every element. Drums carrying harmony is a signature; over-tuned drums are a chime.
