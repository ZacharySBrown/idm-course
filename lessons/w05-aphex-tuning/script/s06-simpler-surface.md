Open Ableton. Drag a kick one-shot onto an empty MIDI track. Ableton wraps it in Simpler automatically. Set the mode to **Classic** — the button at the top of the Simpler window, next to One-Shot and Slicing. Classic is the mode where pitch and time are linked, the way hardware samplers did it before time-stretching became a default. <Citation bib="ableton_live_12_manual" />

The Roland R-8, the Casio FZ-10M, the Akai S950 — every sampler RDJ used on the early records worked this way. Pitch down, and the sample plays slower. Pitch up, and it plays faster. This is a feature, not a bug. The kick getting longer when you detune it is exactly the thing that turns it into a bassline.

Three controls matter on the Simpler front panel. [pause 200ms]

First — **Transpose**. In the Pitch section, the big knob. Range is plus or minus forty-eight semitones. This is the coarse move. A kick recorded at C3 in the sampler, transposed down to E1, is your new bassline seed. That is a move of roughly minus twenty semitones. Dial it in with the numeric readout, don't ear-guess it for the first pass.

Second — **Detune**. Right next to Transpose, plus or minus fifty cents. This is the fine knob. After the coarse transpose, play the kick against the track's reference note and detune until the phase beating stops. Your ear will tell you within two seconds when it locks.

Third — **Spread**. The unison control. Leave it at zero for kicks — spread on low frequencies just introduces phase smear. On snares pitched up to a fifth, push Spread to around fifteen percent for a slightly-chorused chest.

In a Drum Rack, every pad is its own Simpler. Each pad has its own Transpose and Detune. This is the same affordance the R-8 exposed per pad in hardware, four decades later, for seven hundred dollars of stock Ableton instead of three grand of vintage gear. [pause 200ms]

You already own it.
