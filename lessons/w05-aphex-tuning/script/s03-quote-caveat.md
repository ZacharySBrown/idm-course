Quick caveat, because this one matters. [pause 200ms]

The quote you've seen pinned to the top of ten different producer forums — *"when it's right in tune, something's off"* — is a community paraphrase. Not a verbatim RDJ quote. Treat it as such.

The closest published version is in the 2017 Pitchfork piece tied to the Monologue launch. James, roughly, and I'm reading the edited transcript: *"A lot of composers before me have been on this mission to change the world by getting off equal temperament, and I'm definitely one of those. When it's right in tune, it's like there's something slightly off."* <Citation bib="aphex_pitchfork_microtuning" />

Which is meaningfully different. The received version reads as mysticism — *right is wrong, wrong is right, the magician speaks.* The actual version is a technical argument about equal temperament. [pause 300ms]

Twelve-tone equal temperament — 12-TET, the default tuning of every piano you've ever heard and every synth you've ever loaded — is a compromise that evenly distributes a mathematical error across all twelve keys. It lets you transpose music without re-tuning the instrument. That's its entire reason for existing. But the cost is that no interval except the octave is *actually* in tune, physically. Thirds are seven cents sharp. Fifths are two cents flat. You have been listening to detuned intervals your entire life. [pause 200ms]

Aphex's claim is that if you stop obeying the compromise — load a just-intonation scale, or retune per-track, or tune individual drums to the key — you can hear the difference. Not a mystical difference. An acoustic one.

So — cite the quote with the asterisk. "Community-attributed paraphrase, near-quote of the 2017 Pitchfork piece." Don't put it in quotation marks on someone else's page as if it's verbatim. The real version is less quotable and more useful.

That's the caveat. Moving on.
