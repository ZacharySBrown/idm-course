Slide s01 · title

Warp is not for tempo matching

Warp is not for tempo matching

Confession. Warp lives in Live’s Sample tab next to Fade, Gain, and a tempo box. The manual files it under time-stretching. Every tutorial on YouTube treats it as the thing you click to make a sample line up with the grid.

That framing is correct, and it is also a waste of the feature.

Warp mode is a sound-design surface. Pick the wrong one and a kick drum turns into wet cardboard. Pick the right one and a six-second drone becomes a sixteen-bar texture bed without a plugin in sight.

This week we treat the Warp dropdown as an instrument. Six modes, each with its own character, its own cost. We will A/B them on one stem — the drums from NEU’s “Hallogallo” — and pay attention to the thing that changes: transient shape, vowel content, noise floor.

Slide s03 · reference

The warp-mode menu, short version

The warp-mode menu, short version

Live 12 ships six modes. In plain language:

  • Beats — slices on transients, resyncs slices to the grid. Kicks stay punchy; held notes glitch. Drums, percussion, short one-shots.
  • Tones — monophonic pitch-tracking granular. Vocals, single-note bass, solo synths.
  • Texture — polyphonic granular. Pads, chords, noisy layers, drones. Flux and Grain Size are creative controls.
  • Re-Pitch — no time-stretching. Speed and pitch are locked together. Varispeed tape simulator. Chipmunk soul lives here.
  • Complex — legacy phase-vocoder. Use Complex Pro instead.
  • Complex Pro — phase-vocoder with Formants and Envelope. Full mixes, layered sources, anything polyphonic where Texture is too weird.

Rule of thumb: if the audio is mostly transient, Beats. Mostly tonal, Tones or Complex Pro. Mostly drone, Texture. Want a tape deck, Re-Pitch.

Cite: bib:ableton_live_12_manual, Warp Modes section.

Slide s04 · concept

Complex Pro — Formants and Envelope

Complex Pro — Formants and Envelope

Two controls do the heavy lifting.

Formants (0–100). Formants are the resonant bumps in a signal’s spectrum — for a voice, the vowel frequencies. Set to 100%, Complex Pro preserves them across pitch shifts. Pitch a vocal up an octave with Formants 100 and it still says “ah,” not “eeeeh.” Set to 0 and you get the aliased-chipmunk character — cheap and sometimes exactly right.

Envelope (16 / 32 / 64 / 128 / 256). Window size, in samples. Smaller windows = better time resolution, worse frequency resolution; larger = the inverse. Defaults:

  • Envelope 64 — hi-hats, cymbals, anything with fast transients
  • Envelope 128 — most full mixes and mid-range content
  • Envelope 256 — bass, kick sustain, sub-heavy material

If the clip is smeary on the top end, drop to 64. If the bass is chattering, raise to 256.

Cite: bib:ableton_live_12_manual.

Slide s05 · concept

Beats mode — transients intact, grid hostile

Beats mode — transients intact, grid hostile

Beats mode is a slicer wearing a warp costume. Under the hood: detect transients, cut the audio at every marker, play each slice at its original speed, and truncate or loop the tail to hit the next grid position.

Three controls matter:

  • Preserve (1/2, 1/4, 1/8, 1/16, 1/32, Transients). Sets the slice grid. Preserve 1/16 = a slice every sixteenth. Transients = slice at every detected hit.
  • Transient Loop Mode (Loop / Loop Off / Loop Forward). Off = clean tails, occasional gaps. Loop = fills gaps by looping the slice tail — can artifact on held notes.
  • Transient Envelope (0–100). Short decay on each slice. 100 = no envelope, clicks at every edge. 0 = fast fade, zipper noise on sustained content.

Beats mode preserves kick punch. It also mangles anything tonal — sustained synths, vocals, chord stabs. Use it on drums and you are safe. Use it on a pad and you will notice.

Cite: bib:ableton_live_12_manual.

Slide s06 · caveat

The pitfall — Complex Pro softens kicks

The pitfall — Complex Pro softens kicks

Drop the Hallogallo drums stem on a track, set warp mode to Complex Pro, and listen to the kick. Something is off. The leading edge is rounded. The click is gone. It reads more like a floor tom than a kick.

This is a known cost of Complex Pro, and it is not a bug. Phase-vocoder reconstruction averages spectral frames across the Envelope window; transients get spread across adjacent frames and lose their attack. Per the compass notes: “Complex Pro softens kick transients.”

Two fixes:

  1. Nudge warp markers. For every kick, drag the warp marker a few milliseconds before the transient. This gives the algorithm a head-start so the attack sits on the grid cleanly.
  2. Layer Beats underneath. Duplicate the track, set the duplicate to Beats mode, high-pass it around 80 Hz or so, low-volume underneath. The original carries the body; the Beats copy carries the punch.

Pick per source. Do not default.

Slide s08 · concept

Texture mode — Flux from stable to cloud

Texture mode — Flux from stable to cloud

Texture is polyphonic granular warping. Two controls, both creative:

  • Grain Size (in milliseconds). 50 ms = smooth, blended, pad-like. 10 ms = glitchy, chattering, broken-CD character. Anywhere between is legitimate.
  • Flux (0–100%). The amount of random variation in grain positioning. Flux 0 = stable, locked-phase, almost Complex-Pro-like. Flux 80–100% = evolving cloud, each grain sampling a slightly different part of the source. The clip never quite repeats.

Practical: take the Hallogallo other stem — guitar and drift — stretch it 2× by halving Seg. BPM, mode = Texture, Grain 50 ms, Flux 60%. You now have a sixteen-bar pad that tracks the original harmonic content but never loops identically.

At Flux 100% with Grain 10 ms, Texture on a drum stem produces a noise wash you can use as a bed. Not what the source sounded like. That is the point.

Slide s09 · concept

Re-Pitch mode — chipmunk-soul territory

Re-Pitch mode — chipmunk-soul territory

Re-Pitch is the simplest warp mode in Live. It does not time-stretch. It speeds the playback up or down; pitch and time are locked together. A tape varispeed in software. Pitch up a semitone, the clip gets shorter. Pitch down an octave, the clip plays at half speed.

This is the mode that built Kanye’s early beats and Just Blaze’s Roc-A-Fella catalog. Chipmunk soul — per the lexicon, per the compass artifact — is Re-Pitch mode with the transpose dialled up 4 to 7 semitones on a soul record. Pitch it up 5 semitones and a baritone turns into a tenor, a tenor turns into a kid. Coupled pitch-and-tempo is the artifact; time-stretch loses the character.

We will spend all of Week 9 on this. For today: pitch the Hallogallo drums up 5 semitones in Re-Pitch. Notice the formant shift, the faster kick decay, the shorter snare tail. Bank the sound.

Settings to remember, pitfalls to flag

What a warp mode actually is

What a warp mode actually is

A warp mode is a dispatch table. Live looks at the clip, looks at the playhead, and routes the audio through one of five algorithms — BeatsBeats warp — Warp mode that slices on transients and retriggers each slice at grid — preserving attack, introducing audible tails or gaps between hits. Default for drum loops. Preserve 1/4 with Transient Envelope 0 gives choppy stretched segments. Fast, low-CPU, honest about its artifacts. wikipedia↗, Tones, TextureTexture warp — Granular warp mode. Grain parameter controls window size (10 ms glitchy, 50 ms smooth). Flux adds random variation between grains — 0 for stable, 80–100% for evolving cloud. The mode that turns any source into a pad. wikipedia↗, Re-PitchRe-Pitch warp — Warp mode that links pitch and tempo the way tape varispeed does — stretching a clip to half length takes it up an octave. The authentic chipmunk-soul artifact. Time-stretch modes lose this character because they preserve pitch. wikipedia↗, or Complex ProComplex Pro warp — Ableton's highest-fidelity phase-vocoder warp mode. Preserves formants at heavy pitch shifts (dial 0–100%), with an Envelope parameter that tunes window size — 64 for hats, 256 for bass. Softens kick transients as a side effect; nudge markers pre-kick or layer Beats mode to fix. wikipedia↗ — before sending samples to the output bus. Complex (legacy) is the same algorithm as Complex Pro with fewer controls; treat them as one.

Each algorithm solves a different problem. Beats solves “keep transients sharp.” Complex Pro solves “pitch and time independently on polyphonic material.” Texture solves “make this into a cloud.” Re-Pitch does no solving at all — it changes the playback rate and hands you the coupled pitch-time result that tape always gave you.

Pick wrong and the algorithm costs more than it gives. The rest of this mini-module is the cost ledger.

Slide wm02 · concept

Beats mode — slice, play, truncate

Beats mode — slice, play, truncate

Beats modeBeats warp — Warp mode that slices on transients and retriggers each slice at grid — preserving attack, introducing audible tails or gaps between hits. Default for drum loops. Preserve 1/4 with Transient Envelope 0 gives choppy stretched segments. Fast, low-CPU, honest about its artifacts. wikipedia↗ does three things in order.

  1. Detect transients. Live runs an onset detector across the clip and drops a slice marker at every significant attack.
  2. Play each slice at source rate. When the transport hits a slice, the slice plays back at the speed it was recorded. Transient intact.
  3. Fill to the next grid point. If the slice ends before the next grid position, the remainder is either silenced (Loop Off), repeated (Loop), or played forward and stopped (Loop Forward).

This is why Beats is cheap and fast: no FFT, no re-synthesis. The trade is that anything tonal — a held chord, a vowel, a sustained synth — gets chopped at arbitrary points and relooped, which produces an audible zipper or chatter.

Use on drums, percussion, one-shots. Not on pads.

Cite: bib:ableton_live_12_manual.

Beats mode is a slicer. Transients stay sharp; held-note tails get cut. Per Ableton Live 12 manual (bib:ableton_live_12_manual).

Complex Pro — phase vocoder block diagram

Complex Pro — phase-vocoder block diagram

Complex ProComplex Pro warp — Ableton's highest-fidelity phase-vocoder warp mode. Preserves formants at heavy pitch shifts (dial 0–100%), with an Envelope parameter that tunes window size — 64 for hats, 256 for bass. Softens kick transients as a side effect; nudge markers pre-kick or layer Beats mode to fix. wikipedia↗ is a phase vocoderphase vocoder — An FFT-based algorithm that decomposes a signal into short overlapping spectral frames, allowing independent manipulation of time and pitch. The engine behind most time-stretch plugins — Ableton's Complex and Complex Pro warp modes, Elastique, PaulStretch. wikipedia↗. The chain:

  1. Cut the signal into overlapping windows. Hann window is the textbook default, hop size typically a quarter of the window length — Ableton does not publish Complex Pro’s actual internals, so treat these as the standard phase-vocoder recipe you’d find in Roads 1996, not as spec. What you actually hear as artifacts is vertical phase coherence breakdown (phasiness on polyphonic transients) and transient smearing — Complex Pro adds formant preservation and phase-locking heuristics not shown in this diagram.
  2. FFTFFT — Fast Fourier Transform — an O(N log N) algorithm that decomposes a signal into its frequency components. The underlying math of every spectrogram, EQ analyzer, and phase vocoder in your session. wikipedia↗ each window to get a complex spectrum.
  3. Split into magnitude and phase. Keep magnitudes as-is, compute the phase advance per frame (how fast each bin’s phase is rotating).
  4. Re-propagate the phase at a new hop size — this is where time changes without pitch changing.
  5. IFFTIFFT — Inverse FFT — converts frequency-domain data back to the time domain. Always paired with an FFT on the way out of a spectral process. In image-to-sound tools like MetaSynth or Photosounder, the IFFT is where the drawing becomes audio. wikipedia↗ back to the time domain.
  6. Overlap-add the windows at the new hop.

That is the entire mechanism. “Formants 100” adds an extra step that shifts the spectral envelope back to the source envelope after pitch shifting — which is how it keeps vowels stable when you transpose. “Envelope” is the window size in samples; it sets the time/frequency trade.

Slide wm04 · concept

Complex Pro — pitch and formant as independent axes

Complex Pro — pitch and formant as independent axes

The point of the Formants knob is that it gives you a second axis. Tape’s varispeed only lets you move along the diagonal: pitch up, everything shifts up, vowels included. That is why pitched-up vocals sound like chipmunks — the resonant bumps that define “ah” versus “ee” (the formants) move with the pitch.

Complex Pro separates the two. You can pitch up +5 semitones with Formants at 100%, and the formants stay at the source frequency — the vocal is higher but still sounds like the original singer. Drop Formants to 0% and the formants ride with the pitch — welcome back to chipmunk territory. Anything in between is a legitimate sound design choice.

The diagram makes it explicit: four points in a plane, only one of which tape could reach.

Slide wm05 · concept

Texture — grain cloud, Flux = stochastic offset

Texture — grain cloud, Flux = stochastic offset

Texture modeTexture warp — Granular warp mode. Grain parameter controls window size (10 ms glitchy, 50 ms smooth). Flux adds random variation between grains — 0 for stable, 80–100% for evolving cloud. The mode that turns any source into a pad. wikipedia↗ is granular synthesisgranular synthesis — Sound built from thousands of short grains (1–100 ms slices) of a source, scattered in time and pitch. Theorized by Iannis Xenakis, formalized by Curtis Roads. In practice: feed any field recording to Granulator III and turn the Scan knob. wikipedia↗ with two creative controls.

  • Grain Size — the length of each individual grain in milliseconds. 50 ms = smooth blended clouds; 10 ms = chatter that reveals pitch of the grain boundary rather than pitch of the source.
  • Flux — the allowed randomness of the grain’s sampling position within the source. Flux 0 = strictly sequential, pseudo-Complex-Pro behaviour. Flux 60 = grains drift a windowed amount. Flux 100 = the grain is allowed to wander, and the clip never repeats the same way twice.

The diagram shows three flux settings for the same four grid positions. Flux 0 locks to the dashed markers; Flux 100 scatters anywhere in the source.

Practical: Texture is what turns a drone into a pad bed; it is also what turns a drum loop into a noise wash when you dial Flux up. The cost: because Flux is stochastic, the print is different every time. Bounce the output, not the source.

Re-Pitch — pitch and time locked, no DSP at all

Re-Pitch — pitch and time locked, no DSP at all

The console — Neve 88RS, 48 channels, discrete

The console — Neve 88RS, 48 channels, discrete

The centrepiece at Tarbox is a Neve 88RS — Neve’s flagship in-line console from the early 2000s, class-A discrete preamps on every input. Per the published Tarbox equipment list (bib:tarbox_road_equipment), 48 channels of 88RS sit in the control room with associated 88R outboard racks.

The four controls that matter for the Fridmann bass chain, annotated on the photo:

  1. Mic pre gain — pushed hot. The 88RS preamp has musical-sounding non-linearity at the top of its gain range. The grit starts at the pre.
  2. Channel EQ — used minimally before compression. Post-compressor EQ happens at the return.
  3. Insert send/return — the point where the Dorrough lives on the bass channel. Insert is pre-fader, pre-EQ-post-pre depending on the routing block.
  4. Bus assign — routes the post-insert signal to the bus that feeds the Studer tape machine.

Signal path — bass DI → 88RS pre → Dorrough insert → tape bus

Signal path — bass DI → 88RS pre → Dorrough insert → tape bus

The whole chain fits on one line of Mermaid. The point is that the bass tone is not a plugin on a track — it is a sequence of analog stages, each adding harmonic content, before the signal ever sees tape.

  • DI or mic on the bass amp. Fridmann historically takes both; the balance varies.
  • Neve 88RS mic pre, gain into the musical non-linear region.
  • Channel insert out to the Dorrough 610.
  • Dorrough 610 — three-band compressor, broadcast-grade limiter at the output. Driven hard enough to crunch.
  • Insert return back into the 88RS channel.
  • Bus send to tape.
  • Studer A80 VU II — 2-inch 24-track. Prints at +3 to +6 VU on peaks; tape adds its own soft-clip and low-end thickening.
  • Playback from tape into the mix.

Every stage is a choice. Slide gd-tarbox-04 · gear deep dive

Dorrough 610 — FM broadcast processor, misused on purpose

Dorrough 610 — FM broadcast processor, misused on purpose

The Dorrough 610 Discriminate Audio Processor was built by Dorrough Electronics in Woodland Hills, California. Its intended customer was an FM radio station. Its intended purpose was to maximise perceived loudness at the transmitter while preventing over-modulation. Broadcast engineers set it up and never touch it.

Fridmann touches it. The four controls that carry the technique:

  1. Low-band threshold — the knob that produces the crunch. Fridmann pushes it far enough into gain reduction that the compressor is working constantly. The low band’s compression is not transparent; at deep reduction, it produces audible harmonic distortion. This is the texture.
  2. Mid-band threshold — sets where the growl lives. A separate decision from the low band; this is the whole point of a multiband.
  3. High-band threshold — kept tame on bass. The box’s high-band behaviour is where broadcast engineers shape the “air” — if the unit is ever put on a mix bus, this knob changes role entirely.
  4. Output limiter — the hard ceiling. Built so FM transmitters never clip; here it caps the crunch at a predictable level.

How Fridmann drives the Dorrough — crunch as a choice

How Fridmann drives the Dorrough — crunch as a choice

Receipts first. Fridmann does not talk chain-and-verse in interviews — the davefridmann.com archive (bib:fridmann_davefridmann_com) is deliberately light on signal-flow — but the consistent pattern across Tarbox coverage:

  • The bass is committed to tape with compression and saturation already printed. This is a choice; most mix engineers keep options open. Fridmann closes them on purpose.
  • The Dorrough is the character shaper, not a transparent dynamic control. If a mix engineer wanted clean bass compression, a 1176 or dbx 160 would do it. The Dorrough is chosen because the non-transparent distortion profile is the sound.
  • The compression is extreme. Per Happy Mag’s reconstruction of the MGMT “Kids” vocal chain (bib:happymag_mgmt_oracular_engineering), Fridmann also reached for “substandard 1990s compressors” when he wanted more breakage — the Dorrough sits on that same aesthetic spectrum: gear used past its transparent range.

The parallel to Week 4: you can reach for Complex Pro because it produces a softened kick, the same way Fridmann reaches for a broadcast compressor because it distorts. Artifact as design.

The Studer A80 as insert — tape is part of the compressor

The Studer A80 as insert — tape is part of the compressor

The Studer A80 VU II is not downstream of the Dorrough — it is part of the same stage. Fridmann prints the Dorrough-crushed bass hot to tape, which means the tape itself performs a second round of soft-clip compression and harmonic addition. The two non-linearities stack.

Three tape controls worth naming:

  • Record level. Hot. ~+3 to +6 VU on peaks. This is where the tape’s soft clip begins. Running the tape at unity would skip this stage.
  • Bias. The high-frequency bias signal governs the hysteresis character. Too much bias dulls the top. Too little makes the highs brittle and lowers headroom at the bottom. Tarbox’s bias setting is an engineering recipe, not a knob Fridmann tweaks on every session.
  • Tape speed. The A80 runs at 15 or 30 IPS. 15 IPS saturates the low end harder (more low-frequency harmonic content); 30 IPS is cleaner. Choice depends on whether the record wants thick or articulate low-end.

Tape is not a warm glow. It is a programmable non-linearity with a specific transfer curve. The next slide shows the curve.

Compressor math — threshold, ratio, attack, release, knee

Compressor math — threshold, ratio, attack, release, knee

The compressor transfer curve is the function from input level to output level. Below threshold, it is a straight line at unity gain (1:1). Above threshold, the slope flattens to 1/ratio. A 4:1 ratio means: for every 4 dB of input above threshold, only 1 dB appears at the output.

Four parameters to hold in your head:

  • Threshold — where compression starts.
  • Ratio — how hard it squashes above threshold.
  • Attack — how fast it gets to the ratio slope after threshold is exceeded.
  • Release — how fast it lets go when input drops below threshold.

The diagram compares two settings on the same axis. The blue line is a gentle bus compression — threshold −18 dB, ratio 4:1. The red line is a Fridmann-style setting — threshold −30 dB, ratio 10:1, fast attack. At those parameters, the compressor is in gain reduction nearly all the time, and the knee region (the curved corner) is where audible harmonic distortion starts to appear. This is the mechanism behind the crunch.

Compressor transfer curve. Above threshold, output rises at 1/ratio. Fridmann's setting is not subtle — 10:1-ish with a very low threshold drives the Dorrough into constant gain reduction, which is where the harmonic distortion shows up.

Tape hysteresis — the algorithm nobody codes on purpose

Tape hysteresis — the algorithm nobody codes on purpose

Magnetic tape’s input/output function is a hysteresis loop. At low levels the response is nearly linear; as input rises, the magnetic domains in the tape oxide saturate and the output compresses; when input reverses, the output lags. The shape traces a loop, not a line — the output is a function of both the input and the recent history of the input.

Three consequences for audio:

  1. Harmonic addition. The non-linear transfer function generates second- and third-order harmonics. On bass, this adds perceptible “weight” by doubling the fundamental an octave up.
  2. Soft-knee compression. At high input levels, the loop flattens — the tape compresses without an explicit threshold.
  3. Low-frequency thickening. At 15 IPS tape speed, the low-end saturation is heavier; the record label’s thump on The Soft Bulletin is in part this.

Modern tape plugins (Kramer Tape, U-he Satin, Ableton’s Saturator in Soft Sine mode) model this loop. None reproduces it perfectly, because the physical system has thermal noise, tape formulation variance, and head wear that any static model ignores. Close is close enough for the Ableton bridge.

Multiband Rack build — the Dorrough approximation

Multiband Rack build — the Dorrough approximation

Build order.

  1. New Audio Effect Rack on the bass track.
  2. Inside the Rack, create three chains labelled low, mid, high.
  3. On each chain, first device is EQ-8 in bandpass mode:
  4. low: lowpass, 200 Hz, 48 dB/oct
  5. mid: bandpass, 200–1200 Hz
  6. high: highpass, 1200 Hz, 48 dB/oct
  7. After each EQ-8, a Glue Compressor:
  8. low chain: threshold −30 dB, ratio 10:1, attack 1 ms, release 100 ms — this is the crunch band
  9. mid chain: threshold −24 dB, ratio 6:1, attack 3 ms, release 60 ms — growl
  10. high chain: threshold −18 dB, ratio 4:1, attack 10 ms, release 100 ms — gentle control
  11. After the Rack, insert Saturator: Analog Clip, +6 dB, Dry/Wet 60%.

The low-chain Glue at 10:1 with a fast attack is where the broadcast-style crunch comes from. The Saturator afterwards is the tape role.

Caveat: Live’s Glue is emulating an SSL bus compressor, not a broadcast limiter. The character is not identical to a Dorrough — it is the same class of tool. If you need more aggression, swap the Saturator for Decapitator Style N (next slide).

Plugin shortcut — Decapitator Style N (Neve germanium)

Plugin shortcut — Decapitator Style N (Neve germanium)

If you own SoundToys Decapitator, there is a faster route. Style N models a Neve 1057 germanium stage — not the 88RS, but same manufacturer, same era of non-linearity. Per the compass artifact’s Decapitator decoder (bib:soundtoys_decapitator_manual):

  • Style N — dull/weighty, thickens synths and bass
  • Drive 7 — deep into the modelled non-linearity
  • Punish on — engages an extra compression stage inside the plugin
  • Low-Cut halfway — keeps DC and mud out of the saturator
  • High-Cut ~6 kHz Steep on — drops the high-band character the way a Neve channel would

Drop Decapitator Style N after the multiband Rack. The plugin handles both the “hot Neve pre” role and the “tape thickening” role in one stage. Less surgical than separate Saturators, more authentic character. Pick per mix.

Caveat: the Style N model is of a Neve input stage, not a broadcast multiband compressor. It gets you the harmonic-character half of the Fridmann bass — it does not replace the multiband part. Keep the Rack upstream.
