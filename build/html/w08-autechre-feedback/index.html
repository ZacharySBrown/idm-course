Slide s01 · title

Autechre — generative, not random

Autechre — generative, not random

Everyone tells you Autechre used Max. Nobody tells you the part that makes the music work: they refuse to use random operators.

Sean Booth, WATMM thread, paraphrased from the SOS April 2004 sit-down: we don’t use random operators because every time you run the process it sounds different. How we play the system dictates how the system responds.

That is the entire week. Not “generative music.” Not “algorithmic.” Procedural. Rule-based, repeatable, composed.

Your Ableton build this week:

  • Rhythm generator → Recombine (Shuffle) → Time Warp → Velocity Shaper → Ornament.
  • Commit after each step.
  • Run it through Roar’s multiband feedback.
  • Bounce. Delete source.

Procedural, not random — the Booth distinction

Procedural, not random

From a WATMM post Booth made on the Confield threads, archived and re-quoted for years: “Algorithmic just means procedural. parhelic triangle wasn’t algo at all, sim gishel was slightly, uviol was a lot… but none of it’s in the way you prob think of ‘algorithmic’ with loads of randomness. It was all planned out, with bits of it being executed after intervals etc — like a track but codified.”

Three tiers on one record:

  • parhelic triangle — procedural, zero algorithmic content
  • sim gishel — slightly algorithmic
  • uviol — highly algorithmic

The distinction matters because it defines your constraint for the week. If a process you build sounds different every render, it is not composition — it is a toy. Re-seed it. Record the run. The run is the piece.

Slide s03 · concept

Confield (2001) — nested sequencers and fluid math

Confield (2001) — what the patch actually did

SOS April 2004, Booth speaking directly: Max/MSP was acquired in 1997 and became the organising metaphor for the studio. “Most of Confield came out of experiments with Max that weren’t really applicable in a club environment.”

Three ingredients of the Confield sound, per the same interview and the WATMM follow-ups:

  • Navier-Stokes fluid-dynamics simulations used as slow continuous modulators for filter, pan, pitch. Fluid math as an LFO replacement.
  • Nested analogue sequencers. One sequencer’s control voltage decides what step another sequencer plays. A third sequencer drives the second at a different rate. Output: quantifiable complexity, not noise.
  • 20+ minute takes edited down. The evening’s job is curation.

This is composition by selecting from a rule-bound output stream. You are not writing bars. You are tuning a system and then choosing.

Slide s05 · concept

Draft 7.30 (2005) — the discipline of pulling back

Draft 7.30 (2005) — the pullback

Confield expanded. Draft 7.30 contracted. On purpose.

Booth, SOS April 2004 follow-up material and later WATMM AMAs: Draft was written “using straight-up normal sequencers and samplers. It’s written note by note, where we know exactly what we put on.” They refused to download new software during production.

Why this matters for you:

  • Expansion without contraction is a trap. You become a tool-collector.
  • After every generative-heavy track you ship, commit to one track built note-by-note with no Max for Live, no new devices, no rhythm generators.
  • The constraint is the composition. If you enjoyed the loop for 8 bars, break it — Rick Rubin via the arrangement chapter.

Every third track: no generators. Compose by typing.

NTS Sessions (2018) — versioning as practice

NTS Sessions (2018) — versioning as practice

Eight hours of music across four 2-hour broadcasts. Described in press and WATMM consensus as “a persistent Max/MSP environment” — the same patch family reworked across years.

Booth, widely quoted from the 2018 press cycle (flag — attributed via multiple Pitchfork + forum transcriptions; we list the interview in needs_bib): “I think the oldest thing is from 2011… weird recent jams using old patches.”

The practice: archival, version-controlled composition. Save many states. Revisit years later. Treat a Max patch the way a software engineer treats a repo.

In Ableton, this maps to:

  • Save Live sets with version numbers. v01, v02, v03.
  • Use Chain Selector + Macro Variations (12.2+) to store 8–16 recallable patch snapshots inside one rack.
  • When a sound idea works, save it as a rack preset with a dated name. Raid the preset folder six months later.

Slide s07 · concept

The four-commit procedural MIDI chain

The four-commit procedural MIDI chain

This is the core exercise of the week. Built from Ableton 12 MIDI Transform & Generate tools per the Live 12 manual.

The chain, in order:

  1. Rhythm generator — Steps 16, Density 55%, Division 1/16, Shift 0. Generate the raw grid.
  2. Recombine (Shuffle) — re-orders the card deck. Commit the new MIDI.
  3. Time Warp — breakpoint speed curve 2.0x → 0.25x across two bars. A snare roll that decelerates.
  4. Velocity Shaper — draw a curve, apply. Kill uniformity.
  5. Ornament — Length 1/64, Density low. Flams and mordents.

Commit each step. Right-click the MIDI clip → Freeze/Flatten, or apply the transform and keep moving. Between steps, drag a copy of the clip into a scratch track so you can revert.

You are performing four discrete compositional acts. Do not stack transforms and apply once — the edits become uncollectable.

Slide s08 · concept

Euclidean + clip envelopes unlinked = polymeter

Euclidean + unlinked clip envelopes = polymeter

Two tricks, stacked.

Euclidean generator (Live 12 manual): distribute N pulses across M steps with rotation R. The Ali Jamieson method:

  • Kick 16/4/0
  • Snare 16/3/2
  • Hat 16/7/1
  • Clave 12/5/0

Four clips, unequal lengths, one Drum Rack. Pattern repeats only at the least common multiple. Forty-plus bars before anything literally recurs.

Clip envelopes unlinked from loop length. In a clip envelope, disable “Linked” on, say, the filter cutoff. Envelope length 7 bars. Audio clip length 4 bars. The filter sweep never lines up with the drum cycle the same way twice.

  • 4-bar drum audio + 7-bar filter LFO = the Autechre polymeter posture.
  • 3-bar pan + 5-bar amp envelope = fully non-repeating motion on a pad.

No randomness touched. Every moment is procedurally derivable. This is the point.

Slide s09 · concept

Roar as the Ableton-native feedback matrix

Roar — the Ableton-native feedback matrix

Before we touch Roar: an apology. The marketing calls it a saturator. It is three distortion stages taped together with a feedback loop. That is better.

Per the Live 12.2+ manual, the IDM drum-bus configuration:

  • Routing: MultiBand, three shaper stages.
  • Low stage (<120 Hz): Tube, Drive 40%, Mix 70%.
  • Mid stage (800 Hz–3 kHz): Diode, Drive 60%, Mix 50%.
  • High stage (>3 kHz): Shards, Drive 25%, Mix 30%.
  • Feedback section: Time 1/16, Amount 15%, routed post-shaper. Start at 15%. Past 25% it screams.

The feedback matrix is what approximates the Autechre Shure Auxpander patchbay routing in a single plugin. “Stuff can go back in and back out as many times as we want it to” — Booth, SOS April 2004.

A/B the stemforge render. stemforge:w08-moanin-ambient-feedback gives you moanin_dry.wav and moanin_ambient.wav. The wet is Moanin’ pushed through the ambient pipeline; your Roar pass runs on top.

Detour — wrongness as signature

Slide s11 · concept

The Max for Live ecosystem — chaos, Markov, probability

Max for Live — chaos, Markov, probability

Three devices. Each replaces a hand-built Max/MSP structure the Autechre duo would have patched themselves.

  • K-Devices ESQ / HEXO. Per-step probability step sequencers. Each step carries its own chance value. ESQ is melodic; HEXO is percussive. This is the “we may have one fader that determines how often a snare does a little roll” posture from the SOS 2004 piece, ported to a UI.
  • Dillon Bastan — Strange Mod. 3D chaotic attractors (Lorenz, Rössler, Thomas). Navier-Stokes-adjacent. Map X/Y/Z outputs to filter, pan, pitch. This is the Confield modulator, 2026-edition.
  • Markov Variations. Markov-chain note sequencer. Feed it a source melody, it generates statistically consistent derivatives. Same family as the Oversteps-era voice-writing patches Booth has described on WATMM.

Pick one. Add the others after you ship a track with the first. Tool-collecting is the trap Draft 7.30 warned us about.

Slide s12 · concept

CPU, Freeze, Bounce in Place — the unglamorous part

CPU, Freeze, Bounce in Place — the unglamorous part

Generative chains get heavy. Three Max for Live devices plus Roar plus a Drum Rack full of sample chains and your session will start glitching.

Per the Live 12 manual, three tools, used in this order:

  • Freeze Track. Right-click a track header → Freeze. Ableton renders the track to a temporary audio file and suspends device processing. CPU recovers. Frozen tracks can still be moved, but device parameters are locked until you Unfreeze.
  • Flatten. After Freeze, right-click → Flatten. The frozen audio becomes a new audio clip; the MIDI and devices are removed. This is the commit.
  • Bounce in Place (Live 12.3 Bounce Groups). For subgroups of tracks. Commit a generative drum bus to one audio track. Delete the source group.

Rule: once a generative chain sounds right, Flatten within the same session. Do not come back to unfreeze. You are buying decision closure.

Exercise — 32 bars, four commits, one bounce

Slide d01 · concept

The four-commit chain, visualized

The four-commit chain, visualized

Four discrete procedural acts. Not a macro. Not a preset. A sequence of decisions, each of which renames the MIDI in place before the next decision sees it.

The shape is: generate a grid → scramble while preserving content → bend time non-linearly → un-uniform the velocity → decorate. Per the Live 12 manual’s MIDI Transform & Generate chapter, each operation can be previewed before commit — use that. Audition, decide, commit, move on.

If you try to chain two transforms without committing in between, the second one operates on a preview, not on notes, and you lose the ability to revert step-by-step. One commit per act. The commits are the composition.

Slide d02 · concept

Procedural vs random — same generator, two runs

Procedural vs random — same generator, two runs

Imagine the same 16-step rhythm generator, run twice.

Run A — random. Each step’s probability rolls a fresh Math.random() at playback. Render 1 gives you hits at 1, 4, 7, 9, 13. Render 2 gives you hits at 2, 5, 8, 10, 11, 15. The pattern is different every render. Booth’s complaint lives here: you cannot play this system, you can only wait for a take you like.

Run B — procedural. Steps are chosen by a rule-based modulator — a counter, an LFO quantized to step positions, a Markov chain with fixed transition weights. Render 1 and render 2 produce the same pattern. Change the seed or the weights — the pattern changes predictably. You can play this system.

The Autechre rule: if the render isn’t reproducible, it isn’t finished. Commit the run. The run is the piece.

Elektron Machinedrum + Monomachine — the live rig

Elektron Machinedrum + Monomachine — the live rig

Mid-2000s, Autechre stopped touring with laptops. The live rig that replaced them: Elektron Machinedrum SPS-1UW MKII on drums, Elektron Monomachine SFX-60 MKII on synths, a Nord Modular G2 for modulation glue, and an Akai MPC1000 for occasional playback. The whole thing ran without a computer.

The reason this matters for us: the parameter lock workflow Elektron shipped in these two boxes is the first mass-market implementation of what Autechre were already doing in Max — procedural variation baked into a step sequencer UI, no randomness required.

Studio rig was different (Max/MSP in MOTU Digital Performer, Shure Auxpander patchbay) — this deep-dive is the live-era paradigm.

Slide gd-elektron-02 · gear deep dive

Machinedrum tour — the five controls that matter

Machinedrum tour — the five controls that matter

Six-track pattern sequencer (16 steps, 16 patterns × 16 banks = 256 patterns), two global effects, and per-step parameter locks on every control visible on the front panel.

What to look at on the panel:

  • Trig keys (16). Per-step note on/off. Classic Roland-style entry.
  • Function + Trig. Hold FUNC, hit a trig — you arm a p-lock on that step. Whatever knob you move next is now locked to that step only.
  • Param encoders (6 rows × 8 columns). Synthesis parameters per machine. On the UW model: SYN, AMP, FLT, EFF, LFO, ROUT — each 8 knobs.
  • Kit banks. 64 kits per project. A kit is a machine-type + parameter snapshot per track.
  • Machine selector. TRX (analog modelling), EFM (FM drums), E12 (12-bit sample playback), P-I (physical impulse), GND (noise-based). Swapping machine type on a track is a single button press.

Cite for all of the above: Machinedrum SPS-1UW MKII User Manual, chapter 8 (Trig Conditions and Parameter Locks).

Monomachine tour — six voices, six engines

Monomachine tour — six voices, six engines

Six-track mono synthesizer. Same step-sequencer architecture as the Machinedrum, same p-lock discipline, different sound-generation backend.

The machine types you can load per track:

  • SID — emulates the Commodore 64 6581 chip. Square/pulse/tri, ring mod, hard sync. Gritty.
  • FM — 4-operator FM. DX100-adjacent. The Autechre metallic-bell timbre lives here.
  • WAVE — single-cycle wavetable. 16 banks of 64 waves.
  • VO — formant-synthesis vocal-ish. Bizarre.
  • DPRO — digital pulse. Cheap PWM with modulation.
  • SuperWave — analog-modelling supersaw.

Per-track effects: AM, distortion, filter (2-pole LP/BP/HP/BS), delay, reverb send. All p-lockable.

The live interplay: Monomachine sequences melodic material; Machinedrum sequences drums; Nord G2 routes audio-rate modulation between them via CV; both Elektrons sync to the same MIDI clock.

Technique — p-locks as live-programmed variation

Technique — p-locks as live-programmed variation

The move is: program a static pattern, then lock a single parameter to a different value on every step.

Example — classic Machinedrum snare variation:

  1. Track 2 (SD), machine type E12, sample slot 17 (a rimshot).
  2. Program trigs on steps 4, 8, 12, 16 — the backbeat.
  3. FUNC + step 4 → SYN/Pitch encoder → +0. Step 8 → +5. Step 12 → +2. Step 16 → –3.
  4. Hit play. The snare backbeat is now a four-pitch melodic fragment. No randomness. Every step deterministic, every step different.

The Autechre extension, per Elektronauts forum analysis of live sets: p-lock every knob on every step, then vary sequencer length per track (track 1 plays 16 steps, track 2 plays 15 steps, track 3 plays 13 steps). The pattern repeats only at the LCM — 16×15×13 = 3120 steps, or about 13 minutes at 120 BPM.

That is “procedural, not random” implemented in an interface. The variation is composed.

Slide gd-elektron-05 · gear deep dive

Technique — cross-sequencing the two boxes

Technique continued — cross-sequencing Machinedrum and Monomachine

Autechre did not run the two boxes as two independent sequencers. They cross-sequenced them. The receipts for the exact routing are community consensus on Elektronauts and WATMM (flag: not an officially documented patch — forum-reconstructed from live-set video analysis), but the general move is well documented:

  • Machinedrum LFO → Monomachine CC input. Use the Machinedrum’s per-track LFO (shape, rate, depth) as a modulator routed via MIDI CC to a Monomachine filter cutoff. The drum track “plays” the synth filter.
  • Monomachine MIDI sequencer tracks → Machinedrum trigger input. Monomachine has two MIDI-out tracks; route one to a Machinedrum drum track’s trigger. The synth pattern triggers the kick.
  • Nord G2 as glue. The G2 takes in CC data from both, applies chaotic modulation (built-in Lorenz-style oscillators), and routes back.

Booth in SOS April 2004 on the broader studio philosophy: “Stuff can go back in and back out as many times as we want it to.” The same routing mindset, implemented in a MIDI mixer box.

Algorithm — step sequencer with per-step override

Algorithm — step sequencer with per-step parameter override

The Elektron data model, simplified:

Pattern[step] = {
  trig: bool,                    // note on/off for this step
  note: int,                     // pitch (for melodic tracks)
  velocity: int,                 // 1–127
  plocks: {param_id → value}     // per-step parameter override dict
}

At playback, for each step the sequencer emits the trig + note, and for every plock entry it writes the locked value to the parameter bus before the voice is retriggered. When the step passes, the parameter bus reverts to the kit’s base value.

Three properties that matter:

  1. Deterministic. Same pattern, same run, same output. No seed required.
  2. Independently modulatable. Because plocks are per-step, a parameter can have 16 different values across a 16-step pattern — the “16 snares” effect.
  3. Per-track length. Each of the 6 tracks can have its own length (1–64 steps). Polymeter falls out for free.

This is a UI-level generative system. The “procedural not random” constraint lives at the hardware level: there is literally no random-trigger parameter on the front panel.

Slide gd-elektron-07 · gear deep dive

Algorithm — constraints and limits

Algorithm continued — constraints and limits

The Machinedrum + Monomachine duo are not magic; they are constrained in very specific ways that shaped Autechre’s live sound.

Hard limits:

  • Max 16 steps per pattern, 1/16 resolution. For finer timing you either chain patterns or use the TRC (trig condition) feature on later OS versions. Micro-timing is per-step offset, not continuous.
  • Six voices per machine. Drum kits are limited to 6 concurrent tracks; the Monomachine likewise 6. Stacking sounds means committing upstream or muting live.
  • No random operators in the sequencer. Later OS versions added Trig Conditions (A:B, PRE, NEI, FILL) which are probabilistic — Autechre largely pre-date this on the rig they toured. Forum consensus: they used Retrig and length variation, not probability.
  • Mono audio per track. Stereo is achieved via routing to the stereo master bus or external effects. Intra-track stereo imaging is limited.
  • No audio recording of the internal state. You record the stereo output; the per-step plock values are not exported.

The constraint set is what makes the rig composable. Random would have broken the promise.

Ableton bridge — Push 3 step mode

Ableton bridge — Push 3 step mode + per-step automation

The Elektron p-lock paradigm maps cleanly onto two Ableton 12 surfaces.

Push 3 step edit mode. Per the Push 3 manual: hold a step pad, turn any device knob — that parameter value is locked to that step only. Same gesture as FUNC + step on the Machinedrum. Push also exposes the Rhythm generator Drum Rack layout for live Auto-generation per voice.

Clip envelopes per parameter. On a MIDI or audio clip, expose an envelope for any device parameter (filter cutoff, FM ratio, send level). Draw 16 breakpoints across 16 steps. The clip now carries per-step parameter data just like a Machinedrum pattern.

Parallel M4L — HEXO, ESQ, Euclidean Pro

Parallel Max for Live devices

Three devices replicate specific Machinedrum/Monomachine moves.

  • K-Devices HEXO. Per-step probability percussion sequencer with six tracks. The straight translation of the Machinedrum trig-condition workflow. Unlike the Elektron, HEXO does allow probability — use it deterministically (100% or 0% per step) if you want the Autechre posture, or tune probability per step if you want the later-OS Elektron conditional feel.
  • K-Devices ESQ. Same idea for melodic voices. Six tracks, per-step pitch/velocity/duration/probability locks. Maps to Monomachine MIDI tracks.
  • alkman Euclidean Sequencer Pro. Per-track Euclidean distribution with rotation. Combine with HEXO for the per-track-length polymeter effect — 16/4, 15/3, 13/5 — that the Elektrons achieved through independent track lengths.

Rack structure: one MIDI track per voice, HEXO or ESQ at the head of each chain, Drum Rack or synth downstream, Macro Variations storing “kits” as full rack snapshots. Select kit, play, commit, save. Same workflow as selecting Machinedrum kits mid-pattern.

Slide gd-elektron-10 · gear deep dive

What else — chord stabs, granular, audio-rate LFO

What else the hardware can do

Not every Machinedrum move is a drum move. The UW (“user-waveform”) revision of the Machinedrum shipped with sample playback — the thing becomes a 6-track sample instrument the moment you load a kit of non-drum samples.

Non-obvious uses, pulled from Elektronauts threads and Autechre live-set reconstructions:

  • Chord stabs on a drum track. Load a 4-second pad sample into an E12 machine, p-lock pitch per step. The “drum” is now a chord sequencer.
  • Granular-adjacent playback. UW machines with very short samples (<200 ms) and p-locked start point per step. You get a micro-grain stream that is still step-quantized.
  • Audio-rate LFO via Nord G2. Route an audio signal from the Machinedrum into the Nord G2, use the G2 as an audio-rate modulator for a Monomachine filter. The drum becomes the modulator.
  • Monomachine as drum synth. FM machine tuned low with a very fast decay envelope — the Monomachine kick is a DX100-style kick. This is how Autechre got the metallic/woody drum-synth sound without using the Machinedrum.

The rig is not six drums + six synths. It is twelve voices with interchangeable roles.

Parallel modern workflows — Push 3, Digitakt II, Renoise

Parallel use cases in modern workflows

Exercise A — the 16-pitch snare (replicate)

Exercise A — the 16-pitch snare

Replicate the Machinedrum p-lock move in Ableton. No Elektron hardware required.

Steps:

  1. Open Push 3, create a new MIDI track, load Drum Rack with one slot — slot D1 is a snare Simpler loaded with any crisp rim/snap sample.
  2. Push: step mode. Program trigs on steps 1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 13, 15 — every odd step.
  3. For each trigged step: hold the step pad, turn the Simpler Transpose knob to a different semitone value. Write down the 8 values.
  4. No randomization. No dice. You are picking eight pitches by hand.
  5. Loop, listen. Commit the MIDI clip (right-click → Freeze + Flatten the track if you want the audio).

Deliverable. One 2-bar bounced snare pattern with 8 deliberate pitches. Nothing else — no reverb, no effects. The point is the p-lock discipline in isolation.

Target time: 15 minutes.

Exercise B — nested LFOs modulating each other (explore)

Exercise B — nested LFOs modulating each other

The Navier-Stokes territory Booth and Brown were chasing — slow, continuous, non-repeating modulation — is achievable today with one Max for Live device and an afternoon.

Build it:

  1. New MIDI track, Wavetable or Meld instrument, one sustained note held by a 64-bar MIDI clip.
  2. Insert Max for Live LFO device #1 on the track. Routing: LFO1 output → LFO2 Rate parameter.
  3. Insert Max for Live LFO device #2 on the same track. Routing: LFO2 output → Wavetable filter cutoff.
  4. Now insert Max for Live LFO device #3. Routing: LFO3 output → LFO1 Rate. The three LFOs modulate each other’s rates in a cycle.
  5. Set all three to different base rates (LFO1: 1/4, LFO2: 1/3, LFO3: 1/8) and different depths.
  6. Play. The filter cutoff will move in a way that is fully deterministic (no random) but non-periodic at any listenable timescale.

Deliverable. A 32-bar recording of the sustained note with the self-modulating filter. The render is reproducible — same clip, same automation, same output. Procedural, not random.

This is the poor-person’s Lorenz attractor. The next deep-dive slide (d02 / gd-maxmsp-13) gives you the real thing.

Max/MSP — the studio as patched graph

Max/MSP — the studio as patched graph

Cycling ‘74’s Max started life as Max (1986, control-rate only), grew MSP in 1997 (signal-rate audio), added Jitter in 2002 (video/matrix), and shipped gen~ in 2012 (sample-accurate codebox DSP). Booth acquired a copy in 1997 per SOS April 2004: “Most of Confield came out of experiments with Max that weren’t really applicable in a club environment.”

The paradigm: every audio or MIDI signal is a wire between objects. No timeline. No clip. No session. A patch is a graph; the graph is the instrument.

This deep-dive treats Max as a composition environment — the thing that generated Confield, LP5, Untilted, Oversteps, Exai, and the NTS Sessions patch family — then maps it onto Max for Live, which is the same engine embedded inside Ableton 12.

Slide gd-maxmsp-02 · gear deep dive

Tour — the five object families

Tour — the five object families

Max patches look chaotic until you recognize the types.

  • UI objects — number, int, flonum, toggle, button, message, slider, dial. These are the inlets and outlets a human touches.
  • Max (control-rate) objects — metro, counter, select, gate, route, pack/unpack, trigger. These run at the scheduler rate (ms precision). MIDI lives here.
  • MSP (signal-rate) objects — cycle~, phasor~, noise~, gate~, *~, +~. The tilde suffix means “audio-rate.” These run at the sample rate (44.1 kHz / 48 kHz).
  • Jitter (matrix) objects — jit.matrix, jit.noise, jit.op. Video and multi-dimensional arrays.
  • Abstractions / patchers — patcher, bpatcher, poly~. User-defined subpatches. A big Autechre patch is 99% abstractions.

The visual grammar: thin black cords = Max messages, red/yellow striped cords = signal-rate audio, green cords = MIDI. Cords travel top-to-bottom, left-to-right.

Slide gd-maxmsp-03 · gear deep dive

Tour — scheduler clock vs signal vector

Tour continued — the scheduler and signal vectors

Two clocks run inside a Max patch.

The scheduler clock. Drives Max (non-tilde) objects. Resolution is typically 1 ms; configurable via “Overdrive” and “Scheduler in Audio Interrupt” toggles in the options pane. A metro 250 object emits bangs every 250 ms. Control-rate data is ms-accurate, not sample-accurate.

The signal vector. Drives MSP (tilde) objects. The DSP engine processes audio in fixed-size chunks — default 64 samples at 44.1 kHz = about 1.45 ms per vector. Signal Vector Size and I/O Vector Size in DSP options determine latency vs CPU tradeoff. Smaller vector = lower latency, higher CPU.

gen~ collapses the signal-vector distinction for audio DSP. Inside a gen~ patcher, every operation is sample-accurate — you write one-sample-at-a-time code (via the codebox object) and Max compiles it into an efficient DSP chain at load.

For Autechre-grade micro-timing drums, the Max scheduler is not precise enough — you want gen~ or phasor~-driven triggering for sample-accurate hits. This is why the Max patches you see in WATMM screenshots have a lot of tildes on the audio path.

Technique — Confield-era nested sequencer patches

Technique — Confield-era patches

SOS April 2004, Booth: the Confield compositional move was nested analogue sequencers emulated in Max, with fluid-math modulation routed to filter, pan, pitch.

Reconstructed patch shape (community consensus, not a leaked .maxpat):

  • Sequencer 1 — counter + coll (a data store), outputs a CV-like value per step. Step period 1/16.
  • Sequencer 2 — identical structure, but its rate is set by the output of Sequencer 1. When Sequencer 1 outputs a low value, Sequencer 2 runs slow. When high, fast.
  • Sequencer 3 — gates Sequencer 2’s output. When Sequencer 3 is low, Sequencer 2’s notes are muted. Sequencer 3’s rate is set by an LFO driven by a Navier-Stokes fluid simulation rendered in Jitter.
  • Final stage — a gen~ drum synth, triggered by the Sequencer 2 output. The kick pitch is p-locked to Sequencer 1’s current value.

Output: a drum pattern that evolves over minutes, never repeats in the first 20, is fully deterministic. Record 20 minutes to disk. Edit down. Ship.

“That is the patch.” is an oversimplification — the real patches have dozens more objects — but this is the compositional skeleton.

Technique — gen~ for audio-rate DSP

Technique continued — gen~ for audio-rate DSP

Plain MSP objects are fine for LFOs and mixers; for custom oscillators, filter algorithms, and waveshapers you want gen~.

gen~ exposes a sample-accurate language (GenExpr, or a node-based patcher) that compiles to optimized DSP. Key idioms:

  • history — a one-sample delay. Lets you write feedback equations. y = x + history(y) * 0.5 is a single-pole IIR filter.
  • codebox — write GenExpr code inline. For-loops, conditionals, sample-accurate state.
  • data / buffer — read/write buffers at audio rate.
  • Chaotic attractors — a Lorenz or Rössler system is four lines of codebox. Output the X/Y/Z channels at audio rate; resample down for modulation.

The Autechre move is to use gen~ patches as self-contained instruments: a whole drum synth lives in one gen~ box. Inputs are trigger, pitch, decay. Outputs are left/right audio. The patch above — five Max-rate objects + one gen~ drum synth — is a credible Untilted-era skeleton.

Algorithm — the dataflow model

Algorithm — dataflow model

Max is a dataflow language. Execution is not “top to bottom line by line” — it is “right-inlet-first, then left, then the next message.”

Three rules that catch every beginner:

  1. Right-to-left, top-to-bottom order. When an object has multiple outlets, Max fires the rightmost outlet first, then middle, then leftmost. Same for inlets. Mis-wiring here gives you the “my patch is one tick late” bug.
  2. Hot vs cold inlets. A left (hot) inlet triggers output. Right (cold) inlets just store values. The classic [+] object: right inlet sets the addend, left inlet triggers output. [trigger b b] (“t b b”) is how you enforce explicit fire order.
  3. Messages vs signals. Messages are discrete events with a timestamp. Signals are continuous sample streams. Connecting a message outlet to a signal inlet is legal (converted via sig~) but the other direction requires snapshot~ or change to sample the signal into a message.

The dataflow model is why Max patches look like schematics. They are schematics. Read them like analog synth patch cables, not like a script.

Slide gd-maxmsp-07 · gear deep dive

Algorithm — the scheduler's cost

Algorithm continued — the scheduler’s cost

Max’s scheduler is a cooperative single-threaded event queue. A long-running object blocks everything downstream.

Practical constraints for Autechre-style patches:

  • Overdrive on — scheduler runs at higher priority than the UI thread. Turn this on always for music use.
  • Scheduler-in-Audio-Interrupt (SIAI) — scheduler runs inside the audio thread. Tighter MIDI/audio sync at the cost of lower allowed DSP headroom.
  • Signal Vector Size 64 — 1.45 ms DSP latency. Any lower and CPU goes up. For live-performance patches with dozens of voices, many Autechre-adjacent patches run at SVS 128 or 256.
  • poly~ for polyphony — spawn N voices of a subpatch on demand. The voice-allocation pattern.
  • thispoly~ for voice management — lets each voice know when to free itself.

Limits that bit Autechre in the Confield era and still bite you: CPU cost scales with active voices, and a Max patch that was fine at 4 voices will crackle at 16. Freeze sub-patches to audio when you can; use poly~ with a modest voice cap; run DSP at a larger vector when performance matters more than latency.

Ableton bridge — Max for Live is Max embedded

Ableton bridge — Max for Live is Max embedded

Max for Live is literally the same Max runtime running inside Ableton. A .amxd file is a Max patch plus a small wrapper that exposes Live API objects.

Three kinds of M4L device:

  • Max MIDI Effect — between a MIDI input and an instrument. For generative MIDI (Markov, Euclidean, probability).
  • Max Audio Effect — inserts in the audio chain. For custom DSP (gen~-based saturators, grain engines, filters).
  • Max Instrument — a synth. Receives MIDI, outputs audio.

The Live API exposes the session tree: tracks, clips, devices, parameters, transport. A M4L device can read and write any Live parameter by walking the tree. This is how Dillon Bastan’s data.mod writes modulation into any mapped parameter — it opens a Live API connection and pushes values.

Upshot: every Max patch you can think of can be ported to M4L. The Confield sequencer skeleton above (5 Max-rate objects + gen~ drum synth) becomes a single .amxd instrument. Drop it into a MIDI track, done.

The M4L ecosystem — who ships what

What else Max can do

What else Max can do

Max is not just the IDM-patch thing. Once the dataflow clicks, it is the most flexible prototyping environment in the studio.

Non-IDM uses worth knowing:

  • Instrument mapping layer. A Max patch that takes in OSC from a phone, remaps, and sends MIDI to Live. Zero code for a custom controller.
  • Sample management. Build a sample browser with umenu + sfinfo~ + sfplay~. Preview, tag, drop into Live via drag.
  • Generative video. Jitter is a full-on video environment. jit.gl.* objects render OpenGL. Sync to Live’s transport.
  • Hardware protocol bridges. Serial → MIDI, USB-HID → OSC, MQTT → anything. Max speaks every protocol with one or two external objects.
  • Compositional sketches. Before committing a generative idea to a full M4L device, prototype it as a standalone patch. You do not have to open Live at all until the idea works.

For the course scope, we stay in M4L. But knowing the ceiling exists is useful — a lot of Autechre live-set mysteries dissolve once you realize Max can do literally anything you can wire into it.

Parallel modern workflows — Pd, SuperCollider, Bitwig Grid

Parallel modern workflows

Max has competitors. Some share DNA; some take the idea in different directions.

  • Pure Data (Pd) — the open-source cousin, by the same person (Miller Puckette) who wrote Max’s predecessor at IRCAM. Visually similar; fewer high-level abstractions; runs on a Raspberry Pi.
  • Reaktor Core (Native Instruments) — similar graph-based DSP, different object library, shipped with Komplete.
  • VCV Rack — modular-synth-as-software. Modules are wired like Max signal objects. Much narrower scope.
  • Bitwig Studio Grid — built into Bitwig, audio-rate + modulation graph. The most elegant modern take on the signal-flow idea.
  • SuperCollider — text-based, client-server, has the longest research pedigree. Not visual; closest to academic computer music.
  • TouchDesigner — Jitter’s video-side competitor. Sound is a second-class citizen; visuals are first-class.

Autechre have worked in several of these at different times. Booth has mentioned SuperCollider and custom C code on WATMM. The point: the paradigm is transferable. Learn the dataflow model once, port it to whichever tool the session demands.

Exercise A — M4L four-note step sequencer (replicate)

Exercise A — a four-note step sequencer, M4L

Build the simplest possible Max-for-Live step sequencer and drop it on a drum track.

Steps:

  1. In Live, create a new MIDI track. Add a Drum Rack with four slots (kick, snare, closed hat, open hat).
  2. Add a Max MIDI Effect device on the track. Hit the Edit button (pencil icon) — Max opens.
  3. Build this graph:
  4. metro 125 (fires every 125 ms = 16th at 120 BPM) → counter 0 3 (counts 0,1,2,3 repeating).
  5. counter output → route 0 1 2 3 (fans into four outlets by value).
  6. Each of the four outlets → makenote 127 100 (velocity 127, duration 100ms) → noteout.
  7. Each makenote has a different note value: 36 (C1), 38 (D1), 42 (F#1), 46 (A#1) — standard GM drum map.
  8. Freeze the patch. Save.
  9. Hit play in Live. You hear kick, snare, closed hat, open hat in a 4-step loop.

Deliverable. One M4L MIDI device that plays a 4-step pattern into a drum track, checked in. 20 minutes.

Extension: replace counter with random 4 — note the pattern now changes every bar. Then remove the random. That is the Autechre choice, made in one gesture.

Exercise B — gen~ Lorenz attractor modulator (explore)

Exercise B — gen~ Lorenz attractor as rhythm generator

The ancestor move. A Lorenz system is three coupled ODEs; its phase-space trajectory is a butterfly. Used as a modulator, it produces slow, non-repeating, fully-deterministic motion. This is the Navier-Stokes idea, simplified.

The equations:

dx/dt = σ(y − x)
dy/dt = x(ρ − z) − y
dz/dt = xy − βz

With σ=10, ρ=28, β=8/3, the system is chaotic but bounded.

Build as a Max for Live audio effect (or MIDI effect with snapshot~):

  1. New M4L audio effect. Inside, make a gen~ object.
  2. Inside gen~, use a codebox: History x(1.0), y(1.0), z(1.0); sigma = 10; rho = 28; beta = 8/3; dt = 0.005; dx = sigma * (y - x); dy = x * (rho - z) - y; dz = x*y - beta*z; x += dx * dt; y += dy * dt; z += dz * dt; out1 = x / 20; out2 = y / 30; out3 = z / 40;
  3. Outside gen~, snapshot~ 20 samples the three outputs 50 Hz. Route to three live.thisdevice modulation outputs mapped to a filter cutoff, a drum rack macro, and a reverb size on the track.
  4. As a rhythm source: compare z-axis to a threshold. When z crosses, emit a trigger. The crossing rate is irregular but fully deterministic.

Deliverable. A 64-bar rendered audio clip where the filter, macro, and reverb are all driven by the same Lorenz system. Render twice — outputs will be identical. Deterministic. Non-periodic. Ship it.
