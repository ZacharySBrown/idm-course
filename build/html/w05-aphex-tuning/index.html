Slide s02 · concept

Drums are harmonic content — the 1992 receipt

Drums are harmonic content — the 1992 receipt

The technique is older than the term. Per Discogs and the contemporaneous Reverb Machine gear reconstruction — RDJ’s early-London rig ran a Roland R-8 with the 808 expansion card, a Casio FZ-10M for ~80% of sampling, and a butchered SH-101.

The R-8 had individual outs and individual tuning per pad. The 808 kick is a sine with a pitched envelope — detune it, and the fundamental moves. Detune it far enough and it stops being percussion and starts being a note.

On “Xtal” and “Ageispolis,” that note is the bassline. There is no second bass part underneath. The kick is tuned to the root. The snare and hats sit on other scale degrees. The drum bus is carrying the harmony.

This is not a mix decision. It is a composition decision made at the sampler.

Slide s03 · caveat

The quote everyone paraphrases — what he actually said

The quote everyone paraphrases — what he actually said

The line pinned to producer forums — “when it’s right in tune, something’s off” — is a community paraphrase, not a verbatim quote. Flag it as such.

The closest published version is in the 2017 Pitchfork piece tied to the Korg Monologue AFX launch. James, roughly: “A lot of composers before me have been on this mission to change the world by getting off equal temperament… When it’s right in tune, it’s like there’s something slightly off.”

The received one-liner compresses that. Cite it with the asterisk.

Why it matters: the real claim is not mystical. 12-TET — the default of every piano and synth you’ve touched — is a compromise, and the compromise is audible if you stop obeying it.

That is the pillar this week rests on.

Slide s04 · concept

What microtuning actually is, in cents

What microtuning actually is, in cents

One semitone = 100 cents. One octave = 1200 cents. Cents are linear; frequency is logarithmic.

Three regimes worth naming:

  • ±1–7 cents — animation range. The shimmer you hear when two oscillators are slightly detuned. Aphex uses this on sustained pad notes.
  • ±15–30 cents — non-12-TET territory. Just intonation, meantone, Bohlen-Pierce. Load these as Scala files into Live 12.
  • ±100 cents = ±1 semitone — transposition. The knob you turn for kicks.

The Korg Monologue AFX has microtuning built in — per-key cent offsets, user-programmable scales, six RDJ-designed presets. You do not need the hardware. Simpler has the same knob.

Slide t02 · concept

12-EDO, 19-EDO, 17-EDO — the equal-division grid

12-EDO, 19-EDO, 17-EDO — the equal-division grid

Equal Division of the OctaveEDO (equal divisions of the octave) — A tuning system dividing the octave into N equal steps. 12-EDO = standard Western temperament. 24-EDO = quarter tones. 31-EDO, 53-EDO approximate just intonation to varying degrees. Scala (.scl) files can describe any of them, and Live 12's tuning systems can load them. wikipedia↗ — the category that contains 12-TET as its most famous member. Divide the 1200-cent octave into N equal steps. N=12 is the piano. N=19 is a Renaissance favorite. N=17 is a common Arabic-classical approximation.

  • 12-EDO — 100 cents per step. Every interval is a compromise against pure integer ratios.
  • 19-EDO — 63.16 cents per step. Better minor thirds (315.8 cents ≈ the pure 5:4 at 315.6 cents — wait, that’s a major third). Better at approximating 5-limit JI intervals than 12.
  • 17-EDO — 70.59 cents per step. Sharp fifths (705.9 cents vs pure 701.96 cents), zero pure thirds. Jagged. Aphex’s appetite.
  • 24-EDO — quarter-tones. Middle Eastern maqam scales approximate here.

Scala files (.scl) encode any EDO or non-EDO tuning as a flat text list of cent offsets or ratios. Live 12 reads them natively in the Tuning Systems dialog.

The Huygens-Fokker Stichting hosts ~5000 historical tunings as free .scl downloads. Most of them were not designed for drums. That is their opportunity.

Slide t03 · concept

Just intonation vs equal temperament — the ratio war

Just intonation vs equal temperament — the ratio war

Just intonationratio (just intonation) — A tuning system where intervals are small-integer frequency ratios (3:2 for a perfect fifth, 5:4 for a major third) rather than equal divisions. Pure-sounding in a single key; wanders out of tune when you modulate. The ancestor system to equal temperament. wikipedia↗ tunes every interval to a small-integer frequency ratio. Perfect fifth = 3:2. Major third = 5:4. Minor third = 6:5. Minor seventh = 7:4. Beat-free. Ancient.

Equal temperament tunes every interval to a fixed 100-cent step. It is deliberately wrong on every interval except the octave, so that every key sounds equally acceptable.

Interval Just ratio Just (cents) 12-TET (cents) Error
Perfect fifth 3:2 701.96 700.00 −1.96¢
Major third 5:4 386.31 400.00 +13.69¢
Minor third 6:5 315.64 300.00 −15.64¢
Minor seventh 7:4 968.83 1000.00 +31.17¢

The minor-seventh row is where “right in tune sounds slightly off” stops being a vibe. A 12-TET dominant seventh chord is 31 cents sharp of the pure 7:4 harmonic. Aphex’s 7-limit pitches on Syro-era pads are un-reachable from a piano keyboard.

Load a .scl with those ratios and your Operator plays them. That is the entire Tuning Systems story.

Slide t04 · concept

The Pythagorean comma — why the piano lies

The Pythagorean comma — why the piano lies

Stack twelve pure 3:2 fifths starting at C. You expect to land back on C, seven octaves up. You don’t. You land 23.46 cents sharp of C. That gap is the Pythagorean comma.

The math: (3/2)^12 = 129.746. 2^7 = 128.000. The ratio 129.746 / 128.000 = 1.01364 = 23.46 cents.

12-TET’s founding move is to smear that comma equally across all twelve fifths — shrinking each pure 3:2 by 1.96 cents — so the circle of fifths closes. Every fifth is now 1.96 cents flat, and every third is 13.69 cents sharp, and the keyboard works in any key.

It is a bookkeeping compromise from 1722. The piano has never been in tune. You have just never been told.

When RDJ says he wants to “get off equal temperament,” he means he wants his synth to keep the 3:2 pure and let the comma fall somewhere audible instead of everywhere inaudible. That is what the Monologue’s user-programmable scales do, and what a Scala file in Live 12 does.

The piano stays out of the session. The math comes with you.

Slide t05 · concept

7-limit, 11-limit — where Aphex lives and 12-TET can't follow

7-limit, 11-limit — where Aphex lives and 12-TET can’t follow

Prime limit in just intonation = the largest prime number used in any ratio of the scale. 5-limit uses 2, 3, 5 — the Western Renaissance palette. 7-limit adds the blues/barbershop 7:4 and 7:6 intervals. 11-limit adds the quarter-tone-ish 11:8 tritone and 11:9 neutral third.

Why this matters for the lesson’s theme:

  • 5-limit — 12-TET approximates it with ≤14 cents of error. Listenable.
  • 7-limit — 12-TET has no good approximation of 7:4. The tempered minor seventh is +31 cents sharp. This is the interval Aphex reaches for when the Syrobonkers interview mentions his Cirklon pattern carrying “1–20 cent per-step micro-pitch offsets.” The offsets are pulling the pattern toward 7-limit targets that 12-TET can’t name.
  • 11-limit — no approximation at all in 12-TET. A 11:8 tritone sits at 551 cents, 49 cents off from the piano’s tritone. You can only get there with a loaded tuning.

“When it’s right in tune, something’s slightly off” — when the tuning is 7-limit JI, the tempered piano part next to it sounds wrong, not the JI part.

Flag: the forum-pinned paraphrase compresses this into a mystical one-liner. The actual claim is arithmetic.

Slide t06 · concept

Scala, TUN, MTS — the three file formats that carry tunings

Scala, TUN, MTS — the three file formats that carry tunings

Before the deep-dives: know the file extensions.

  • .scl (Scala) — plain-text scale definition. Lists the pitches as either cent offsets or integer ratios from the root. Does not specify what the root is; pair with a .kbm keyboard-mapping file to pin scale degree 1 to a MIDI note. Origin: Manuel Op de Coul’s Scala program, 1990s. Live 12 reads .scl natively in Tuning Systems.
  • .tun (AnaMark TUN) — binary-style 128-entry table, one cent offset per MIDI note. Absolute — each MIDI note gets its own frequency. Used by older VSTs (Zebra 2, some Synth1 builds). Live 12 does not read .tun directly; convert via Scala software.
  • .mts / MTS-ESP — MIDI Tuning Standard, runtime MTS-ESP protocol from ODDSound. Sends tuning as SysEx/MIDI messages to any compatible plugin in real time. Lets one master clock retune every synth in the session simultaneously. Live 12’s Tuning Systems is MTS-ESP-compatible per the 12.2 manual.

The practical default: free .scl from Huygens-Fokker, drag into Tuning Systems, done. The practical upgrade: MTS-ESP for live tuning modulation — you can automate the scale itself, not just the pitch played in it.

Every format is public and documented. Ignorance is a skill issue, not a gatekeeper.

Slide s06 · concept

Simpler, Transpose, Detune — the three knobs that matter

Simpler, Transpose, Detune — the three knobs that matter

Load a kick one-shot into Simpler. Set mode to Classic — pitch and time linked, the way the R-8 did it in 1989.

Three controls own the outcome.

  • Transpose (±48 semitones) — the coarse knob. A kick at C3, transposed down to E1, is your bassline seed.
  • Detune (±50 cents) — the fine knob. Pull the transposed kick in tune after your ear flags it.
  • Spread (0–100%) — leave at 0 for kicks. On snares at +7 semitones, push to ~15% for a slightly-detuned chorus.

In a Drum Rack, every pad is its own Simpler. Each pad has its own Transpose and Detune — the same affordance the R-8 exposed per pad in hardware, forty years later.

You already own it.

Slide s07 · concept

Live 12 Tuning Systems and the Scale device

Live 12 Tuning Systems and the Scale device

Live 12 shipped a feature Ableton under-sold: a global tuning system. Load a Scala (.scl) file as the active tuning. Operator, Wavetable, Meld, and Drift all play the loaded scale.

The Huygens-Fokker archive has thousands of free historical tunings.

Two different tools, don’t confuse them.

  • Tuning Systems (top-right toolbar) — the instrument-side fix. Changes what the synth plays.
  • Scale device (MIDI effect, rack-droppable) — the sequencer-side fix. Constrains incoming MIDI before the instrument sees it.

For tuned drums you need neither. Simpler transposes the sample from the MIDI note number. For melodic content later, load the track’s key as a Scala file and stop hitting wrong notes.

Second-order: the Pitch MIDI transformation (Live 12.2+) applies per-note cent offsets to committed clips.

Slide s08 · concept

Snare to the fifth — harmony from the backbeat

Snare to the fifth — harmony from the backbeat

Kick to root is the headline move. Snare to the fifth is what makes the drum bus sound composed rather than sampled.

Load a snare one-shot in a Drum Rack pad, Simpler, Classic. If the track is in E minor, the fifth is B. Transpose until the snare body lands there — usually +5 to +9 semitones, then 10–20 cents of Detune.

Listen on the 2 and 4. The backbeat stops being impact — it suggests the chord. You now have drums carrying the root (kick) and the fifth (snare). A power chord played by percussion.

Extensions, once it works:

  • Hats to the octave — high-pass at 3 kHz.
  • Tom fills to scale degrees.
  • Second kick layer at root −12 for sub.

Then stop. Over-tuning turns drums into a chime.

Slide s10 · caveat

The cost of tuned drums — what you give up

The cost of tuned drums — what you give up

Every technique has a price. Name this one before you commit.

  • Modulation gets louder. Key changes break the tuning. Fix: re-bounce per key change, or Scale-device a live transpose. Both are work.
  • Snare body thins. Pitching up 5–9 semitones via Classic mode shortens it and raises the formants. Layer an untuned snare at −6 dB underneath for chest.
  • The low end gets crowded. Kick at E1 + bass at E1 masks. Either sidechain, or drop the kick to E0.
  • Over-tuning kills groove. Every drum tuned and the ear hears melody, not rhythm. Stop at kick + snare.

Aphex’s records sound like Aphex’s records because the arrangement earns it. Tuned drums on a weak arrangement sound like tuned drums on a weak arrangement.

Caveat logged. Move on.

The five controls that matter on the Monologue panel

The five controls that matter on the Monologue panel

Ignore the LFO, the amp envelope, the drive — every mid-range analog has those. The microtuning-specific controls, in order of how you use them:

  • EDIT MODE button — hold + turn the program dial → parameter 17 is Microtuning. Here you pick from 12-TET, equal-temperament user 1–6, or “scale user” 1–6. Six slots of user microtuning per program.
  • Microtuning edit page — accessible via EDIT MODE + key group. Per-key cent offsets from −75 to +75, in 1-cent steps. This is the heart of the machine: a 25-key piano where every key carries its own cent offset.
  • PROGRAM EDIT → SLIDE TIME — separate per-patch, affects how microtuned targets glide. Short slide + microtuning = the “chopped, slightly wrong” Aphex lead.
  • Sequencer STEP mode — per-step microtuning and parameter motion. A single step can play the current scale’s 7-limit third; the next step can detune that by an additional motion-sequenced 15 cents.
  • MICROTUNING COPY — SysEx-dumpable. This is how .scl-equivalent data moves between the Monologue and a DAW; you need Korg’s Sound Librarian + the MTS-ESP conversion chain.

Everything else is wallpaper.

  1. EDIT MODE: Hold + program dial → parameter 17 is Microtuning. Selects 12-TET, ET user 1–6, scale user 1–6.
  2. PROGRAM dial: Doubles as parameter-picker in EDIT MODE. This is the gate to the microtuning edit page.
  3. key group / 25 keys: Each key carries its own ±75 cent offset. Twelve offsets per user scale.
  4. SLIDE TIME: Per-patch portamento time. Short slide + microtuning = Aphex lead texture.
  5. sequencer STEP mode: Per-step pitch + motion sequencing. One step: scale degree. Next step: +15¢ via motion.

How Aphex programs a microtuning, step by zero

How Aphex programs a microtuning, step by zero

The Korg manual documents the surface. The method is from the Pitchfork piece and the Syrobonkers interview:

  1. Pick a reference pitch — not A=440. RDJ’s SoundCloud dumps carry patches tuned to A=432 or A=444 depending on the track. The Monologue’s master tune (±50 cents) sets the global offset before the scale does anything.
  2. Load or write a scale — James uses six slots. Scales are not transposed versions of 12-TET; they are idiosyncratic per-track constructions. Typical move: build a scale where the 3rd and 7th degrees are 7-limit JI, the rest is 12-TET, then motion-sequence 1–20 cent offsets on sustained steps.
  3. “Like casting light on a rough surface.” That’s his phrase from the Cirklon interview. The microtuning is not a system — it is a texture applied to the same pattern played twice, producing different patterns of beating.
  4. The patch dictates the scale — every Monologue preset carries its scale as part of the sound. You don’t pick a scale and play patches in it. You recall a patch and the scale arrives pre-bound.

The “right in tune sounds slightly off” one-liner is shorthand for this workflow. The sound is the tuning, and the tuning belongs to the patch, and the patch belongs to the song.

Three levels of binding. One knob.

The algorithm — cent math inside the Monologue's MCU

The algorithm — cent math inside the Monologue’s MCU

Under the panel: a microcontroller that converts MIDI note numbers to pitch control voltages for two VCOs. The microtuning layer is a lookup table inserted between MIDI-in and CV-out.

For MIDI note N arriving at the synth: 1. Compute base CV = (N − 69) × (1 V / octave) / 12 — the default 12-TET mapping. 2. Look up the scale’s offset for N mod 12 — a signed integer in ±75 cents. 3. Scale-adjust the CV: cv += offset_cents × (1 V / 1200). 4. If a motion sequence is active on this step, add its cent offset to the running total.

All math is done in the microcontroller before the CV reaches the DAC feeding the VCOs. Korg’s spec sheet lists 16-bit pitch resolution internally; the microtuning edit page exposes 1-cent user offsets — well below perceptual threshold. The Monologue’s limitation is not precision but scope — six user scales per program, 12 cent offsets per scale. 7-limit JI fits comfortably. 19-EDO does not (needs 19 steps per octave; the Monologue only offers 12).

The same lookup-table pattern lives inside Ableton Live 12’s Tuning Systems engine, just in 32-bit float and polyphonic.

The hardware is not magical. The file format is the technology.

graph LR A[MIDI note N] --> B[base CV<br/>12-TET lookup] B --> C[add scale offset<br/>±75¢] C --> D[add motion-seq offset<br/>±75¢] D --> E[16-bit pitch resolution<br/>1¢ user-facing offsets] E --> F[VCO 1 + VCO 2]

The Ableton bridge — Tuning Systems + Operator + Scale device

The Ableton bridge — Tuning Systems + Operator + Scale device

The Monologue feature set ports to Live 12 in three devices working together.

  • Tuning Systems (top-right toolbar) — load a .scl file. The six RDJ scales from the Monologue firmware are not publicly shipped as .scl files, but the Korg Sound Librarian exports them as SysEx; the Surge Synth Team’s Scale Workbench converts. For the exercise: the Huygens-Fokker 7-limit-just.scl is the closest publicly-available match to James’ documented preferences.
  • Operator (4-op FM) — the Ableton stand-in for the Monologue’s VCO pair. Tuning Systems applies globally; Operator plays the loaded scale on every note.
  • Scale device (MIDI effect) — MIDI-side filter; rides in front of Operator. Use it to constrain incoming notes to scale degrees when the Tuning Systems scale is non-12-EDO.

What else the Monologue-pattern does — creative uses beyond leads

What else the Monologue-pattern does — creative uses beyond leads

Microtuning is not only for leads. Four moves past the stereotype:

  • Tuned drum rack — load the same .scl you used for the lead synth into Tuning Systems. Map kick one-shots across a Drum Rack at different MIDI notes. Because Simpler respects Tuning Systems’ semitone quantization on melodic mode (not Classic), dropping into Melodic mode + loading a JI scale + placing kicks at scale-degree notes gives you scale-degree-tuned kicks for free.
  • Drone beds in 17-EDO — a sustained Operator note under a 12-TET drum kit. The 17-EDO neutral third clashes with the kit’s tempered harmonics and creates audible beating — a drone that pulses without a LFO.
  • Microtuning as modulation — automate Tuning Systems’ load-slot (Live 12.2+) or use MTS-ESP to swap scales mid-phrase. Same melody, three different tunings, back-to-back. The listener hears modulation that has no conventional name.
  • The Novation Bass Station II “AFX Mode” sibling — same per-key parameter lock philosophy; James advised Novation too.

Stop treating microtuning as a one-shot effect. It is a scale you compose against.

Exercise B — build your own microtuning, then write against it

Exercise B — build your own microtuning, then write against it

Yamaha Disklavier — a MIDI-driven acoustic piano

Yamaha’s Disklavier line, launched 1987, is an acoustic grand (or upright) with solenoids under every key and pedal. Send it MIDI; the solenoids play the keys; the strings speak. The piano is a real piano with real hammers and real strings — the difference is the motor.

The Drukqs-era Aphex signature: send a MIDI sequence that no human has fingers for, then mic the piano body, not the soundboard. Every key-strike ends with a solenoid click. Those clicks become the record’s secondary rhythm track. “Avril 14th,” “Nanou 2” — listen past the melody; the clicks are playing a pattern of their own.

From RDJ’s SoundCloud metadata: “played & programmed customised Yamaha Disklavier Pro, Recorded To Nagra IVS 5.” The Nagra tape is the color. The Disklavier is the rhythm section.

This deep-dive: how the solenoid behaves, what “prepared Disklavier” means after John Cage, and how to fake the click-rhythm layer without owning a Disklavier.

What's under the lid — the annotated mechanism

What’s under the lid — the annotated mechanism

Three systems stacked into one piano:

  • MIDI input jack — DIN-5 on early models, USB on Mark IV and later. Takes any standard MIDI stream: 16 channels, note-on/off, velocity, CC1, CC64 (sustain pedal), CC66 (sostenuto), CC67 (soft pedal). The piano accepts SMF and XG-extended files. Velocity range: 1–127, mapped to solenoid drive current.
  • Solenoid bank under the keys — 88 solenoids, one per key. Each solenoid is an electromagnet that pulls a plunger upward, which presses the key from below. The key’s downward travel pushes the hammer action — the physical linkage is unchanged from a normal grand. Key-strike latency is ~30ms on Mark IV, <10ms on Enspire Pro. The click is the plunger seating.
  • Pedal solenoids — three, mounted behind the pedal lyre. Sustain, sostenuto, soft. These click the loudest. When Aphex mics inside the body, the pedal solenoids become a percussion voice distinct from the string hits.

What’s not instrumented: microtuning. The Disklavier is 12-TET to the bone; the keyboard layout is fixed, one hammer per string, no per-key detune. You layer microtuning on top in the DAW after recording.

The Drukqs technique is not about pitch. It is about the mechanical noise the piano makes when played by a machine.

  1. solenoid bank: 88 solenoids, one per key. Plunger seats with an audible metallic click at end of travel.
  2. pedal solenoids: Three — sustain, sostenuto, soft. Loudest mechanical noise in the instrument.
  3. MIDI DIN / USB: Accepts standard SMF + XG-extended. Velocity 1–127 maps to solenoid drive current.
  4. body cavity mic position: Aphex placed condensers under the lid, not above the soundboard. Captures click + body woof.

The Drukqs technique — solenoid click as rhythmic layer

The Drukqs technique — solenoid click as rhythmic layer

How Aphex made “Avril 14th” sound like a music box that knows about percussion:

  1. Write the sequence in a tracker — Drukqs was “mostly written in PlayerPro for the mac, my fave tracker” per James’ July 2017 Bleep Q&A. Step-time; no real-time performance. This is why the note density is physically impossible.
  2. Send the tracker MIDI to the Disklavier — via a dedicated MIDI-to-SMF converter. The Disklavier plays the notes at the velocities written.
  3. Mic INSIDE the piano body — not above the soundboard. Two condensers under the lid, aimed at the action/hammers, not the strings. This is the key decision. Regular piano recording places mics 20–40cm above the strings. Aphex placed them at the mechanism.
  4. Record to Nagra IVS 5 at 15 IPS — two-track, reel-to-reel. The tape saturation folds solenoid clicks into the noise floor just enough to integrate them with the piano tone.
  5. Prepare the strings, John Cage style — screws, bolts, felt strips between strings on some Drukqs tracks. Changes timbre without affecting pitch tracking.

The rhythmic layer that results: solenoid click on every note-on, louder pedal click on every sustain-engage. Against the piano melody, that click pattern has its own rhythm. Same notes, two voices, one instrument.

Community note, worth flagging: the “Aphex built MIDI solenoid drum machines himself” claim comes from forum lore not confirmed in a primary interview. The Disklavier technique is confirmed by his own SoundCloud notes.

The algorithm — velocity-to-solenoid and why the click is velocity-dependent

The algorithm — velocity-to-solenoid and why the click is velocity-dependent

The Disklavier’s velocity curve is the model.

MIDI velocity V arriving at the piano: 1. Lookup: solenoid drive current I = f(V) where f is a calibrated curve — nonlinear, approximating a trained pianist’s force-to-velocity map. Typical: V=1 produces ~0.3A pull, V=127 produces ~1.1A pull. 2. Solenoid pulls for a duration T = g(V) — shorter at high velocity (faster strike), longer at low velocity (slower, more controlled). 3. The plunger accelerates toward the key. Mechanical impact: two audible events — the key-bottoming (soft thump) and the solenoid seating at end-of-travel (metallic click). Higher velocity = louder click and louder string. 4. The hammer, driven by the key, strikes the string. The string vibrates as a Helmholtz resonator — fundamental f = (1/2L)√(T/μ), where L is string length, T is tension, μ is linear density. 5. The soundboard radiates the string vibration. The body cavity resonates at ~60–80 Hz regardless of which note is played — a body resonance that adds a low-end “woof” to every note.

The click and the string are independent signals that share a cause. Velocity controls both, but mic placement controls their relative level. Under-lid miking flips the ratio. Body-cavity miking captures the ~70 Hz thud separately from either.

Three layers from one MIDI note. That is the mechanical receipt for why Drukqs sounds three-dimensional.

The Ableton bridge — Collision, piano VST, parallel click bus

The Ableton bridge — Collision, piano VST, parallel click bus

Simulate the Disklavier in Live 12 without owning one.

Signal flow:

MIDI clip ─┬─> Piano VST (NI Noire or Ableton Grand Piano)  ─┐
           │                                                  ├──> Master
           └─> Collision (tuned for solenoid click)  ─> HPF ──┘
                    + Drum Rack of piano-mechanism samples

Device breakdown:

  • Collision — Live’s built-in physical-modelling instrument. Set Mallet layer ON, Mallet Stiffness 95%, Mallet Noise 40%. Resonator type: Plate, Decay 200ms, Material 80%. This is the solenoid click — short, metallic, velocity-scaled.
  • Piano VST — NI Noire or any felted-piano patch carries the tone; Collision carries the mechanism.
  • Parallel click bus — route Collision to its own audio return. HPF at 600 Hz so it reads as click, not pitched note. Sidechain the return’s compressor off the kick if needed.
  • Velocity mapping — duplicate the MIDI clip onto both tracks so Collision and the piano always fire together. Use Velocity Shaper (Max for Live) to send low velocities to Collision-only — the simulated “soft-pedal click without full hammer strike.”

What this will not capture: the body-cavity ~70 Hz woof. Add a Drum Rack with low-velocity kick samples pitched to ~70 Hz on a parallel send to fake it. Not identical. Close enough.

Budget-$0 Disklavier. The tradeoff: no physical strings, no Helmholtz, no Nagra tape saturation. All three matter; none are showstoppers for a demo.

Three-way parallel bus recreating the Drukqs under-lid miking: string tone, solenoid click, body cavity woof.

What else Collision-as-Disklavier does — past the piano stereotype

What else Collision-as-Disklavier does — past the piano stereotype

Stop using Collision for piano-like tones. It is a better percussion engine than most drum synths. Four moves:

  • Collision as tuned wood-block kit — Mallet layer only, Resonator type: Beam, Material 100%, Decay 500ms. Play chromatically; it sounds like a Balinese gamelan sampled through a physical model. Pair with a trap-style MIDI pattern; the contrast is the point.
  • Collision as detuned marimba — Resonator type: Membrane, Ratio set to 7:4 or another 7-limit JI interval. Now the model resonates at a non-tempered pitch. Layer with a Tuning-Systems-loaded Operator for coherent JI drums.
  • Collision as percussion-only shell — mute the Mallet layer entirely, use external MIDI CC to modulate the impact. The resonator “rings” without being struck. This is the sound Autechre often chases in Max/MSP.
  • Collision as solenoid-click printer — strip the resonator completely (Decay 20ms, Ratio 0.01). What’s left is the mallet attack. Layer that over any drum bus and you get the Drukqs “mechanical noise bed” — cheap, per-note-triggered.

The Disklavier technique generalizes: the attack mechanism and the resonator are separable, and separating them is where the interesting percussion lives. Aphex figured this out with microphone placement. You figure it out with Collision’s two-layer architecture.

Exercise A — layer Collision clicks under a piano VST

Exercise A — layer Collision clicks under a piano VST

Replicate Drukqs-style solenoid-click-as-rhythm without a Disklavier.

Setup (10 min): 1. New Live 12 set. Track 1: MIDI → NI Noire or Ableton’s built-in Grand Piano. Track 2: MIDI → Collision. 2. Collision settings: Mallet ON, Stiffness 95%, Noise 40%. Resonator: Plate, Decay 200ms, Material 80%. Mallet layer level: +0dB. Resonator level: −12dB. 3. Route Track 2 to a new return (Return B). Return B insert: HPF at 600 Hz, Glue Compressor 4:1, makeup +3dB.

The move (15 min): 4. Import a MIDI piano file — any Chopin prelude, any Satie piece, or write a 16-bar melody in Piano Roll. Drop it on both tracks simultaneously. 5. Play. You now hear piano tone from Track 1 and solenoid clicks from Return B, synchronous. 6. Modulate Collision’s Mallet velocity response via a Velocity Shaper M4L device — send MIDI velocities <60 to Collision only (fake soft-pedal click without hammer). 7. Add a subtle Drum Rack on Track 2 with a ~70 Hz kick sample triggered at low velocity — the body cavity “woof.”

Deliverable. 20-second WAV. One sentence per layer: what did removing Collision sound like? What did removing the piano sound like?

Exercise B — Collision as a drum synth, not a piano surrogate

Exercise B — Collision as a drum synth, not a piano surrogate

Past replication. Use Collision as the percussion source for a full drum pattern. No piano. No Disklavier reference. Just the physical model doing what physical models do.

Setup (15 min): 1. New MIDI track with a Drum Rack. Drop one Collision instance onto pad C1, another on D1, a third on F#1 (kick, snare, hat). 2. For each Collision: different Resonator type. C1 = Membrane (kick-ish), D1 = Plate (snare-ish), F#1 = String (hat-ish). Tune the Ratio parameter per-pad to taste. 3. On each pad, set Mallet Stiffness 70%, Noise 50%, Decay 40ms. You want short, wood-tone attacks — not pretty piano hits.

The move (30 min): 4. Program a four-bar break pattern — any grid, any tempo. The Drum Rack fires Collision on each pad. 5. Tune each pad to a scale degree via Ratio parameter or via the Pitch MIDI transformation. Match the exercise: kick to root, snare to fifth. 6. Listen to the timbre. Collision’s attack is harder than a 808 sample and softer than a drum synth. It has a characteristic “wooden” tone that is unmistakably physical-modeled. 7. Bounce 30 seconds.

Deliverable. 30-second WAV + one paragraph on the timbral difference between Collision drums and your normal sample-based drum rack. What did the physical model give you that a sample didn’t? What did it fail to give you that a sample did?

Aphex made drums from a piano. You can make drums from a beam, a plate, and a membrane. The physical modeling is the point.

For further listening — the Fredonia pipeline

For further listening — if the microtuning bug has bitten, there’s a program at SUNY Fredonia called Sound Recording Technology that takes forty students every two years and turns out working engineers. They run a mastering lab where precision is treated as craft, not afterthought. One of the professors, Bernd Gottinger, has a pile of GRAMMY nominations for classical and orchestral work. Another alumnus, Matt Estep, is Fridmann’s full-time assistant at Tarbox Road. The program does not advertise the pipeline. But it exists.
