IDM / W10 Week 10 — Variation generation, the five mutations

Slide s01 · title

The parable before the method

The parable before the method

Parable — in the summer of 1997, The Flaming Lips and Dave Fridmann decided to make an album that played on four CDs simultaneously. This was not a gimmick. It was an arrangement constraint: each CD had to carry a partial version of the song that only made sense in combination with the other three. Halfway through, they realized you cannot split a normal song across four CDs and threw out months of work. The record that shipped, Zaireeka, was the first project ever tracked at Tarbox Road.

The lesson for our five-mutations framework this week: the constraint is the generator. If you are stuck, it is because your constraint is not strong enough.

That is the whole week in one sentence. The rest is receipts and knob positions.

Slide s02 · concept

The thesis — constraint is the generator

The thesis — constraint is the generator

Week 2 was densest-first, mute-backward. This week is the inverse. One loop, five duplicates, five prescribed mutations. You do not pick the mutations. You apply them and ship.

Lineage: Finish More Music calls it “spawn from the same seed.” Spec A §5.4 calls it Variation Generation. Eno’s Oblique Strategies — “Honor thy error as a hidden intention” — is the same idea with different packaging.

Mechanically:

  1. Duplicate your 16-bar loop five times along the Arrangement timeline.
  2. Apply one mutation per duplicate — drum swap, bass seed, filter sweep, reverse, new layer.
  3. Reorder for arc. Often 3–1–2–5–4 reads better than 1–2–3–4–5.
  4. Bounce. Move on.

Five sections share DNA because they started identical. Coherence is baked in at the duplicate step.

Slide s03 · concept

The locator template, before you touch a sound

The locator template, before you touch a sound

Spec A §5.2 is explicit: place locators before the first audio plays. Cmd-drop on the Arrangement timeline:

  • 0:00 — Intro (texture, filtered drums)
  • 0:45 — A section (core beat + one melodic element)
  • 2:00 — B section (second layer)
  • 3:15 — Reduction (strip to texture)
  • 3:45 — Peak (all elements + added chaos)
  • 5:00 — Outro (deconstruction, end ~5:45)

The locators are suggestions, not contracts. Placing them converts the session from open-ended into finite.

Hebden, Tape Notes 140: “Arrangement is just everything.”

Your five mutated loops map 1:1 to the sections between Intro and Outro. Intro and Outro are the same loop filtered down to texture. Five mutations, two bookends. Done.

Slide s04 · concept

Mutation 1 — drum swap

Mutation 1 — drum swap

Rule: replace the drum pattern wholesale. Keep everything else identical.

Two clean paths in Live 12:

Path A — MIDI Recombine. Select the drum MIDI clip. Right-click → Transform → Recombine → Shuffle. Live rotates and reshuffles the hits. Same kit, same velocities, new order.

Path B — Drum Rack hot-swap. Keep the MIDI, swap the entire Drum Rack. Same notes, unfamiliar voices. Spec A §5.4 permits either; Path B generates more contrast per minute.

Limitation. Drum-swap variations can feel like the drummer quit mid-song if you do not bridge them. Insert a one-bar crash or reversed cymbal on the bar before the swap. The edit point is where this mutation lives or dies.

Do not tune the new kit to the track. That was Week 5. This week you are moving fast.

Slide s05 · concept

Mutation 2 — bass seed

Mutation 2 — bass seed

Rule: rerun the bassline with a new Seed generator. Drums and everything above stay fixed.

Live 12’s Seed generator produces an acid-style bassline within a ±7 semitone range, constrained to key.

Steps:

  1. Duplicate the bass MIDI track. Mute the original.
  2. Drop a Seed generator on the duplicate. Pitch ±7. Density 50–70%.
  3. Hit Roll. Audition. Budget ten rolls.
  4. When you land one, Capture MIDI (Cmd+Shift+C) to freeze the seed output as editable notes.

Caveat. Seed is a dice-roller inside key constraints, not a composer. Half the rolls will be forgettable. Ten rolls, one pick, move on.

Aphex’s early rig used the R-8 pitched kick as the bass itself. You already know that move from Week 5. Seed is the quick substitute when the pitched kick needs a counter-line above.

Slide s06 · concept

Mutation 3 — filter sweep across the whole section

Mutation 3 — filter sweep across the whole section

Rule: one Auto Filter, macro-controlled, across the 16-bar duplicate. Nothing else changes.

Setup:

  1. Group the whole loop into a Group Track.
  2. Insert Auto Filter on the group. Low-pass 24 dB, Resonance 15%.
  3. Map Frequency to Macro 1.
  4. Draw a clip envelope on Macro 1 ramping fully closed at bar 1 to fully open at bar 16.

Unlinked-envelope trick. Set envelope loop to 7 bars while audio loops at 4. Sweep never lands in the same place twice — Autechre polymeter, one envelope. Spec A §5.7.

Limitation. A filter sweep alone is 2012 festival music. It earns its keep because the section is otherwise identical. You are selling the return to full bandwidth on the next downbeat.

Automate one parameter. Not two.

Slide s07 · concept

Mutation 4 — reverse one element

Mutation 4 — reverse one element

Rule: reverse one element. Not the whole loop. One.

Candidates, ranked by impact-per-minute:

  1. Reversed cymbal swell into the downbeat. Consolidate (Cmd+J), right-click → Reverse, slide to land on bar 1.
  2. Reversed vocal chop. Week 9 vocal, 1-bar chunk, reversed, on beat 3 of bar 15.
  3. Reversed snare tail. 2 bars consolidated, reversed, layered under the forward snare.
  4. Reversed pad. Whole-section pad, reversed, crossfaded against the forward pad at the midpoint.

Receipt. DJ Dahi on “Money Trees” reversed a Beach House sample for the hook. The whole track hangs on the backwards two-bar loop. Human ears mark backwards audio as coming from somewhere else, which is what a variation needs to telegraph.

Do not reverse the drum loop. That is next week’s lesson, and it is its own category of pain.

Slide s08 · concept

Mutation 5 — new layer that was not in the original

Mutation 5 — new layer that was not in the original

Rule: introduce one sound absent from the original loop. One.

Spec A §5.4 says one layer and means it. Every new layer costs a mix decision. One = variation; three = a song you did not write.

Candidates, effort-to-payoff:

  • Held sub-bass on the root. 16 bars, no movement. Weight without activity.
  • Two-note ostinato on a new patch — Meld resonator, or Simpler with a field recording.
  • Reversed texture pad. Warp Texture, Flux 80%.
  • Single vocal syllable from Week 9, pitched +7, on beat 1 and beat 9 only.

Caveat. The layer must have been absent from sections 1–4. If it reappears, it is a re-entry — those belong in the Peak.

Rule. Bounce inside the 45-minute time-box. Past 46 = rabbit hole.

Slide s10 · concept

Follow Actions 2.0 — the non-repeating variation pool

Follow Actions 2.0 — the non-repeating variation pool

Live 12 shipped Follow Actions 2.0: per-scene probability, legato, and an Any option that mixes a deck of clips non-repeatingly.

Recipe, Spec A §5.7:

  1. Build 8–16 one-bar MIDI variants in one Session track. Small hand-edits — hat drops, ghost snares.
  2. Each clip: Follow Action Any, Chance 20% Play Again / 60% Other / 20% Any. Trigger 1 bar.
  3. Hit play. Live deals variants.
  4. In Arrangement, arm and use Capture MIDI.

Committed, non-repeating drum take. The pool is the instrument.

Stacked with unlinked envelopes. 4-bar audio, 7-bar envelope on filter cutoff. Autechre polymeter, one checkbox.

Commit. Bounce Groups (12.3) prints the generative chain as a fixed stem.

Slide s11 · concept

The Resample-and-Mutate engine — Mr. Bill's forcing function

The Resample-and-Mutate engine — Mr. Bill’s forcing function

Spec A §5.6. Mr. Bill describes this as a core loop, not a technique.

Every 8–16 bars:

  1. Route drum bus (or full mix) to a new audio track — Audio From → Resampling, or the bus.
  2. Record 4–8 bars.
  3. Delete the source MIDI.
  4. Chop into slices. Drum Rack Slice-to-Transients, or Simpler Slicing.
  5. Pitch-shift, reverse, bit-crush a few. B and C section material.

Why delete. Frozen audio is psychologically expensive to un-freeze. That expense is the feature.

Sample Offset envelope. Draw ±8 sixteenth-notes on the printed loop. New patterns from the same audio — MPC-style beat repeat.

Consolidate every 8–16 bars. Cmd+J. The bounce is the arrangement move.

Slide s12 · caveat

Versioning discipline — the gap both specs missed

Versioning discipline — the gap both specs missed

Neither Spec A nor Spec B names this. Filling the gap.

Rule. Before every bounce, save a timestamped copy of the Live set:

~/IDM_COURSE_versions/YYYY-MM-DD_wNN_track_N.als

Example: ~/IDM_COURSE_versions/2026-04-20_w10_track_01.als. Re-edit, re-save as _02, _03. Never overwrite. Disk is cheap.

Why here specifically. The five-mutations method and the Resample-and-Mutate engine both delete the source. That is the point — commitment forcing. But if you delete the source and later want Mutation 3 back with inverted sweep, you are stuck. The versioned .als is your one-way-back.

Autechre do this forever. Booth in Sound on Sound April 2004 describes persistent Max patches across albums. The NTS Sessions reportedly used patches from 2011 — forum consensus, not confirmed. The archive is the instrument.

Pattern, one line. Save timestamped .als. Consolidate. Delete. Bounce. Move on.

Tarbox Road Studios — Fridmann's constraint machine

Dave Fridmann at Tarbox Road

Tarbox Road Studios, Cassadaga NY. A converted dairy in the woods. Fridmann built it for Mercury Rev and the Lips, then it became the headquarters for records that refused to be made in LA.

The transferable move — the constraint decides the arrangement before the arranger does. Slide d01 · concept

Follow Actions — the state-machine diagram

Follow Actions — the state machine, drawn

Each clip is a node. Each Action is a weighted edge. The trigger fires every N bars. Two edges out; Live rolls Chance A vs Chance B and takes one.

Above — a simplified view of a three-clip pool with the Pattern 1 recipe (20% self, 80% other). Pool size N=3 gives roughly equal draw among the other clips.

Reading it:

  • The self-loop on each node is Play Again at Chance 20.
  • The cross-edges are Other at Chance 80 / (N−1) = ~40 each.
  • Any (not drawn) would add a cross-edge back to the node itself.

Why this matters for variation generation — the five mutations produce five distinct 16-bar variants. Drop them into one Session column. Set each to Action A Play Again 10 / Action B Other 90. Now you have a variation pool inside the arrangement. Capture MIDI on the row-output gives you a committed Arrangement that never repeats.

The state machine is how Autechre-style non-repetition lives in a DAW without a single patch cord.

Three-clip Follow Actions pool with Pattern 1 weights (20% self, 80% other). Every trigger interval Live rolls the edge weights and dispatches.

Slide d02 · concept

Resample-and-Mutate — the pipeline

Resample-and-Mutate — the pipeline, drawn

The Mr. Bill engine, rendered as a data flow. One-way arrows only; you cannot go back.

Reading the diagram:

  • Source tracks on the left. MIDI drum + bass + pad.
  • Resample bus captures their sum.
  • Print — the bus is bounced to a new audio track. At this moment the MIDI is deletable.
  • Chop splits the printed audio to Simpler Slicing.
  • Mutate — pitch, reverse, bit-crush, Sample Offset envelope.
  • B/C section material — the chopped mutants are dropped back into the Arrangement as variation source.

The dashed red line is the commitment boundary. Left of the line, MIDI is editable and the rabbit hole is open. Right of the line, audio is printed and the only moves are mutate or delete.

In Live 12.3, the whole pipeline collapses to Bounce Groups + Paste Bounced Audio. Before 12.3 you ran it manually with resample tracks.

Slide d03 · concept

The variation tree — one source × 5 mutations

The variation tree — one source, five variants

One 16-bar loop. Five prescribed mutations. Five sibling variants that share DNA because they branched from the same root.

Reading the tree:

  • Root — your source loop, unchanged.
  • Five branches — each branch applies exactly one named mutation. No combining.
  • Leaves — the five usable arrangement sections: drum-swap variant, bass-seed variant, filter-sweep variant, reverse variant, new-layer variant.

Coherence is inherited. Because every variant started identical, the sections sound related even when the mutation is dramatic — the reversed cymbal swell in variant 4 and the held sub in variant 5 both sit over the same core arrangement underneath.

This is the generative property. You do not need to design the sections to relate; you design one loop, and the branching does the relating. Spec A §5.4, Finish More Music.

Arrangement tip, again — often 3–1–2–5–4 reads better than 1–2–3–4–5. Filter sweep up top sets the entry, drum swap at the A, bass seed at the B, new layer at peak, reverse at outro.

graph TD R[Source loop · 16 bars] --> V1[Mutation 1 · drum swap] R --> V2[Mutation 2 · bass seed] R --> V3[Mutation 3 · filter sweep] R --> V4[Mutation 4 · reverse one element] R --> V5[Mutation 5 · new layer] V1 --> S1[Section A] V2 --> S2[Section B] V3 --> S3[Intro / Reduction] V4 --> S4[Outro bridge] V5 --> S5[Peak]
Five branches from one root. Leaves map to arrangement sections. Coherence is inherited from the shared root, not designed.

Slide fa-01 · concept

Follow Actions 2.0 — state-machine architecture

Follow Actions 2.0 — state-machine architecture

Follow Actions are not random. They are a two-action-per-clip state machine with weighted edges. Live 12 shipped the 2.0 revision with per-scene Follow Actions, probabilityFollow Actions 2.0 — Ableton's per-clip logic for triggering the next clip in a track. 2.0 (Live 11+) added per-scene follow actions, probability distributions, and legato launching. Set 8–16 drum variants to Any with mixed probabilities — non-repeating drum tracks built from hand-crafted parts. wikipedia↗, and legato support.

Every clip carries:

  • Action A + Chance A
  • Action B + Chance B
  • a trigger interval (bars/beats/sixteenths)

At each interval Live rolls Chance A vs Chance B, then dispatches to the chosen Action — Stop, Play Again, Previous, Next, First, Last, Any, Other, Jump-to-.

The pool is the whole Session track column. The state machine selects within it.

Key terms — Any draws uniformly from the column including the current clip. Other excludes the current clip, which is how you get non-repeating variation with zero code.

Slide fa-02 · concept

Probability weights — Any vs specific jumps

Probability weights — Any vs specific jumps

Three patterns cover 90% of generative use.

Pattern 1 — Non-repeating pool. - Action A: Play Again · Chance 20 - Action B: Other · Chance 80

Result — mostly variation, occasional self-repeat so the ear does not catch the rotation. Spec A §5.7 baseline.

Pattern 2 — Card shuffle. - Action A: Any · Chance 100

Pure uniform draw. Any includes the current clip, so ~1-in-N chance of self-repeat in a pool of N.

Pattern 3 — Directed fills. - Main clip: Action A Next · Chance 10 · Action B Play Again · Chance 90 - Fill clip: Action A First · Chance 100

Main plays mostly itself with occasional advance to the next (a fill), which snaps back to First. That is a classic 4-bar-main-plus-every-Nth-fill pattern without writing the sequence. Spec B receipts.

Slide fa-03 · concept

Legato vs restart — the phase-preservation switch

Legato vs restart — the phase-preservation switch

Each clip has a Legato toggle. It changes what happens at the moment of transition.

Legato off — the next clip starts from its own bar 1. Phase resets. Good for drum variants where you want the new pattern to begin cleanly.

Legato on — the next clip starts at the current playhead position within the clip’s length. Phase rides through. This is the setting for pads, long melodic phrases, and any loop where you want the transition to feel like a mode-switch rather than a cut.

Legato plus Launch Mode: Gate gives you the Autechre move — hold the row change with a foot switch, release, clip restarts. Spec B 12.3 §4.

Anti-pattern — legato on across drum variants with different rhythmic anchors. The downbeats slide and you get a phaser-on-kick problem. Legato off for drums, legato on for pads. Default answer.

Slide fa-04 · concept

Combining Follow Actions with Clip Envelopes

Combining Follow Actions with Clip Envelopes

Follow Actions gives you which clip plays next. Clip Envelopes give you what the clip sounds like while it plays. Stacked, you get generative arrangement from a small source.

Clip Envelopes unlinked from clip loop — set the envelope’s loop length to a value different from the audio/MIDI loop. Live 12 manual §13.

Example — 4-bar audio clip. Filter-cutoff envelope set to 7-bar loop. The filter sweep never lands in the same place across four plays of the clip. Now stack this under Follow Actions: on each bar-1 trigger, FA may advance to a different 4-bar clip, at which point the 7-bar envelope continues from wherever it was.

That is polymeterpolymeter — Two or more parts cycling at different bar lengths over the same pulse — e.g., a 5/16 hi-hat pattern against a 4/4 kick. They drift out of alignment and reconverge after the least common multiple of their lengths. Autechre's bread and butter. wikipedia↗ on two axes — clip rotation on axis one, envelope phase on axis two. Autechre spent albums on this. Live does it with two checkboxes.

Sample Offset envelope on the printed loop is the same trick for drum playback — see ram-04.

Slide fa-05 · concept

The Follow Actions panel — annotated

The Follow Actions panel — annotated

Five fields matter. Ignore the rest on first pass.

  1. Trigger — how often FA fires. Bars, Beats, or Sixteenths. 1 Bar for drums and sections. 1/16 for stutter-fills.
  2. Action A / Action B — the two edges out of this clip’s state.
  3. Chance A / Chance B — the weights. Live normalizes, so 20/80 and 1/4 are equivalent.
  4. Legato — phase preserve vs reset, per fa-03.
  5. Linked (scene-level) — when on, all clips in the scene transition together. Off, each clip’s FA fires independently.

The scene-level FA is the per-scene sibling added in 12. Set it once at the scene header, and every clip in the scene inherits unless the clip overrides. That is where you control section advance (scene 1 → scene 2) separately from within-section variation (clip rotation inside scene 1). Spec B §4.

Two-axis control — scene FA drives structure, clip FA drives texture.

Slide ram-01 · concept

Resample-and-Mutate — the core loop, step-by-step

Resample-and-Mutate — the core loop, step-by-step

Mr. Bill’s engine, written as a recipe.

The loop (every 8–16 bars):

  1. Route. Create a new audio track. Set Audio From → Resampling (or pick the drum bus directly).
  2. Arm and record 4–8 bars while the source plays.
  3. Mute or delete the source. Mute first if nervous. Delete after one play-through confirms the print.
  4. Consolidate the printed clip (Cmd+J) so it becomes a single warped audio file.
  5. Chop. Right-click → Slice to New MIDI Track → Simpler (Slicing mode, transient markers).
  6. Mutate. Pitch ±12st on two slices, reverse one, bit-crush one, leave the rest.
  7. Drop into B and C sections.

That is the whole engine. Repeat every 8–16 bars of finished material. The mutated output is the B and C sections you would otherwise have to compose.

The commitment is what makes this a workflow and not a toy. The printed audio survives undo stacks. The deleted MIDI does not.

Slide ram-02 · concept

Bounce Groups (Live 12.3) — the engine, natively

Bounce Groups (Live 12.3) — the engine, natively

Before 12.3, Resample-and-Mutate meant routing tracks, arming Resampling, and watching a meter. Live 12.3 shipped Bounce Groups and Paste Bounced Audio — the loop as a right-click menu.

The 12.3 path:

  1. Select a group track in Arrangement.
  2. Right-click → Bounce Group. Dialog opens.
  3. Pick range, sample rate, bit depth, whether to include returns.
  4. Delete source toggle — check it. This is the Mr. Bill move, native.
  5. Click Bounce. Live renders, inserts the printed audio, optionally clears the source MIDI.

Spec B receipts — “Bounce to New Track (12.2), Bounce Groups and Paste Bounced Audio (12.3) enable the bounce-and-arrange workflow as a native loop.”

Caveat — Bounce Groups does not capture the state of Follow Actions-driven Session clips. For FA output you still need Capture MIDI → Arrangement first, then Bounce Groups on the Arrangement tracks.

Slide ram-03 · caveat

Why delete — the anti-perfectionism machine

Why delete — the anti-perfectionism machine

“Bounce and delete” sounds like a loss of power. It is a loss of optionality, which is a different thing.

Undo is a procrastination engine. If your kick is one Cmd+Z away, you will spend thirty minutes flipping between versions. Print the kick bus, delete the source, and the decision is made. The only remaining move is forward.

Rick Rubin, The Creative Act — “The goal is to be done, not perfect.” The bounce is how you operationalize that sentence.

Eno, Oblique Strategies — “Discard an axiom.” Same thing, phrased as a card.

The observable failure mode this prevents — spending ninety minutes tweaking a hi-hat inside a loop that has been finished for the first eight of those minutes. The loop is fine. You were in a rabbit hole.

One caveat, important. Versioning discipline (s12) is the safety net. Bounce and delete within the Live set. Save the set timestamped outside the Live set. The two disciplines hold each other up.

Slide ram-04 · concept

Sample Offset envelope — new patterns from one audio file

Sample Offset envelope — new patterns from one audio file

Once the drum bus is printed as audio, you have a new parameter you did not have as MIDI — Sample Offset. Clip Envelope → Clip → Sample Offset. Grid set to 16ths.

Draw ±8 sixteenth-notes of offset over the 2-bar loop. At playback, Live jumps the playhead around inside the audio file at each 16th boundary. Same audio, new pattern.

Four shapes to try:

  1. Stutter every 4th 16th. Offset drops to −1/16, then snaps back. MPC beat-repeat.
  2. Slow climb. Linear ramp +0 to +4 over 2 bars. The loop slides forward inside itself.
  3. Random staircase. Steps at random values ±4. A whole new rhythm from the same file.
  4. Unlinked loop length. Set the envelope’s loop to 3 bars, audio loops 2 bars. Pattern never repeats inside eight bars.

Stack this under Follow Actions — Sample Offset envelope on each clip in the pool, different shape per clip, FA rotating them. Three levels of indirection from one printed loop.

Mutation 6, if we allowed a sixth. We do not. Save it for your own records.

Drum swap — the Ableton walk-through

Drum swap — the Ableton walk-through

Two named paths from s04, now as click-by-click.

Path A — MIDI Recombine → Shuffle.

  1. Select the duplicate’s drum MIDI clip.
  2. Right-click the clip → Transform → Recombine.
  3. In the dialog, enable Shuffle. Leave Pitch and Velocity off.
  4. Click Transform. Live rewrites the clip in place.
  5. Audition. If the result is too far, Cmd+Z once, re-open Recombine, reduce Shuffle amount.

Path B — Drum Rack hot-swap.

  1. Select the Drum Rack on the duplicate’s drum track.
  2. Click the Hot-Swap button (headphone icon, top-right of the Rack header).
  3. Browser filters to Drum Racks. Arrow-key through options while the clip plays.
  4. Enter to commit. Cmd+Z to revert.

Do not tune the new kit. Week 5 move, not this week. Ship.

Limitation. Both paths break flow at the edit point unless bridged. Insert a 1-bar reversed cymbal or a half-beat silence on the bar before the duplicate. That sells the transition. Mutation 4 candidates also work for bridges.

Bass seed — the Ableton walk-through

Bass seed — the Ableton walk-through

Seed is a MIDI Generator, not a Transform. It draws notes from scratch into the clip’s grid under rule constraints.

Click-by-click:

  1. Duplicate the bass MIDI track. Mute the original. Rename the duplicate bass seed.
  2. Draw an empty 2-bar MIDI clip. Set Scale on the clip to match the track’s key.
  3. Open the clip. MIDI editor footer → Generators → Seed.
  4. Parameters: Pitch Range ±7 semitones. Density 60%. Note Length 1/16 or 1/8.
  5. Hit Roll. Live fills the clip.
  6. Audition. If bad, Roll again. Budget ten rolls.
  7. When one lands, close the Generator popover. The notes are now editable MIDI. Capture MIDI (Cmd+Shift+C) is a separate path if you Roll while the clip plays and want to keep what you heard.

Receipts. Spec B 12.3 — “Seed generator produces acid-style basslines within key constraints; combine with Chance 60–75% on hats for non-repeating patterns.”

Caveat. Seed obeys the Scale setting on the clip. If the clip’s scale is C-major but your track is D-minor, the result will clash. Check the clip scale before rolling.

Filter sweep — the Ableton walk-through

Filter sweep — the Ableton walk-through

One Auto Filter, one macro, one envelope. The whole trick is the unlinked envelope loop.

Setup:

  1. Shift-select all tracks involved in the duplicate. Cmd+G to Group. Rename the group var3 group.
  2. Insert Auto Filter on the group’s insert chain. Filter type LP24 (Clean). Frequency 20 kHz, Resonance 15%.
  3. Cmd-right-click the Frequency knob → Map to Macro 1. The macro is now your sweep control.
  4. Right-click the group clip → Show Envelope. Envelope selector → Macro 1.
  5. Draw a ramp: bar 1 at macro 0 (closed), bar 16 at macro 100 (open).

Unlinked envelope:

  1. Click the Envelope pane’s Linked/Unlinked toggle. Set envelope loop to 7 bars. Audio loops 16.
  2. The sweep now never lands at the same position across repeats. Autechre polymeter trick.

Limitation. A 24 dB LP sweep eats low end before it reaches the bass register. If the kick disappears, switch to BP with Resonance 40% for a body-preserving sweep, or keep LP but side-chain-bypass the kick channel from the group.

One parameter. Not two.
