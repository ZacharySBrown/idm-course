IDM / W1 Week 1 — Listen like an engineer, subtract like a producer

Slide s01 · title

The course begins with silence

The course begins with silence

Specifically — the silence after you mute the drums on Benjamins and realize the bass was doing most of the work.

This is not a production course. Not yet. This is a listening course that happens to use Ableton.

Twelve weeks from now you will have shipped twelve tracks. Week 1 is the door. You do not open synths. You do not touch Roar. You load a reference track, pull a stem, and write down what is actually happening in the mix.

The rest of the course is built on the assumption that you can do that.

Slide s02 · concept

Listen like an engineer — the critical-listening protocol

Listen like an engineer

The protocol is not romantic. Berklee Online OMPRD-382 teaches it in one paragraph, and every intermediate-producer curriculum repeats it: load reference and WIP on adjacent tracks, level-matched via LUFS meter, listen in 30-second chunks, switch.

Notes, five axes:

  • Low-end energy — sub doing work, or implied by kick body?
  • Stereo width — mono at 80 Hz, wider by 400 Hz, collapsing at the vocal
  • Vocal/lead clarity — where it sits against everything else
  • Transient sharpness — click vs thump
  • Reverb depth — dry, 15 ms slap, or 2-second plate

Notes get written on paper. The screen is the enemy this week. — Booth: “the screen has to go off. It’s an illusion that totally pollutes what you’re thinking and what you’re listening to.”

Slide s03 · caveat

What you listen on matters, and less than you think

What you listen on matters, and less than you think

The rig sidebar, because it comes up every week:

  • Yamaha HS5 (2014) — the dull, honest near-field. Translates to laptop speakers because it is almost a laptop speaker with a tweeter.
  • Neumann KH80 DSP (2016) — better near-field, room correction, if you have the room to justify it.
  • AKG K371 (2019) — closed-back, Harman-curve-ish, the cheap tracking headphone most charts agree on.
  • Sennheiser HD600 (1997) — open-back, mid-forward, still the reference your mastering engineer has in the hallway.

Pick one pair of speakers and one pair of headphones. A/B between them every session. The goal is not flat — it is known. You cannot mix against a surface whose lies you have not memorized.

This is why monitor arguments online are unwinnable. — Live’s Reference track control assumes you already solved it.

Full mix vs drums-muted

Slide s05 · concept

Drop one stem — the single most useful ear exercise

Drop one stem

The exercise is older than the course. Put a song in your DAW. Mute one part. Listen to what remains. Mute a different part. Listen again.

Order of operations on Benjamins:

  1. Play the full mix for 15 seconds. Note the hook.
  2. Mute benjamins_drums.wav. Play again. Where does the time go? Most students realize the bass was holding the tempo.
  3. Un-mute drums, mute benjamins_bass.wav. The harmonic skeleton disappears; what is left is a rhythmic shell.
  4. Mute everything except benjamins_other.wav. This is where the identity of the track was hiding — the texture was doing more work than the arrangement hierarchy suggested.

Write a single sentence per stem. Not an essay. One sentence.

The trained ear is the one that can hear the absent part. Separators make that trainable without an analog console.

Slide s06 · concept

Subtract like a producer — the densest-case principle

Subtract like a producer

Subtraction is the most finish-friendly of the five arrangement methods this course teaches, and the one you can apply as a listener before you have a loop of your own.

The move, formally:

  1. Identify the densest bar of the track.
  2. Treat that as the master state.
  3. Every other section is a mute of the master state — intro is everything muted except texture, A adds drums, B adds bass, breakdown drops drums, peak is unmuted, outro inverts the intro.

Week 2 is where you build a track this way. Week 1 is for hearing it in someone else’s mix. Play Benjamins and identify which version of the loop the producer treated as master state. Almost always the chorus, rarely the drop, occasionally the second verse.

Slide s07 · caveat

The 45-minute rabbit hole — separate the mentalities

The 45-minute rabbit hole

A rule that sounds arbitrary and is not: every new sound gets 45 minutes, then you commit or you move on. Kitchen timer on the desk. When it rings, bounce-in-place or freeze.

This is not a productivity hack. It is a workflow that assumes you have already lost the other version of this fight. Separate the four mentalities into four separate sessions:

  • R&D — sound design. Save presets. Nothing used yet.
  • Writing — only patches already saved. No new synths.
  • Arrangement — monitors down, Arrangement View full-screen, laptop lid closed.
  • Mixing — separate pass after arrangement is locked.

Mixing inside a loop is the canonical intermediate-producer mistake. Every curriculum says so. — Eno keeps the cards on the desk for this moment.

Slide s08 · concept

Pre-place the locators, then close the laptop

Pre-place the locators, then close the laptop

A session template, not aspirational, load-for-every-session:

  • Locators at 0:00 intro / 0:45 A / 2:00 B / 3:00 breakdown / 3:45 peak / 5:00 outro / 5:45 end.
  • A reference track on a muted audio lane. One Aphex, one Autechre, one Dilla, one Kendrick. A/B every 20 minutes.
  • Drum / Bass / Melody / Texture / Vocal groups pre-grouped in the Session.
  • One return for plate, one for tape delay, one for granular, one for Roar feedback. You are not using them this week — they are there so you do not spend the next 45 minutes building them.

The locators are suggestions, not contracts. The point is that placing them converts the session from open-ended into finite.

Then close the laptop lid, route to speakers, and write notes on paper.

Slide s09 · reference

Twelve in twelve — the cadence the course assumes

Twelve in twelve

One track per week, twelve weeks. No track is the track. The track is the log.

Constraint and recombination beats sound design. Completion beats perfection. A shipped three-minute sketch in Week 3 teaches more than six months tweaking a kick.

The pattern the finishing producers share:

  • Kieran Hebden released Three near-unmastered because the mix was good enough and the idea was right.
  • Porter Robinson rebuilt “Get Your Wish” dozens of times over two years — the craft is re-shipping, not the first pass.
  • Burial on Sound Forge: “Fuckit, I’m going to stick to this shitty little computer program.”

After each track, three lines: what worked, what was hard, what to try next. Over ten tracks, patterns emerge.

Slide s10 · caveat

A confession, and one Sound on Sound aside

A confession, and one aside

Confession first. Every author of this kind of course will tell you gear does not matter and then publish a paragraph naming eight pieces of gear. The honest version: gear mostly does not matter, except when it does, and the skill is noticing which case you are in. — Jenkinson, asked about the QY700, answers mostly about why he refuses a computer rather than why he chose the sequencer.

Aside, Sound on Sound-style. Ableton’s Reference track control in Live 12’s mixer was shipped because Reference 4-style plugins had made it obvious engineers were already doing this manually. The protocol predates the button by thirty years. The button is a convenience.

You will not need the button this week. An unmuted audio clip is the same protocol with fewer abstractions.

Slide s11 · exercise

Ship the listening — the 30-second mute-the-drums clip

Ship the listening

The deliverable. Thirty seconds. No synths. No processing. This is a listening exercise and it stays a listening exercise.

Steps:

  1. Load benjamins_drums.wav, benjamins_bass.wav, benjamins_vocals.wav, benjamins_other.wav on four adjacent audio tracks. Align to bar 1.
  2. Solo each for 20 seconds. Write one sentence per stem on paper — what it is doing rhythmically, what it is doing harmonically.
  3. In Arrangement View, place a locator at 0:00 and 0:15. From 0:00 to 0:15, mute the drums track. At 0:15 un-mute it. Everything else plays through.
  4. Bounce the result as a 30-second stereo file named w01_<yourname>_mute-ab.wav. 44.1 kHz / 24-bit.

Deliverable: the WAV and the four one-sentence notes. Post both. That is Week 1.

No triumphant wrap-up. Go listen.

Slide i02 · concept

The five axes, one pass at a time

The five axes, one at a time

Unpacking the protocol. Each pass through a 30-second chunk, you’re listening for exactly one thing. No multitasking.

  • Low-end energy — is the sub a distinct element, or is the kick’s body carrying the low octaves?
  • Stereo width — mono around 80 Hz, widening through the midrange, narrowing again at the vocal.
  • Vocal/lead clarity — where does the lead element sit against the bed, and what got carved out to make room.
  • Transient sharpness — click vs thump. Short attack vs long attack. Engineers notice this first.
  • Reverb depth — dry, 15 ms slap, or two-second plate. Name the space.

Five chunks, five sentences. That’s the whole method.

Paper, not screen

Slide d01 · concept

Where the four stems live

Where the four stems live, frequency-wise

Rough occupancy, in Hz, for the four-stem Demucs split. Ranges overlap — that’s the point; separation is a probabilistic classification, not a clean crossover network.

  • Drums (kick + snare + hats) — kick body sits 40-120 Hz, snare fundamental 150-250 Hz, hat content 3 kHz and up.
  • Bass — 40-250 Hz for the fundamental, harmonic content up to 800 Hz depending on synthesis.
  • Vocals — 200 Hz to 4 kHz for the lead range, sibilance out to 8-10 kHz.
  • Other — everything else: pads, leads, textures, typically 100 Hz to 8 kHz with the densest content in the 300 Hz-2 kHz band.

The Demucs U-Net learns these masks from training data; it doesn’t apply a fixed filterbank.

Approximate frequency occupancy per stem. Ranges overlap; the separator classifies by more than frequency.

Slide d02 · concept

The listening protocol, as a loop

The listening protocol, as a flowchart

The four-step loop. Run it until the reference and your WIP stop surprising you differently.

The LUFSLUFS — Loudness Units relative to Full Scale — the EBU R128 / ITU-R BS.1770 standard for perceived loudness, measured over time with K-weighting. Streaming platforms target roughly -14 LUFS integrated (Spotify, Apple Music); mastering for vinyl or club -8 to -10. Replaced RMS as the honest loudness metric. wikipedia↗ level-match step is the one students skip. Without it you’re comparing loudness, not balance — the louder track always wins the A/B. Integrated LUFS on a short-term window, matched within half a dB, is the floor.

One sentence per pass. Not an essay. If you can’t summarize the chunk in a sentence, you weren’t listening for one thing.

graph LR A[Load reference + WIP<br/>adjacent tracks] --> B[Level-match<br/>LUFS short-term] B --> C[Chunk to 30s<br/>one axis per pass] C --> D[A/B switch] D --> E[Write one<br/>sentence] E --> C
The Berklee critical-listening loop. Level-match first, chunk, switch, write — then repeat.

Slide d03 · concept

Demucs, at arm's length

How the stems got made — Demucs, briefly

Demucs is a U-Net-shaped neural separator. In the hybrid version, one branch processes the waveform directly, one branch processes a time-frequency representation, and the outputs fuse into four masks — drums, bass, vocals, other.

You do not need to train it. stemforge wraps the pretrained weights and runs a single command: stemforge default --no-slice benjamins.wav.

The separator is not clean. Sibilance leaks into other. Kick sub-harmonics smear into bass. Noticing the artifacts is itself a listening exercise — where the separator fails tells you what was tangled in the mix.

graph LR A[benjamins.wav<br/>stereo 44.1kHz] --> B[Demucs U-Net<br/>hybrid waveform+STFT] B --> C[drums.wav] B --> D[bass.wav] B --> E[vocals.wav] B --> F[other.wav]
Simplified stemforge dispatch. The real U-Net has hundreds of layers; this is the block view.

Slide i04 · concept

A spectrogram, in passing

A spectrogram, in passing

Before we leave the separator — a spectrogram is just time on the x-axis, frequency on the y-axis, amplitude as color. Most producers look at one maybe twice a year and get nothing from it.

The exception: when a stem separator fails, the failure is visible in the spectrogram. Drums leaking into the vocals stem shows up as wideband transient slashes at the attack points. Bass leaking into other shows up as low-frequency blobs under melodic content.

You will not need this view this week. But the vocabulary matters for W8 when we come back to resynthesis.

Slide i05 · concept

The densest bar is master state

The densest bar, as master state

Subtraction inverts the usual additive workflow. You do not build the intro first. You build the densest bar first — chorus, drop, peak — and treat every other section as a mute of that state.

  • Intro — everything muted except one texture.
  • A — drums unmute.
  • B — bass joins.
  • Breakdown — drums drop, texture returns.
  • Peak — master state, all unmuted.
  • Outro — inverse of the intro.

This is finish-friendly because the arrangement becomes a mute-automation problem, not a sound-design problem. Rubin, in The Creative Act, frames it as removing everything that is not the song.

Slide i08 · caveat

The half-dB threshold (approximately)

The half-decibel threshold

Andrew Huang’s claim — roughly: most listeners, under controlled A/B, cannot reliably identify a half-decibel level difference. Above one dB, they can. Below half a dB, it’s a coin flip.

This is why LUFSLUFS — Loudness Units relative to Full Scale — the EBU R128 / ITU-R BS.1770 standard for perceived loudness, measured over time with K-weighting. Streaming platforms target roughly -14 LUFS integrated (Spotify, Apple Music); mastering for vinyl or club -8 to -10. Replaced RMS as the honest loudness metric. wikipedia↗ level-matching matters in the reference protocol. If your reference is 1.5 dB louder than your WIP, the louder track always wins the A/B — not because it’s better, because it’s louder.

The linter policy at the top of this lesson flags unconfirmed attributions. The half-dB claim is well-repeated in production YouTube; an exact peer-reviewed source is not in bibliography.json. Treat as a studio-rat heuristic, not a double-blind result.

Slide le02 · reference

Yamaha HS5 — the dull, honest near-field

Yamaha HS5 — the dull, honest near-field

Yamaha HS5 (2014). Two-way, 5-inch woofer, 1-inch tweeter, 70 Hz-30 kHz, 54 dB SNR. The spiritual successor to the NS-10, which was the studio reference nobody actually liked listening to for thirty years.

The HS5 translates to laptop speakers because it is almost a laptop speaker with a tweeter. Mixes that sound balanced on HS5 usually survive the car, the phone, and the AirPods test. Mixes that sound good on HS5 are suspicious — they probably have hyped lows you will hear on everything else.

Entry-level near-field. If you only own one pair, you could do worse.

Slide le03 · reference

Neumann KH80 DSP — better, if the room deserves it

Neumann KH80 DSP — better, if the room deserves it

Neumann KH80 DSP (2016). Four-inch woofer, one-inch tweeter, onboard DSP with room-correction via the MA 1 measurement mic. 57 Hz-21 kHz (-3 dB).

The DSP is the feature. The KH80 can EQ out the worst of your room’s bass modes and first-reflection buildup, within the limits of what EQ can fix (which is: less than you’d hope, more than nothing).

A better speaker than the HS5. Also twice the price, and wasted if your room has a slap-back off the back wall you haven’t treated. Buy the treatment first.

Slide le04 · reference

AKG K371 + Sennheiser HD600 — the headphone A/B

The headphone A/B — AKG K371 and Sennheiser HD600

Two headphones, two jobs.

AKG K371 (2019) — closed-back, 50 mm driver, 5 Hz-40 kHz, 32 ohm. The cheapest headphone that measures close to the Harman target curve, which is the best-available proxy for what most listeners find neutral. Use it for tracking, late-night work, and when you cannot play through speakers.

Sennheiser HD600 (1997) — open-back, 300 ohm, 12 Hz-40.5 kHz. The reference your mastering engineer has in the hallway. Mid-forward, honest, leaky. The 300-ohm impedance needs a real headphone amp; laptop outputs will sound anemic.

Cross-reference between them. Mixes that agree on both are probably fine.

Slide le05 · concept

Cross-reference, do not pick one

Why you cross-reference, not pick one

No surface is neutral. The HS5 hypes 8 kHz. The KH80 is flatter but reveals room problems you would otherwise miss. The K371 has a closed-back low-frequency lift. The HD600 is mid-forward and slightly thin at 80 Hz.

The cross-reference habit:

  1. Mix on speakers.
  2. Check on the closed-back headphone.
  3. Check on the open-back.
  4. Check in the car if you have one. Check on AirPods.
  5. When four out of five surfaces agree, the mix is probably done.

The studio rat’s joke: the mix is done when you have run out of surfaces that disagree.

Slide le06 · concept

Room acoustics — first reflections and bass modes

Room acoustics, the irreducible floor

Two things a room does to your mix that you cannot EQ out:

  • First reflections — sound leaves the monitor, bounces off the side wall or desk, arrives at your ear 3-15 ms after the direct sound. Your brain fuses the two into a comb filter. Result: midrange peaks and nulls that move when you move your head. Fix with absorption panels at the mirror points.
  • Bass modes — low-frequency standing waves between parallel walls. A 3-meter wall puts a mode at ~57 Hz; a 4-meter wall at ~43 Hz. Below 200 Hz the room is always louder than the speaker in some spots and quieter in others. Fix with bass traps in corners, or accept that sub-bass decisions made at this desk are negotiable.

This is why the KH80’s room correction helps and does not solve. EQ cannot delete a reflection; it can only pre-distort for one.
