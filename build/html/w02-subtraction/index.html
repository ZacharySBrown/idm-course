IDM / W2 Week 2 — The Subtraction method: finish by removing

Slide s01 · title

The loop is finished. You are the one who is not.

Slide s02 · concept

Subtraction, stated plainly

Subtraction, stated plainly

The method in one paragraph. Build the single densest loop your track will ever contain — the Peak. Duplicate that loop across a six-minute timeline. Then mute backward. Every other section — Intro, A, B, Reduction, Outro — is made by switching elements off, not by writing new ones.

Why this works.

  • Cohesion is free. Every section derives from the same master state.
  • The “what goes next” question has one answer: less of what you already have.
  • The loop trap breaks because you are no longer trying to make the loop sound finished. The loop is already finished. The track is what’s unfinished.

Source. Zak Handbook §5.3. Track Hebden’s thesis the whole way through: “Arrangement is just everything. There’s no point in having a good sound if it’s not arranged into anything” (Tape Notes #140).

Slide s03 · concept

Place the locators before you touch a sound

Place the locators before you touch a sound

Open Arrangement View. Before you load a single instrument, drop seven locators at these timecodes:

Locator Time What lives here
Intro 0:00 Texture + one constant element
A 0:45 Texture + drums
B 2:00 Texture + drums + bass
Reduction 3:15 Texture + a single vocal chop
Peak 3:45 All elements, densest state
Outro 5:00 Inverse of Intro
End 5:45 Stop

Right-click the ruler → Add Locator, then rename inline. Locator names follow the project into bounces.

What this buys you. A finite session. The locators are suggestions, not contracts — the act of placing them converts open-ended into shaped. Zak Handbook §5.2. bib:ableton_live_12_manual, Arrangement View → Locators.

Slide s04 · concept

Build the Peak first — the densest sixteen bars

Build the Peak first — the densest sixteen bars

Write the climax of the track first. Sixteen bars. Everything on.

Checklist for the Peak loop:

  • Kick, snare, hats, one perc layer
  • Sub + mid bass (two tracks, separated bands)
  • One melodic element (pad or lead, pick one)
  • One texture (field, noise bed, reverb wash on a send)
  • One vocal chop or one-shot
  • One “detail” element — a ratchet, a click, a stutter

The constraint. If it is not in the Peak, it does not exist in the track. You cannot add a new element at bar 96 because the section feels thin. Thin is the point — Subtraction doing its job.

Loop the sixteen bars in Session View. A/B against a reference. When you enjoy it, stop and bounce. Zak Handbook §5.3, §6.1.

Slide s05 · concept

Mute backward: Reduction, Breakdown, Intro

Mute backward: Reduction, Breakdown, Intro

Duplicate the Peak across the full six-minute timeline. Then, inside each locator region, toggle tracks off with the Track Activator (the green square in the track header).

Default mute map — start here, mutate later:

  • Peak (3:45–5:00): nothing muted.
  • B (2:00–3:15): mute perc 2 + stutter.
  • A (0:45–2:00): also mute vocal chop + lead.
  • Reduction (3:15–3:45): mute drums + bass.
  • Intro (0:00–0:45): only anchor + texture remain.
  • Outro (5:00–5:45): inverse of Intro.

The load-bearing trick. If you lose the kick at bar 24, you don’t replace it — you let the Fridmann bass carry the weight, and the listener doesn’t notice the trade. Zak Handbook §5.3.

Slide s06 · reference

The constant anchor — Hebden on what rides through

The constant anchor — Hebden on what rides through

Tape Notes #140 — Kieran Hebden:

“Decide which things ride through the track in a simple way, and which the detail is in.”

Translation into Subtraction. Pick one element in the Peak that never mutes for the whole six minutes. Candidates:

  • A single pad note
  • A hat pattern running 1/16
  • A sub drone on the root
  • A field-recording bed

That element is the constant anchor. Not interesting on its own — that’s the job. It gives the listener a thread through the chaos while everything else mutates.

Deflation. An anchor will not save a bad Peak. If the densest state is thin, the anchor exposes the thinness across the whole track. Finishing move, not a rescue move. Handbook §5.8.

Slide s07 · concept

The 8-bar rule and the one-bar silence

The 8-bar rule and the one-bar silence

The rule — Rubin via every producer who ever shipped: if you enjoyed a loop for eight bars, break it. The moment you want to hear it again is the cue to cut.

How to break it inside Subtraction:

  • Mute one layer for bars 9–16. Re-enable for 17–24.
  • Duplicate the drum clip, shift it back 1/16 for bars 25–32.
  • Insert one bar of true silence at bar 48. No tail. No tape stop. A gap.

Silence is compositional. Rubin: “Perfection is obtained when there’s no longer anything to take away.” (Handbook §5.9; primary source flagged in needs_bib:.) Autechre’s Tri Repetae and Aphex’s #3 both use 2+ bars of near-silence as structural punctuation. Handbook §5.8, §5.9.

Slide s08 · caveat

When Subtraction fails you

When Subtraction fails you

Subtraction is not the only arrangement method. Four more are coming — Variation, Skeleton, Resample-and-Mutate, Bounce-and-Arrange. Use Subtraction when it fits; do not force it.

Subtraction is a bad fit when:

  • Your Peak is thin. Muting a thin Peak gives you a thinner A section. Fix the Peak first or switch to Skeleton.
  • The track wants a new idea at bar 80. Subtraction forbids new elements. If the track is begging for a second motif after the Reduction, that’s a Variation Generation session, not a Subtraction one. Handbook §5.4.
  • You cannot commit to a densest state. If your Peak keeps changing, you’re still in the writing mentality, not the arrangement mentality. Close the session. Come back with a bounced loop.

Receipt. Handbook §1 (“The two-mentality problem”) and §6.1 are explicit: Writing and Arrangement are separate sessions. Do not mix them.

Slide s09 · concept

Drop-one-stem — the subtraction ear-training drill

Drop-one-stem — the subtraction ear-training drill

Stemforge recipe w02-little-by-little-stems splits little_by_little.wav into drums, bass, vocals, and other. We use it to train the ear that Subtraction depends on: what does this track sound like with one stem gone?

A/B listens in this lesson:

  • Full mix vs. drums muted → hear what the kick was doing for the low-end perceived loudness.
  • Full mix vs. bass alone → hear how much of the “vibe” was actually one line on one stem.
  • Full mix vs. vocal-only → hear the anchor. It is almost never the loudest element.

What to write in your notebook during the A/B: Which stem could I mute for the Reduction section of my own track and still keep the listener? That question is the Subtraction method, compressed.

Slide s10 · reference

Finished is better than perfect — Rubin, Conte, and the kitchen timer

Finished is better than perfect — Rubin, Conte, and the kitchen timer

The mantra, Handbook §6.1: “Good is good enough. Finished is better than perfect. Ship the track.” — Jack Conte via Rick Rubin (primary sources in needs_bib:).

Operational rules for this week:

  • 45-minute time-box on any single element. Physical kitchen timer. When it rings, commit.
  • Huang’s half-decibel threshold: adjustments below perceptual threshold = perfectionism. Stop.
  • Eno’s Oblique Strategies when stuck: “Cut a vital connection.” “Just carry on.” (bib:oblique_strategies_eno)
  • Bounce in Place the Peak before muting. Frozen audio is expensive to un-freeze — that expense is the feature. bib:ableton_live_12_manual.

The goal this week is not a great track. The goal is a finished track. Great comes from doing this fifty-two times.

Slide s11 · exercise

Ship a 3-minute mp3 in one hour

Ship a 3-minute mp3 in one hour

The assignment.

  1. Take your most recent 16-bar loop. Not a new one — the last one you made.
  2. Write the Subtraction arrangement on paper. Six sections. For each, list which elements drop. No DAW yet.
  3. Open Ableton. Drop the locators from slide 3. Duplicate the loop across six minutes.
  4. One-hour timer. Mute backward. Do not add elements. Do not open a synth.
  5. Insert one bar of true silence somewhere between bar 32 and bar 80.
  6. Bounce to mp3 at 192 kbps. Three minutes, cut from the middle of the six.
  7. Upload before next session. Paper plan in the thread next to the file.

What we grade. That the file exists. That is the whole criterion. Handbook §6.1; recipe w02-little-by-little-stems for A/B reference.

A 3-minute mp3 rough, Subtraction arrangement only. Built from your most recent 16-bar loop. Paper plan before DAW. One-hour time-box on the Arrangement View pass. Dropbox link or Discord upload by next session.

Slide i01 · reference

Burial, Untrue (2007) — the prehistory of the inverse drop

Burial, Untrue — the prehistory of the inverse drop

Before “drop” meant a festival moment, it meant the place where a track removes its own drums. Untrue (Hyperland, 2007) is the Rosetta stone. Listen to “Archangel” at 1:45: the kick-and-clatter pattern that carried the first minute is gone, and a sidechained pad is left holding the room alone. That gap is the Reduction section, eleven years before producers started naming it.

Why it reads as Subtraction. Burial composed in Sound Forge without a grid — no MIDI, no Arrangement View, no locators. The whole record is regions placed on a timeline, track activators in spirit if not in software. When he wanted a section to change, he muted regions. That is the method. bib:burial_soundforge_quote.

Slide i03 · reference

Rubin in the room — a philosophy is a workflow claim

Rubin in the room — the philosophy is a workflow claim

The reason Rubin keeps appearing in an arrangement-method lesson is not the mystique. It is a specific operational claim: that a finished rough is worth more than an unfinished masterpiece, and that the fastest route to a finished rough is to remove, not add.

What the photo is here to do. Remind you that the person saying “less” spent forty years producing records that sound like more. Def Jam, Johnny Cash’s American sessions, Slayer, System of a Down. The advice is not monastic — it is post-abundance. Rubin had every option available and the method is what he chose anyway.

For the Subtraction lesson. Rubin’s frame: finishing is a compositional decision. Muting a track at bar 64 is the composition. Handbook §5.9.

Slide i04 · reference

Four Tet, Rounds (2003) — the constant anchor

Four Tet, Rounds (2003) — the constant anchor in practice

“Unspoken,” the fourteen-minute closer on Rounds, is the reference recording for the constant anchor idea. A single drum loop rides through the entire track. Everything else — pads, melodica, chopped vocal, live percussion overdubs — mutates around it.

What to listen for. The loop never stops. It gets dirtier, it gets ducked under other material, but it never drops out. Hebden’s thesis in Tape Notes #140: “Decide which things ride through the track in a simple way, and which the detail is in.” The drums ride. The detail is everywhere else. bib:tape_notes_140_four_tet, bib:hebden_unspoken_production.

Why Rounds and not a newer record. Because the constraint was tighter — fewer plugins, fewer options, an anchor-first composer working in a smaller room. The method is more visible in the early work.

Slide i05 · reference

J Dilla, Donuts (2006) — dynamics arc without a drop

J Dilla, Donuts (2006) — dynamics arc without a drop

Donuts is thirty-one beats in forty-three minutes. Most sit under two minutes. None have a festival drop. And yet the dynamics arc on something like “Stop” or “Workinonit” is unmistakable — sections get denser, sections drop out, a sample mutes for half a bar and returns transformed.

What this teaches for Subtraction. Dilla’s method on the MPC3000 was pad-level mutes inside a pattern chain. A pad is a track. Muting a pad at bar nine is the Subtraction method, shrunk to a one-minute beat. Charnas 2022, bib:dilla_time_charnas. MPC3000 on display at the Smithsonian, bib:mpc3000_dilla_smithsonian.

Transfer. Whatever you think you need six minutes to say, Dilla said in ninety seconds, with fewer elements, by muting harder.

Slide i06 · concept

An arrangement view with dynamics arc visible to the eye

An arrangement view, dynamics arc visible to the eye

A well-subtracted track has a shape you can see in the Arrangement View before you hit play. Density low at the edges, peaks around the two-thirds mark, one visible gap inside the reduction.

What a good reference arrangement looks like on screen:

  • Lanes thinning toward the intro and outro (tracks muted → empty regions)
  • A dense block in the Peak region where every lane is lit
  • One narrow vertical gap — the one-bar silence — inside the Reduction
  • A constant lane running edge-to-edge (the anchor)

Why the screenshot matters. Subtraction is partly a visual method. If your Arrangement View looks uniform from bar 1 to bar 144, the listener’s attention will drift at the same rate. Shape on screen predicts shape in the ear. Handbook §5.3.

Slide ar01 · concept

The Locators pane — where the template actually lives

The Locators pane — where the template actually lives

Locators are timeline markers in Ableton’s Arrangement View. They live in the scrub-area above the tracks. Right-click the ruler → Add Locator (or use the ▶ button in the transport toolbar). Rename inline.

What the Locators pane buys you for Subtraction:

  • Named sections that travel. Locator names bake into bounces as chapter markers — when you export to WAV, a DAW with chapter-mark support shows them. Useful for A/B review.
  • Jump-to-section playback. Number-pad digits 1–9 jump to the first nine locators. This is how you audition the Reduction without scrubbing.
  • Snap-to-grid placement. Hold Cmd while dragging a locator to break snap if you want a one-beat offset. Otherwise it locks to the bar.

bib:ableton_live_12_manual, Arrangement View → Locators.

Slide ar02 · concept

Bounce Groups (Live 12.3) — the commit-to-tape button

Bounce Groups — Live 12.3’s commit-to-tape button

Bounce GroupsBounce Groups — Live 12.3 feature — bounce an entire group track to a new audio track in one action, with optional Paste Bounced Audio back into place. Turns bounce-and-arrange from a manual ritual into a keyboard shortcut. wikipedia↗ landed in Live 12.3 (2025). Right-click a group track header → Bounce Group. Ableton consolidates the group’s output to a single audio track, preserving the group’s send/insert state as a frozen artifact.

Parameter table — what the dialog asks:

Parameter Default What it does
Render range Entire arrangement Or loop region, or selected time
Sample rate Project rate (48 kHz) Inherits project unless overridden
Bit depth 32-bit float Keep at 32-bit for reversible headroom
Include return fx On Captures send-returns into the bounced region
Delete source Off Keep off until you are sure; destructive
Preserve sidechain Group-internal only External sidechains are not captured

For Subtraction. Bounce the Peak group before you mute anything. Frozen audio is cheap to mute, expensive to un-freeze. The expense is the feature — it forces commitment. bib:ableton_live_12_manual.

Slide ar03 · concept

Group tracks, Track Activator, and the mute-backward workflow

Group tracks, Track Activator, and the mute-backward workflow

Group tracks in Ableton are folders for tracks. Cmd+G on a selection creates a group. In the group header, the Track Activator (the green square top-left of the header) toggles the entire group.

The Subtraction workflow, in six keystrokes:

  1. Select the Peak loop’s tracks. Cmd+G. Group named “PEAK”.
  2. Drag-select across the Peak locator region. Cmd+D. Duplicates the region across the timeline.
  3. Inside each non-Peak locator region, click individual track activators to mute lanes.
  4. Cmd+0 (zero) toggles the clip-level activator if you want to mute a single region without muting the lane.
  5. S solos the selected track — used for A/B listening, not arrangement.
  6. M mutes the selected track. 0 on the track header does the same.

Distinction. Track Activator (green square) is what Subtraction uses — it persists across the whole track. Solo (the S) is an audition tool only. Don’t confuse them in your arrangement pass. bib:ableton_live_12_manual.

Slide ar04 · concept

The six-minute locator template — density curve

The six-minute locator template — density curve

The template from slide s03 plotted as a density curve. The y-axis is active-track count (a proxy for arrangement density); the x-axis is time.

Read the shape, not the numbers. The curve starts low (Intro: two active lanes), climbs through A and B, drops sharply into the Reduction (one lane), spikes into the Peak (all lanes), and tapers through the Outro. The one-bar silence sits inside the Reduction as the curve’s minimum.

Why this shape and not another. Handbook §5.3: the Reduction’s job is to throw the Peak into relief. If the density curve is monotonic — always climbing or always falling — the Peak lands as predictable. The dip before the peak is what makes the peak feel earned. See the SVG on this slide for the template shape; overlay it on your own arrangement to compare.

Density curve over the Subtraction template. Dip before Peak is what makes the Peak land. Dashed green: the constant anchor rides through.

Slide ar05 · concept

Subtraction vs Bounce-and-Arrange vs Skeleton — side-by-side

Subtraction vs Bounce-and-Arrange vs Skeleton — side-by-side

Three arrangement methods, three entry points. Subtraction is this week’s. The other two are coming in later lessons — placed here so you know when not to use Subtraction.

Subtraction. Start from the densest Peak. Mute backward. Best when: you already have a strong sixteen-bar loop and finishing is the blocker. Worst when: your Peak is thin.

Bounce-and-Arrange. Bounce all stems first, then treat them as audio clips on a fresh Arrangement timeline. Cut, move, silence. Best when: you have too many options in the writing session and need to commit. Worst when: the writing itself is unfinished. Handbook §5.6.

Skeleton. Write only the sparsest version — anchor + one melodic idea + one percussive element — for the full six minutes, then build up. Best when: your Peak doesn’t exist yet. Worst when: you already have a dense loop you love; Skeleton asks you to throw it away. Handbook §5.4.

See the diagram for the signal-flow comparison.

graph TD A[Dense 16-bar loop exists?] -->|Yes, I love it| S[Subtraction: mute backward from Peak] A -->|Yes, but too many takes| B[Bounce-and-Arrange: freeze stems then cut] A -->|No, loop is thin| K[Skeleton: build sparse first, add up] S --> Z[Finished 6-min arrangement] B --> Z K --> Z
Three entry points, one output. The method follows the state of the loop, not the other way round.

Slide ar06 · concept

Consolidate, Bounce in Place, Freeze, Bounce Group — which one, when

Consolidate, Bounce in Place, and Freeze — which one, when

Three Ableton commands that all look like “commit the audio,” all different.

Command What it does Reversible? Use when
Consolidate (Cmd+J) Merges a selection of clips on one track into a single audio clip No — destructive You have ten overlapping MIDI clips in a lane; you want one audio region.
Bounce in Place Renders a track’s MIDI + effects to audio, muting the source Reversible (source preserved, just muted) You want to free up CPU from a plugin chain but keep the option to un-freeze.
Freeze Track Renders to a hidden cache; track becomes non-editable Fully reversible — unfreeze restores CPU pressure during writing; you’re not committing yet.
Bounce Group (12.3) Renders a group track to a new audio track; source group muted Reversible Subtraction’s checkpoint — commit the Peak before muting.

For Subtraction. Bounce Group the Peak. Don’t Consolidate it — Consolidate is destructive and Subtraction needs the option to un-bounce if the Peak was wrong. bib:ableton_live_12_manual.
