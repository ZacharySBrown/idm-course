Slide s03 · concept

Prefuse 73 — the four-step resample ladder

Prefuse 73 — the four-step resample ladder

Herren’s rig: two turntables into Akai MPC2000XL into Pro Tools (bib:akai_mpc2000xl_manual, bib:prefuse_73_xlr8r).

The ladder, per XLR8R:

  1. Sample a 2–4s vocal phrase to a pad.
  2. Slice across 16 pads, syllable- or phoneme-length.
  3. Finger-drum a new rhythm.
  4. Resample to a single pad.
  5. Repeat 2–4 on the resample. Third time too.

Ableton: drop the Hot Pants bounce → Right-click → Slice to New MIDI Track → 1/16 or By Transient. Record with Note Repeat. Freeze, flatten, reload into a fresh Simpler. Repeat (bib:ableton_live_12_manual).

Herren on timing: “I don’t try to match bpms and shit”. Off-grid is load-bearing.

Slide s04 · concept

Dilla time — 1/32 micro-chops and per-pad drift

Dilla time — 1/32 micro-chops and per-pad drift

Per Dilla Time (bib:charnas_dilla_time): slice vocals at 1/32, rearrange until the source is gone, leave per-pad timing deliberately inconsistent. MPC3000 had per-voice swing — hats at one %, kicks at another, snares dragged 20–30 ms late (bib:akai_mpc3000_manual). Polyrhythmic hybrid, not sloppy.

Dilla time is the course term for this per-pad swing signature. Defined here.

Ableton recipe:

  1. Drop Hot Pants bounce on an audio track.
  2. Right-click → Slice to New MIDI Track → Slice to 1/32.
  3. Rearrange the MIDI — don’t replay source order. Ornament flams at 1/64.
  4. In each chain’s Simpler, nudge a handful of notes ±15–25 ms. Different per chain. That is the drift.
  5. Bounce, listen, commit.

Don’t Cry is the A/B reference.

Slide s05 · caveat

Correcting the Donuts SP-303 myth

Correcting the Donuts SP-303 myth

Charnas, Dilla Time (bib:charnas_dilla_time): Donuts was primarily Pro Tools + Akai MPC3000 (bib:akai_mpc3000_manual). The SP-303 and SP-404 arrived at the hospital late, after most of the record was tracked. The Smithsonian holds the MPC3000 as the instrument of record (bib:smithsonian_dilla_mpc3000).

The “made entirely on an SP-303 from a hospital bed” story is wrong. It mistakes circumstances for method. The method was the MPC.

Why it matters here: chasing SP-303 sound won’t get Dilla’s sound. Per-pad swing and 1/32 chops were MPC3000 behaviors. Ableton Simpler in Slicing + Ornament + nudged note starts is the closer analogue. The lo-fi crunch people credit to the 303 on Donuts is mostly vinyl source material plus MPC3000 12-bit bus summing.

Preserve this caveat when citing the lesson elsewhere.

Slide s06 · concept

Chipmunk soul — Re-Pitch, not time-stretch

Chipmunk soul — Re-Pitch, not time-stretch

Just Blaze at RBMA (bib:rbma_just_blaze_lecture): “RZA started it in like 1996… we’ll get seven pieces of a record, put it back together, add a breakdown part and make the sample do the hook.”

“Chipmunk soul” is the course term. Receipt as above.

Ableton move — callback to W4 (bib:ableton_live_12_manual):

  • Drop vocal on audio track. Warp: Re-Pitch. Transpose +5 semitones (try +4 and +7).
  • Re-Pitch is varispeed — pitch and tempo stay coupled. Complex Pro decouples them and kills the character.
  • Chop into seven non-contiguous pieces — vowel one, consonant two, tail three. Reassemble into a new hook. “Seven pieces” is literal.
  • The sped-up artifact is the aesthetic. Do not smooth it.

A/B: same phrase at +5 Re-Pitch vs +5 Complex Pro. Complex Pro sounds correct and wrong. Re-Pitch sounds incorrect and right.

Slide s07 · concept

Kanye/Mike Dean — 6–12 vocal stack, MicroShift, retune-speed zero

Kanye/Mike Dean — 6–12 vocal stack, MicroShift, retune-speed zero

The MBDTF-era choral bed: six to twelve passes of the same phrase, detuned by ±5 and ±10 cents, slight micro-timing offsets, summed to one bed.

Recipe:

  • Duplicate the chopped-vocal track ×6. On each copy, nudge clip start ±5 to ±15 ms and detune ±5 or ±10 cents via clip Pitch Fine.
  • Bus to a group. Insert SoundToys MicroShift, Style II, Detune 100%, Delay 100% (bib:soundtoys_microshift_manual). Style III goes wider.
  • On the group: Auto-Tune, Retune Speed 0. Mike Dean’s “Heartbreak” trick on chopped fragments — locks every pitch to scale, talk-box lock over the detune smear.

Caveat: retune-speed-0 is technique-well-known, but we don’t have a direct Mike Dean on-record quote in the bib yet. Flagged in needs_bib:. Treat as technique-known-not-cited until upgraded.

Bounce the bed. Place under drums. Do not ride the fader.

Slide s08 · concept

Little AlterBoy — three settings worth memorizing

Little AlterBoy — three settings worth memorizing

SoundToys Little AlterBoy (bib:soundtoys_little_alterboy_manual). Three settings cover the lesson:

  1. Chipmunk soul double — Transpose, Pitch +7, Formant +7, linked. Parallel layer under the Re-Pitch main, −10 dB in the group. Doubled sped-up move without touching the master pitch.

  2. Robot lead from MIDI — Robot mode, MIDI input drives pitch, Drive 5. MIDI-controlled vocoder — instant talk-box lead off the chop. Fills the 1/32 holes in the Dilla pattern.

  3. Bass from vowels — Transpose, Formant −8, Pitch 0, Link off. Strips vocal into a low, textured drone. Low-pass at 200 Hz → sub-bass layer made of James Brown backing singers. Devine-style source.

One plugin, three knobs each. Memorize and stop shopping.

Slide s09 · concept

DJ Premier — scratch the loop, then sample yourself

DJ Premier — scratch the loop, then sample yourself

Premier on Ten Crack Commandments (bib:premier_ten_crack_scratches): “That loop is me sampling my scratching by hand. It was me experimenting, and it sounded dope.” Not a breakbeat, not a horn. A scratched performance triggered as a drum.

The method, verbs only:

  1. Perform a pass on a sound — turntable, MIDI retrigger, tape-stop, whatever has a hand on it.
  2. Bounce the pass.
  3. Load the bounce into a sampler and trigger as percussion — not melody, not loop.

Ableton for the Hot Pants vocal: record yourself dragging the clip-start warp handle live. Bounce that performance. Load into Simpler. Trigger from a drum pad under the kick.

Premier’s rig was MPC60 + Technics 1200s. Method is not gear-dependent.

The chopped-vocal signal path, committed

The chopped-vocal signal path, committed

The common path for all four methods this week, expressed in Ableton verbs (bib:ableton_live_12_manual):

  5. Commit rule — every time you print a pass, delete the unused source track. The ladder only works if you can’t go back.

One track in. One bounce out. One commit per pass.

Akai MPC3000 — the machine Dilla actually used

Akai MPC3000 — the machine Dilla actually used

The Akai MPC3000, 1994. Designed with Roger Linn. 16 velocity-sensitive pads, 16-bit 44.1 kHz sampling, SCSI, 760 KB base memory upgradable to 32 MB, 32-voice polyphony. The Smithsonian National Museum of African American History and Culture holds Dilla’s personal unit as the instrument of record (bib:smithsonian_dilla_mpc3000).

Forget the Donuts SP-303 myth — this is the machine that defined Dilla’s sound from Fantastic Vol. 2 through The Shining (bib:charnas_dilla_time). The SP-303/404 arrived late, after the record was already tracked.

What this deep-dive covers: the controls that matter for Dilla timeDilla time — Dan Charnas's term (Dilla Time, 2022) for J Dilla's rhythmic signature — the deliberate superimposition of straight, swung, and micro-shifted elements within the same bar. On the MPC3000, per-pad swing and timing shift make this programmable. Canonical recipe: kicks on straight 16ths, hats at ~60–66% swing, snares dragged 20–30 ms late. wikipedia↗, the per-pad swing mechanic, the 96 PPQN timing grid, and how to approximate it in Ableton without lying to yourself about what’s missing.

Annotated tour, 1/2 — pads, swing, note repeat

Annotated tour, 1/2 — pads, swing, note repeat

The four controls that matter for Dilla timeDilla time — Dan Charnas's term (Dilla Time, 2022) for J Dilla's rhythmic signature — the deliberate superimposition of straight, swung, and micro-shifted elements within the same bar. On the MPC3000, per-pad swing and timing shift make this programmable. Canonical recipe: kicks on straight 16ths, hats at ~60–66% swing, snares dragged 20–30 ms late. wikipedia↗:

  1. 16 velocity-sensitive pads — grouped in banks A–D. Each pad is one voice with its own sample, envelope, tuning, and — critical — its own timing parameters.
  2. Swing % — 50% is straight, 62–66% is triplet-heavy. On the MPC3000 this is set per pad, not globally (bib:akai_mpc3000_manual). That is the whole game.
  3. Note Repeat — hold a pad, tap Note Repeat, and the pad fires at the current subdivision. This is how hand-stepped hi-hat rolls happen without a piano roll.
  4. Timing Correct % — quantize strength, 0 to 100. Dilla set this to 0 on most pads and nudged by ear. On Fantastic Vol. 2 the snare is ~20 ms early, the hats are freehand, the kicks are ±20–30 ms off (bib:charnas_dilla_time).

The 96 PPQN grid is the resolution underneath everything. One 16th note = 24 ticks. One millisecond at 90 BPM ≈ 0.14 ticks. When you nudge by a “tick,” you are nudging by ~7 ms.

  1. 16 pads: Each pad is one voice with independent timing params.
  2. Swing %: Per-pad, not global. Hats at 62%, kicks at 50%, snare at 50%.
  3. Note Repeat: Hold pad + Note Repeat to fire at current subdivision. Hi-hat rolls without a piano roll.
  4. Timing Correct %: Quantize strength. Dilla set this to 0 on most pads and nudged by ear.

Annotated tour, 2/2 — sample edit and signal path

Annotated tour, 2/2 — sample edit and signal path

The sample-edit page is where the chop happens:

  • Truncate — crop head and tail. Destructive on the sample in RAM.
  • Loop points — set start/end loop in frames. Used for the sustained-vowel chipmunk-soul moves, not for the Dilla micro-chop.
  • Pitch (tune) — semitone + fine cents, implemented as varispeedvarispeed — Tape (or old-sampler) speed control that links pitch and duration. Double the speed = up an octave, half the length. The sound of chipmunk soul, of Squarepusher's half-speed bass trick, and of the Akai S950's crude pitch-shift. Re-Pitch warp in Ableton is the digital equivalent. wikipedia↗. Raising pitch shortens the sample; lowering lengthens it. Pitch and time are coupled. This is not a bug. This is the reason every Dilla chop sounds like Dilla.
  • Filter — 2-pole low-pass per voice. The “MPC low-pass” character on Slum Village records came from here, not from an outboard box (bib:akai_mpc3000_manual).

Signal path: pad trigger → sample RAM → varispeed pitch → envelope → LPF → assigned output → DAC → mix out. The DAC is a Burr-Brown PCM61P-K, 18-bit. The summing bus is where the MPC3000 gets its “weight” — a topic producers argue about and Akai never marketed.

Slide gd-mpc3k-04 · gear deep dive

How Dilla executed the technique

How Dilla executed the technique

The step-by-step, from Charnas 2022 (bib:charnas_dilla_time):

  1. Sample a 2–4s vocal or drum break. Assign to pads by chop.
  2. Set Timing Correct = OFF on the sequencer track.
  3. Hand-play the pattern. Do not quantize.
  4. Open the Step Edit view. Find each hit’s position in ticks.
  5. Nudge by ear. Snare: pull ~20 ms early (roughly 3 ticks at 90 BPM). Kicks: leave ±20–30 ms off-grid, direction inconsistent. Hats: do not touch.
  6. Set Swing % per pad. Hats 62–66%. Kicks 50%. Snare 50%.
  7. Play back. Listen. Nudge again.

Questlove’s line, cited by Charnas: “My mission to get accepted by the hip-hop nation was to sound synthetic like a drum machine, to sound like a sample. His approach was to sound as sloppy as a real musician. But it was so sloppy, I know he wasn’t doing it by accident” (bib:questlove_dilla_quote).

The sloppiness is engineered. That is the whole point.

Underlying algorithm — per-pad timing on a 96 PPQN grid

Underlying algorithm — per-pad timing on a 96 PPQN grid

The MPC3000 sequencer runs at 96 pulses per quarter note (PPQN). One 16th = 24 ticks. Timing shift per pad is expressed in ticks, stored as a signed offset in the pattern.

At 90 BPM: - 1 beat = 666.67 ms - 1 tick = ~6.94 ms - 1 ms ≈ 0.14 ticks

So the “20 ms early snare” in practice is a −3-tick offset on the snare pad, stored in the pattern’s per-pad timing-shift field (bib:akai_mpc3000_manual). It applies on playback, after any Timing Correct but before the voice is assigned a sample. Swing % applies on the off-subdivisions of the assigned rate — so “hats at 62% swing” shifts every even 16th by roughly 12% of a 16th, ~8 ticks, ~55 ms at 90 BPM.

Crucially: Swing and Timing Shift are independent per pad. Kicks at swing 50% + shift −3 ticks, hats at swing 64% + shift 0, snare at swing 50% + shift −3 ticks — all in the same pattern. The SP-1200 has one global swing parameter. That is the architectural delta.

Per-pad timing chain on the MPC3000. Timing shift and swing are set independently per pad.

Algorithm continued — varispeed playback and the DAC

Algorithm continued — varispeed playback and the DAC

Sample playback on the MPC3000 is varispeedvarispeed — Tape (or old-sampler) speed control that links pitch and duration. Double the speed = up an octave, half the length. The sound of chipmunk soul, of Squarepusher's half-speed bass trick, and of the Akai S950's crude pitch-shift. Re-Pitch warp in Ableton is the digital equivalent. wikipedia↗ — pitch and duration are coupled. Raise a pad’s pitch by +5 semitones and the sample plays 1.335× faster (ratio = 2^(5/12)). There is no time-stretching algorithm involved — this is literally “read the sample at a different rate.”

Why this is load-bearing for chipmunk soulchipmunk soul — A style of hip-hop built on pitched-up soul vocal samples — the Kanye West / Just Blaze / RZA lineage. The authentic artifact is varispeed (linked pitch/tempo via old-gear sampler), not time-stretch. In Ableton: Re-Pitch warp, +4 to +7 semitones. Time-stretch loses the character. wikipedia↗ (taught in s06): - Pitch up = faster = chipmunk artifact preserved. - Pitch down = slower = tape-slow dusty artifact preserved. - A phase-vocoder or Complex Pro would fix this coupling — and kill the character.

The 18-bit Burr-Brown PCM61P-K DAC and the summing bus give the MPC3000 a specific sound producers call “weight” or “punch.” It is not quantifiable in a spec sheet. Roads 1996 (bib:roads_computer_music_tutorial, Ch. 2) covers why coupled-pitch-time sample playback and simple-DAC summing have characteristic artifacts — intermodulation distortion in the bus, quantization noise floor, aperture error at the DAC — that together constitute “the sound of the box.”

Constraint note: 32 MB RAM ceiling bounded sample lengths and forced the chop-and-rearrange workflow. Memory pressure is compositional pressure.

Ableton bridge — Drum Rack + per-pad Groove Pool

Ableton bridge — Drum Rack + per-pad Groove Pool

The Ableton analogue is not one device. It is a discipline:

  1. Drum Rack — 16-pad grid. Each pad is a chain. Each chain gets its own Simpler in Classic mode (pitch/time coupled) to preserve varispeed character (bib:ableton_live_12_manual).
  2. Groove Pool, per pad — drag a different groove file onto each chain. Swing timing applies only to that chain’s output. Ableton’s Groove Pool is the closest native analogue to MPC3000 per-pad swing.
  3. Per-pad note nudge in the MIDI editor — select a pad’s notes, shift-arrow nudges by the project grid. For sub-grid nudges, turn off snap and drag.
  4. Warp mode = Re-Pitch on audio triggers — if you’re triggering samples from audio clips instead of Simpler, Re-Pitch preserves the varispeed coupling. Complex Pro breaks it.

Parameter table for the approximation:

MPC3000 Ableton equivalent
Per-pad swing % Groove file dragged onto chain
Per-pad timing shift (ticks) MIDI nudge, snap off
Varispeed pitch Simpler Classic mode / Warp Re-Pitch
2-pole LPF per voice Simpler filter, LPF 12 dB
18-bit DAC character Leave headroom; light analog-summing emulation optional

Max for Live parallels — Pad Swing, MPC Groove, Humanize

Max for Live parallels — Pad Swing, MPC Groove, Humanize

Third-party M4L devices that close the gap:

  • Pad Swing (various authors on maxforlive.com) — puts a swing-% knob on every chain of a Drum Rack. The closest one-to-one MPC3000 per-pad swing clone.
  • MPC Groove — a Max device that loads classic MPC groove templates (swing 54, 58, 62, 66%) as groove files. Hand-tuned against MPC hardware by the author. Verify the specific version on maxforlive.com; bib slot flagged in needs_bib:.
  • Humanize (native Live MIDI effect) — randomizes timing within ±N ms. Not ideal — Dilla’s drift is deliberate, not random. Useful for hats only, never for kicks or snares.
  • K-Devices TTAP or alkman Euclidean Pro (bib:alkman_euclidean_pro) — handle polyrhythmic accents if you want to replicate the MPC3000’s quintuplet/septuplet tricks.

The critical move: use these devices to set different values per chain. If every pad has the same swing %, you’ve built a TR-909 feel, not a Dilla feel.

Slide gd-mpc3k-10 · gear deep dive

Beyond Dilla — what else the MPC3000 does

Beyond Dilla — what else the MPC3000 does

The hardware’s architecture enables moves beyond per-pad swing:

  • Chromatic chopping — map one sample chunk across 16 pads in semitone steps. Play the pads as a melodic instrument. Dilla used this on “Runnin’” to extract a bassline from a pitched-up guitar sample (bib:charnas_dilla_time). In Ableton: Simpler Classic, map across keyboard range, ignore warp.
  • Layered kick stacking — three kicks on three pads, each with different pitch and filter. Trigger all three from one MIDI note via pad-link. On “Get Dis Money” the kick is a three-layer stack.
  • Resampling the internal bus — record the MPC’s own mix output back into a new pad. A resample ladder native to the hardware. Prefuse used this on the MPC2000XL (s03); the MPC3000 does the same trick with better summing.
  • 4-bar loop composition against a 7-bar sample — the MPC’s odd loop lengths let you offset bar-counts deliberately. Dilla’s 7-bar Herbie loop under a 4-bar kick pattern is the canonical case.

Exercise A — replicate Dilla swing, snare-only

Exercise A — replicate Dilla swing, snare-only

Goal: a 4-bar boom-bap pattern where the snare lags the grid by ~50 ms and the kicks stay straight. This is the signature Fantastic Vol. 2 move, reduced to its smallest verifiable case.

Steps:

  1. New Drum Rack. Load a kick sample on C1, a snare on D1, a closed hat on F#1.
  2. Program a basic 4-bar pattern: kicks on 1 and 3, snares on 2 and 4, hats on every 16th.
  3. Bounce this as the quantized baseline. Label hp_baseline.wav.
  4. Create a new groove file: Swing = 0%, Timing = +25% (at 90 BPM, +25% of a 16th ≈ +50 ms). Drag only onto the snare chain.
  5. Leave the kick and hat chains with no groove.
  6. Bounce as hp_dillaswing.wav.
  7. A/B the two bounces.

Deliverable: two mono bounces, plus one sentence explaining which one sounds “alive” and why. The 50-ms snare drag is the smallest deliberate-sloppiness move that still reads as Dilla (bib:charnas_dilla_time).

Exercise B — per-pad drift everywhere

Exercise B — per-pad drift everywhere

Goal: a pattern where every hit has a unique per-pad timing offset. Prove to yourself that random offsets sound like noise and deliberate ones sound like a record.

Steps:

  1. Program a 1-bar pattern with 4 kicks, 2 snares, 8 hats on a Drum Rack.
  2. On the kick chain, set MIDI offsets: kick 1 at 0 ms, kick 2 at +5 ms, kick 3 at −3 ms, kick 4 at +10 ms. These are pad-specific, not groove-based — nudge in the MIDI editor with snap off.
  3. On the snare chain: snare 1 at −3 ms (Dilla drag-early), snare 2 at −3 ms.
  4. On the hat chain: leave on the grid.
  5. Bounce as hp_drift.wav.
  6. Re-bounce the same pattern with all kicks at 0 ms. Label hp_nodrift.wav.
  7. A/B.

Reflect (one paragraph): which kick feels most wrong? Which feels most right? The drift is not random — Dilla chose each offset. You are choosing too.

Stretch: try all offsets in the same direction (all +5 to +15). Then all random-signed. The same-direction version reads as “late”; the random version reads as “bad.” That asymmetry is the hidden rule.

Two bounces of a 1-bar pattern (hp_drift.wav with kicks at 0/+5/-3/+10 ms offsets; hp_nodrift.wav with all kicks at 0) plus a paragraph comparing the random-signed drift to a same-direction drift and identifying which feels deliberate.

Diagram — Dilla swing visualized on the 96 PPQN grid

The tick-level reality: the 16th grid marks 24 ticks apart; the snare pad’s notes sit 3 ticks before the grid; the kick pad varies ±3 to ±4 ticks off-grid; the hats stay on swing-64% positions (every odd 16th pushed ~8 ticks late).

Close the diagram with the rule: different offsets per pad, all in the same 4-bar pattern. That is the single architectural fact that separates MPC3000 Dilla time from every SP-1200 record that came before.

At 90 BPM, the perceptual threshold for “late” is about 8–12 ms (roughly 1–2 ticks). Anything below that is “tight.” Anything above ~40 ms starts reading as a separate event. Dilla’s sweet spot: 15–30 ms off-grid per pad, direction varying.

E-mu SP-1200 — the grit machine

E-mu SP-1200 — the grit machine

The E-mu SP-1200, 1987. Designed by Dave Rossum. 12-bit companded PCM, 26.04 kHz sample rate, ~10 seconds of sample RAM divided into 2.5 seconds per sound across 8 pads (two banks of 4), 8-voice polyphony. No anti-alias filter on input. No anti-alias filter on output. That omission is the entire character.

This is the machine that defined a generation of hip-hop: Pete Rock on T.R.O.Y., RZA on Enter the Wu-Tang (36 Chambers), Premier on Illmatic, Slum Village on Fantastic Vol. 1. Dilla used one before the MPC3000 arrived. Kanye chased its sound through software for years.

What this deep-dive covers: the 2.5-second-per-pad constraint as composition force, the 12-bit companded ADC curve, the aliasing cliff at ~13 kHz, and the Ableton chain that approximates the grit without a $4,000 used hardware purchase.

Slide gd-sp1200-02 · gear deep dive

Annotated tour, 1/2 — sample rate, pitch, truncate

Annotated tour, 1/2 — sample rate, pitch, truncate

The controls that matter:

  1. Sample rate: fixed at 26.04 kHz. Not user-adjustable. Nyquist is ~13 kHz. Everything above that folds back as aliasing. Modern recordings at 44.1/48 kHz sound tame by comparison — this is the source of the “dusty” character.
  2. Pitch — semitone + fine tune. Per-pad varispeedvarispeed — Tape (or old-sampler) speed control that links pitch and duration. Double the speed = up an octave, half the length. The sound of chipmunk soul, of Squarepusher's half-speed bass trick, and of the Akai S950's crude pitch-shift. Re-Pitch warp in Ableton is the digital equivalent. wikipedia↗. Raising pitch shortens the sample and raises aliasing artifacts; lowering pitch lengthens it and moves the aliasing down into the mids. Pitch-and-time-coupled just like the MPC3000, but at lower bandwidth.
  3. Truncate — head/tail crop at sample-level resolution. Destructive. The 2.5-second pad limit is a RAM division, not a truncate setting — you cannot load a longer sample, period.
  4. The “aliasing switch” — producers’ slang, not an actual button. Refers to routing via the individual outputs rather than the stereo mix, which avoids the master bus DAC’s slight low-pass and preserves more high-frequency aliasing. Pete Rock and RZA ran out of the individual outs for exactly this reason.

2.5 seconds per pad is a compositional constraint. You cannot loop a whole phrase. You chop or you don’t sample it.

  1. SR 26.04 kHz: Not user-adjustable. Nyquist at ~13 kHz. Everything above folds back.
  2. Pitch (semi + fine): Per-pad varispeed. Pitch-and-time coupled.
  3. Truncate: Sample-level head/tail crop. 2.5 s per pad is a RAM division, not a setting.
  4. Individual outs: Skip the stereo summing bus. Preserves more high-freq aliasing — the 'aliasing switch'.

Slide gd-sp1200-03 · gear deep dive

Annotated tour, 2/2 — signal path and output routing

Annotated tour, 2/2 — signal path and output routing

Signal path, input side: line in → sample-and-hold at 26.04 kHz → 12-bit companded ADC → sample RAM. No anti-alias filter at the input. Any frequency in the source above 13 kHz folds back as aliasing and is printed into the sample. Resampling a hot 16 kHz cymbal returns a ~10 kHz ghost. That ghost is permanent.

Output side: pad trigger → sample RAM → varispeed playback → 12-bit DAC → individual output OR stereo mix bus → line out. The stereo mix bus has gentle summing coloration; the individual outputs skip it. Producers used the individuals for bass, mix bus for drums, or vice versa — personal taste.

No filters, no envelopes per voice. The MPC3000 has a 2-pole LPF per voice; the SP-1200 has nothing. Every shape comes from the sample itself — truncate, pitch, or re-sample.

That radical simplicity is why SP-1200 records sound the way they do. There is no character knob. The character is baked into the ADC curve and the sample rate.

How the RZA / Premier / Pete Rock method worked

How the RZA / Premier / Pete Rock method worked

The canonical SP-1200 sampling move:

  1. Pull record on Technics 1200. Cue to a 1–2-second break.
  2. Sample at 33 rpm or 45 rpm — 45 rpm shortens the sample by 1.35×, freeing pad RAM. Pitch down in the SP-1200 to recover the original key. The sample-at-45 trick is universal among SP-1200 producers (bib:charnas_dilla_time).
  3. Truncate to the break. The 2.5-second limit is hard. If you can’t fit it, you chop.
  4. Assign to a pad. Set pitch. Play.
  5. For the Premier method: sample your own scratching as a pad hit, trigger as percussion (bib:premier_ten_crack_scratches). Covered in s09.

Receipts: - Pete Rock: “I only sample stuff that gives me a good feel” — filtered full-mix stereo records through a studio EQ at Greene Street to extract basslines where no stem existed (bib:charnas_dilla_time via spec). “T.R.O.Y.” is two different sections of Tom Scott’s “Today” pitched to match, drums from James Brown “Say It Loud.” - RZA used the SP-1200 through “Bring the Pain” and “Method Man,” then moved to the Ensoniq EPS-16 Plus for longer loops (bib:rbma_rza_lecture). The 2.5-second limit is why he switched. - DJ Premier on Illmatic and Ten Crack Commandments — MPC60 plus Technics plus SP-1200 grit; the drums were often SP-processed, the basses not.

Slide gd-sp1200-05 · gear deep dive

Technique continued — the 2.5-second constraint as composition force

Technique continued — the 2.5-second constraint as composition force

The RAM division forced specific compositional choices that defined the era:

  • Chops not loops. You cannot load an 8-bar break at 26.04 kHz in 2.5 seconds; you load the first kick + snare + one hat and trigger each as separate pads. Wu-Tang’s “Method Man” and Nas’s “N.Y. State of Mind” drum programming is direct evidence.
  • Short samples, retriggered. The “stabby” feel of SP-1200-era hip-hop is a RAM-limit artifact: every hit is a short sample, not a sustain.
  • 45-rpm sampling to steal headroom. Playing records at 45 rpm halves sample duration. RZA and Pete Rock both did this constantly (bib:charnas_dilla_time).
  • Global swing only. Unlike the MPC3000’s per-pad swing, the SP-1200 had one swing value for the whole pattern. This is why Dilla timeDilla time — Dan Charnas's term (Dilla Time, 2022) for J Dilla's rhythmic signature — the deliberate superimposition of straight, swung, and micro-shifted elements within the same bar. On the MPC3000, per-pad swing and timing shift make this programmable. Canonical recipe: kicks on straight 16ths, hats at ~60–66% swing, snares dragged 20–30 ms late. wikipedia↗ is not an SP-1200 aesthetic — the architecture doesn’t support it.

The result: SP-1200 records sound tight, stabby, aliased, bandwidth-limited. MPC3000 records sound loose, wide, punchy. Same producers could make both; the machine chose the feel.

Underlying algorithm — 12-bit companded ADC

Underlying algorithm — 12-bit companded ADC

The SP-1200 uses a 12-bit companded PCM format. “Companded” means the amplitude range is non-linear — small values are finely resolved, large values are coarsely resolved. It’s closer to µ-law companding than to plain 12-bit linear PCM.

Why companding matters sonically: - Loud transients (snares, kick clicks) hit the coarse end of the curve → visible quantization noise at the attack. This is the “crunch” at the front of every SP-1200 snare. - Quiet content (reverb tails, vocal sustain) sits on the fine end → cleaner than a pure 12-bit linear would give. - The resulting quantization noise floor is signal-dependent, not constant. Linear 12-bit PCM at the same bit depth sounds noticeably different — flatter, less characterful.

Bit-depth math: 12 bits = 4096 levels, theoretical SNR ≈ 72 dB. But with companding, effective dynamic range is pushed into the usable range where content lives, at the cost of top-end detail. Roads 1996 covers companding curves in Ch. 1 (bib:roads_computer_music_tutorial).

Manufacturer documentation for the exact E-mu companding curve is thin — the SP-1200 service manual is the primary source, flagged in needs_bib:.

Companded ADC — dense quantization near zero, sparse near full-scale. The signature 'crunchy transient, clean tail' shape.

Algorithm continued — aliasing and the 13 kHz cliff

Algorithm continued — aliasing and the 13 kHz cliff

26.04 kHz sample rate → Nyquist = 13.02 kHz. Anything in the source above 13 kHz folds back into the audible band as aliasing:

  • A 16 kHz cymbal component folds to 26.04 − 16 = 10.04 kHz.
  • A 20 kHz source harmonic folds to 26.04 − 20 = 6.04 kHz. Now you have a mid-range tone that wasn’t in the original.
  • A properly designed ADC has a steep brick-wall anti-alias filter (~20 kHz for 44.1 kHz systems). The SP-1200’s filter is famously permissive — per the service manual there’s a gentle ~1-pole analog roll-off starting around 10 kHz, so it’s not absent, just lazy. Plenty of high-frequency content that would have been blocked elsewhere folds back into the audible band and gets printed directly into sample RAM.

Combined with varispeed playback: pitch a pad up an octave and the aliased content shifts with the sample, creating non-harmonic content that intermodulates with the source. This is why SP-1200 records sound “musically wrong in a way that works” — they contain frequency content that is not mathematically related to the source spectrum.

The frequency-response cliff is not at 13 kHz — it’s at 26.04 − source-highest. That is a moving target dependent on what you sampled. Which is why every SP-1200 record has its own particular grit.

Aliasing cliff at 13 kHz. Any source content above Nyquist folds back as an inverted-image ghost, added to the sample.

Ableton bridge — Redux + TAL-Bitcrusher + 13 kHz LPF

Ableton bridge — Redux + TAL-Bitcrusher + 13 kHz LPF

The closest in-the-box approximation of SP-1200 character is a three-device serial chain on Simpler in One-Shot mode:

  1. Redux (native Live sample-rate/bit-depth reducer) — set Sample Rate ≈ 13 kHz (or 13.02 kHz if the device permits fine setting) to simulate the Nyquist bandwidth limit. Bit depth 12. Redux’s default anti-alias setting is cleaner than the SP-1200 — for authenticity, turn anti-aliasing off or reduce it (bib:ableton_live_12_manual).
  2. TAL-Bitcrusher (bib:tal_bitcrusher) — set bit depth 12, add a slight non-linear drive curve to approximate companding asymmetry. Alternate: Decimort 2 (bib:decimort_2) has a specific “SP-1200” preset that matches the companding curve closely.
  3. EQ Eight, 12 dB/oct LPF at 13 kHz — enforces the bandwidth limit as a hard cliff. Some producers prefer 16 kHz LPF as more forgiving.
  4. Simpler in One-Shot mode, Warp Re-Pitch on the input sample — preserves varispeed coupling.

Parameter table:

SP-1200 Ableton chain
26.04 kHz sample rate (no AA filter) Redux SR 13 kHz, anti-alias off
12-bit companded PCM TAL-Bitcrusher 12-bit + drive OR Decimort 2 “SP-1200” preset
2.5 s per pad RAM Discipline — do not exceed (see Exercise B)
No per-voice filter Chain’s LPF is global, not per-voice — constraint preserved
Varispeed playback Simpler Classic / Warp Re-Pitch

This is the closest you get without a $4,000 hardware unit. It is not identical. It is close enough that a blind A/B requires careful listening on mids.

Slide gd-sp1200-09 · gear deep dive

Alternative paths — Decimort 2, Roar Bitgrunge, and the 'don't bother'

Alternative paths — Decimort 2, Roar Bitgrunge, and the “don’t bother”

Three other paths to the SP-1200 sound:

  • Decimort 2 (D16 Group, bib:decimort_2) — dedicated decimator with an SP-1200 preset that models the companded ADC curve. Tighter approximation than Redux + Bitcrusher, single plugin. Paid but cheap.
  • Roar’s Bitgrunge shape (native Live 12.1+, bib:ableton_live_12_manual) — one of Roar’s internal saturation models includes bit-crushing behavior. Not a 1:1 SP-1200 clone but useful for per-band dusty character.
  • The “don’t bother” path — accept that you’re not making an SP-1200 record and stop chasing the sound. Use the stem untreated. Some of the best post-SP-1200 producers (Madlib after Madvillainy, Flying Lotus, Knxwledge) stopped reaching for the grit and let the sample breathe. The chase itself becomes a limitation.

Rule of thumb: if the chain is taking longer than the beat, delete the chain. The SP-1200 made records fast because it had no knobs. The Ableton emulation has too many; that is its own trap.

Slide gd-sp1200-10 · gear deep dive

Beyond the boom-bap — creative SP-1200 moves

Beyond the boom-bap — creative SP-1200 moves

The machine’s architecture enables moves beyond chop-and-trigger:

  • Resample loops through the machine. Record a finished loop out, sample back in through the ADC, bounce. The double-ADC pass doubles the companding and aliasing character. Producers call this “running it through the SP” — a verb, not a box.
  • Abuse varispeed extremes. Pitch a sample down by two octaves — the sample plays 4× slower and all frequency content drops to 1/4 original. Aliasing at the top-end becomes audible in the mid-range. Madlib’s “boomsauce” bass lineage.
  • Individual-output tricks. Route each pad to a different output, process each externally, recombine. Pete Rock’s filtered-bass trick (Greene Street EQ) depended on this.
  • Sample the SP-1200 sampling a record. Chain two units. The spec debates whether this is myth or actually happened; either way, the conceptual move is about stacking the ADC coloration.
  • Use it as an effect, not an instrument. Run your modern DAW mix through a hardware SP-1200 and sample back in. The 2.5-second limit means you do this per-measure, not per-track. Kanye and Mike Dean reportedly used one this way during MBDTF sessions — flagged as unconfirmed in needs_bib:.

Modern parallels — who still sounds like this

Modern parallels — who still sounds like this

The SP-1200 ceased production in 1998, but the sound lives on because the aesthetic can be reproduced in software and because the constraint-based composition style is independent of the hardware:

  • Knxwledge, Mndsgn, Swarvy — LA beat-scene producers using Decimort 2 or Redux chains to hit the grit on modern records. The sample-rate-reduction and companded-bitcrush combo is the common core.
  • Madlib (post-Madvillainy) — moved to Boss SP-303 (lower-fi, different character) and software, still composing with the “short sample” discipline (bib:charnas_dilla_time via spec).
  • Pete Rock, Premier, and RZA (when they want the sound) — all three are on record saying they still reach for the hardware when the track demands it, because the Ableton chain is “close, not identical.” Flagged as technique-known-not-directly-cited in needs_bib:.
  • Producers who don’t chase it — Kaytranada, Flying Lotus (post-Until the Quiet Comes), The Alchemist on recent work. They use cleaner chains and trust modern fidelity.

The SP-1200 sound is now a choice, not a given. Which means using it is also a statement about taste, not just gear availability.

Exercise B — the 2.5-second constraint, self-imposed

Exercise B — the 2.5-second constraint, self-imposed

Goal: make a 4-bar beat where every sampled element is ≤ 2.5 seconds. Prove to yourself that the constraint shapes the beat, not the chain.

Steps:

  1. Fresh project. Open a Drum Rack. Nothing loaded yet.
  2. Pick 8 source samples — vocal chops, drum breaks, bass hits, whatever. Each must be trimmed to 2.5 seconds or less in the Simpler start/end markers. Longer source material has to be chopped, not looped.
  3. Program a 4-bar pattern using only these 8 samples.
  4. No stretched loops. No 8-bar phrases. If you can’t fit an idea in 2.5 seconds, you chop it or cut it.
  5. Bounce as hp_2p5sec.wav.
  6. For comparison: redo the same musical idea with no length limit. Bounce as hp_unlimited.wav.

Deliverable: two bounces, plus one paragraph naming the specific musical decisions the 2.5-second limit forced — usually: more chops, shorter phrases, more retriggers, more dense patterns. This constraint is what made Enter the Wu-Tang sound different from Illmatic sound different from Fantastic Vol. 1. Same producers, same records, same limit, different outcomes (bib:charnas_dilla_time).

Diagram — the aliasing cliff and the companding curve

Diagram — the aliasing cliff and the companding curve

Two diagrams, one slide, both load-bearing:

Top: frequency-response cliff at 13 kHz. A 26.04 kHz sample rate means content above 13.02 kHz folds back into the audible band. The diagram shows a source spectrum (0–20 kHz flat) and the SP-1200 output: flat to ~13 kHz, with a mirror-image aliased ghost from 13 to 0 kHz. Folded content is added to the original — there is no filter to remove it.

Bottom: companded ADC curve (µ-law-style). Horizontal axis = input amplitude (linear). Vertical axis = quantization levels used. A linear 12-bit ADC uses evenly spaced levels; a companded ADC uses dense levels near zero and sparse levels near full-scale. The asymmetry is the “crunchy transient, clean tail” signature.

Rule: aliasing is permanent, companding is permanent. Neither can be removed after sampling. That is why they define the sound. Source: Roads 1996, Ch. 1 on quantization and Ch. 3 on sampling theory (bib:roads_computer_music_tutorial).

The two artifacts together are a fingerprint. Together with varispeed pitch and a 2.5-second RAM division, they are the SP-1200.
