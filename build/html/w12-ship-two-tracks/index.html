Slide s02 · concept

LUFS targets — −14 for streaming, louder for the club

LUFS targets — −14 for streaming, louder for the club

LUFS — Loudness Units relative to Full Scale — is the unit every streaming platform normalizes to. ITU-R BS.1770 defines it; every LUFS meter obeys it. Cite the spec, not the forum.

Two targets worth naming.

  • −14 LUFS integrated — Spotify and Apple Music normalization targets. Source above this and the platform turns you down; source below and the platform leaves you alone (Apple) or applies positive gain with a limiter ceiling (Spotify). The community-flagged Spotify documentation is the receipt here; I could not resolve a first-party URL in the bibliography, so treat the number as industry consensus pending a cited page.
  • −9 to −11 LUFS integrated — club and loud-listening contexts. No normalization. The track is heard as-printed. If your record is for a DJ tool, you master louder and accept that streaming will pull it back.

You pick one target per track based on where it lives. Two tracks, two targets, or both to −14 and release a separate club-master if you actually have a club.

Do not master by eye. The peak meter is lying.

Slide s03 · concept

The metering pair — Youlean and SPAN, both free

The metering pair — Youlean and SPAN, both free

Two plugins, free, on the master. They are enough.

  • Youlean Loudness Meter 2 — integrated LUFS, short-term LUFS, true peak, loudness range (LRA), history graph. You insert it last on the master. Read integrated LUFS over a full playthrough, not over the first four bars. This tool’s free tier does the job — needs a first-party citation I have not yet entered in the bibliography; flagged.
  • Voxengo SPAN — spectrum analyzer, long-time averaging, L/R and M/S views. You use it to check sub energy, to catch the 300 Hz mud buildup, to confirm your 10 kHz air is not imaginary. Same caveat on the bib entry.

Insert order on the master: SPAN (analyzer, no effect on signal) → master EQ → master compressor (optional) → limiter → Youlean (measurement only).

The two plugins tell you two different truths. SPAN shows you what is there. Youlean shows you how loud it is. Neither tells you if it sounds good. That is still your job.

Slide s04 · concept

The A/B protocol — level-matched, three references

The A/B protocol — level-matched, three references

You picked up this protocol in Week 1. Same shape, tighter scope.

Pick two or three reference tracks from the same genre as the track you are mastering. Not your favorite tracks. Tracks whose mastering you want to sit next to on a playlist without embarrassment.

Drop them on adjacent audio tracks in Live. Insert a Utility on each and on your master bus. Nudge the Utility gain until every track reads the same integrated LUFS on Youlean over a 30-second loop. That is level-matching.

Now listen. Switch between tracks in 30-second chunks. Five axes, written down:

  • low-end energy
  • midrange weight
  • top-end air
  • stereo width at the chorus
  • transient snap on the snare or the kick

Your track is not trying to beat the references. It is trying to not embarrass itself next to them. The phrase is translation, not competition.

Commit to three A/B passes, max. Then bounce.

Slide s05 · concept

True peak ceiling — −1.0 dBTP, not zero

True peak ceiling — −1.0 dBTP, not zero

The one number that will bite you at upload: true peak, measured in dBTP.

Sample-peak metering shows you the loudest sample in your file. True peak metering shows you the loudest inter-sample reconstruction — the peak the DAC will produce when the file is played back through lossy codecs (AAC, Ogg Vorbis, MP3). Streaming platforms re-encode. Re-encoding raises intersample peaks. A file that hits 0 dBFS in Live can clip on Spotify’s AAC.

Fix: limit to −1.0 dBTP, not 0 dBFS.

  • FabFilter Pro-L 2 — ceiling set to −1.0 dBTP, true-peak oversampling enabled (default).
  • Brainworx bx_XL V2 — one option, not the only option. Useful if you want M/S limiting per band.
  • iZotope Ozone Maximizer — fine if you already own Ozone.
  • Live’s stock Limiter — also fine. Set ceiling to −1.0.

Youlean’s true peak readout is the check. If it reads anything above −1.0, lower your ceiling or lower your input. Re-bounce. Re-check.

This is the one place where “near enough” is actually not.

Slide s06 · concept

Headphones that translate — K371, HD600, monitor compare

Headphones that translate — K371, HD600, monitor compare

Your mastering job is not to make the track sound great on your headphones. It is to make the track sound acceptable on other people’s headphones and phone speakers and cars. That is translation.

Two headphone pairs worth owning. Neither is expensive.

  • AKG K371 — closed-back, studio reference. Frequency response close to the Harman target within ±3 dB from 40 Hz to 10 kHz per independent measurement (Crinacle / oratory1990 sources — I could not resolve a canonical bib URL for the measurement file; flagged). Good low-end translation. Isolates. Ugly cable.
  • Sennheiser HD600 — open-back, thirty-year-old studio workhorse. Honest midrange. Weak sub below 50 Hz, so do not trust it for kick fundamentals. Pair with the K371 or with a single subwoofer check in a car.

Protocol: mix on your main monitors, A/B on K371 headphones, spot-check on a phone speaker. Flag any mix where the kick disappears on one and booms on another.

Your reference tracks go through the same three check-points. If theirs translate and yours does not, the difference is your master, not the room.

Slide s07 · caveat

Self-master or send it out — the Fridmann test

Self-master or send it out — the Fridmann test

The decision is not about skill. It is about finishing.

A line circulates in production forums, attributed to Dave Fridmann: “if it’s not finished, it’s not mastered.” I could not resolve the verbatim source in either the Handbook or the Compendium — treat the wording as community paraphrase that aligns with Fridmann’s public philosophy, flagged in needs_bib:. The meaning holds either way.

The point: mastering is not a process that rescues an unfinished mix. It is a translation pass applied to a mix that is already decided. If the low end is not committed, a mastering engineer cannot fix it. If the arrangement has four options and you have not chosen, no amount of Pro-L 2 will choose for you.

The test is one question, honest: do you know what the track is supposed to sound like? If yes, self-master this week. Use the tools above. Translate. Move on.

If no — send it to an engineer after you finish the mix. Not before. Not instead of. An engineer costs money. An unfinished mix costs more because it will not finish at their desk either.

For the twelve-week course, self-master. Next year, decide per track.

Slide s08 · concept

Delivery — Bandcamp first, distributor later

Delivery — Bandcamp first, distributor later

Three paths. Pick one for this week. You can add the others later.

  • Bandcamp — direct upload, direct sale, direct download. 10–15% revenue cut. Artist page acts as landing page. Allows “private” releases (unlisted, share-link only) which is exactly the shape of this week’s exercise. WAV/FLAC/MP3 all accepted.   • SoundCloud — preview-friendly, single-track ecology, weaker paid-download story. Good for a pre-release or a “free track” drop. Poor for an EP.
  • Distributor — DistroKid, TuneCore, AWAL, CD Baby. They push your track to Spotify, Apple Music, Tidal, every major DSP. Annual fee (DistroKid) or per-release fee (TuneCore). They allocate ISRCs automatically. Use this when you have at least four tracks and you want DSP presence.

For this week’s ship exercise: Bandcamp private EP. No DSPs. No distributor fee. The friction is almost zero and the audience is one person.

Next year, if you have four EP-candidates from the curation exercise, route through DistroKid. For now, Bandcamp.

Slide s09 · concept

Metadata hygiene — ISRC, credits, artwork, file naming

Metadata hygiene — ISRC, credits, artwork, file naming

The boring slide. Boring wins.

  • ISRC — International Standard Recording Code. Twelve characters, allocated per recording. Distributors allocate automatically. If you are Bandcamp-only, you can skip this initial release and add ISRCs when you move to a DSP distributor. The RIAA US ISRC agency page is the canonical reference; flagged as needs_bib:.
  • Composer credits — your legal name or a consistent pseudonym. This is what registers with PROs (ASCAP, BMI, SOCAN). Inconsistent credits across twelve releases means inconsistent royalty collection. Pick one. Write it the same way every time.
  • Genre tags — Bandcamp accepts multiple. “IDM” and “Electronic” plus one sub-tag (“glitch,” “ambient,” “breakbeat”) is enough. Searchability comes from accuracy, not from stuffing.
  • Artwork dimensions — 3000 × 3000 px, JPEG or PNG, under 10 MB. This is the DSP-common minimum. Lower resolution will upload; it will look worse on every platform.

File naming convention worth committing to: artist_trackname_v01_mixprint.wav, then v02, v03. When you export a new master, version it and leave the old one. Do not overwrite. Do not use emoji. Do not use exclamation marks in track titles; some distributors silently strip them. Lowercase is the safer default.

Slide s10 · concept

Voice is the consistency, not the uniqueness

Voice is the consistency, not the uniqueness

Twelve weeks in, you have twelve pieces. Listen to them back-to-back. Some will sound like they were made by twelve different producers. That is the point of the course, and also the problem you now have.

Voice is not the one idea that is unique to one track. Voice is what recurs across tracks.

  • Your kick-to-the-root move from Week 5, used again in Week 7, then again in Week 10 — that is voice.
  • Your tendency to leave 2 bars of silence before the drop, Autechre-style from Week 8 — if you did it twice, that is voice.
  • The Fridmann-bass-distortion setting you discovered in Week 4 and reached for every time the low end felt thin — that is voice.

What is not voice: the Week 9 vocal-chop experiment you never did again. The Week 2 subtraction exercise that produced a track you cannot listen to. The Week 11 beat-switch that only worked because the stem happened to cooperate.

Voice is what you would do without thinking, if you had to make a record in two days. The twelve-week archive tells you what those defaults actually are, not what you told yourself they were.

That is the curation exercise.

Slide mast-01 · concept

LUFS explained — ITU BS.1770-4, K-weighting, gating

LUFS explained — what the meter is actually measuring

LUFSLUFS — Loudness Units relative to Full Scale — the EBU R128 / ITU-R BS.1770 standard for perceived loudness, measured over time with K-weighting. Streaming platforms target roughly -14 LUFS integrated (Spotify, Apple Music); mastering for vinyl or club -8 to -10. Replaced RMS as the honest loudness metric. wikipedia↗ is not a volume knob. It is a perceptual average computed by ITU-R BS.1770-4 — the spec every streaming platform normalizes against.

Three moving parts in the algorithm.

  • K-weighting filter — a high-shelf and high-pass pre-filter that approximates equal-loudness at moderate listening levels. Low bass and sub-bass count less than the 2–4 kHz presence region. The meter hears the way an ear at 83 dB SPL hears.
  • Gating — the measurement drops any 400 ms block below −70 LUFS absolute, then re-gates relative to the running mean at −10 LU. Silence and fade-ins do not pull the number down.
  • Integration — the reading you ship is the integrated LUFS, measured across the whole track. Short-term LUFS (3-second window) and momentary LUFS (400 ms) are diagnostic readouts for the loudest section, not the delivery number.

The practical consequence: a track with a quiet intro and a loud drop reads closer to the drop’s loudness than to the average, because the gate is throwing out the intro below −70 LUFS.

Master to the integrated number. Watch the short-term for chorus-level danger. Ignore the momentary unless you are chasing a limiter spike.

Slide mast-02 · concept

The loudness scale — −14 LUFS, −9 LUFS, −1 dBTP on one diagram

The loudness scale — where −14, −9, and −1 actually sit

Three numbers define the entire mastering deliverable. On the same scale they look like this.

Everything above the streaming target gets turned down on playback. Everything below it is either left alone (Apple Music) or boosted with a platform-side limiter you do not control (Spotify). The club-master sits louder because nothing is normalizing it. The true-peak ceiling is a codec-safety number, not a loudness number — it lives on a different axis entirely (dBTP, not LUFS) but it is the third clamp every master respects.

Do not confuse the two scales. LUFS is perceptual-loudness. dBTP is reconstruction-peak. They read different numbers because they measure different things.

Slide mast-03 · concept

True peak vs sample peak — why −1.0 dBTP, not 0 dBFS

True peak vs sample peak — the four-times oversampling argument

The sample-peak meter lies by omission.

A digital file is a sequence of samples at 44.1 or 48 kHz. The analog waveform the DAC reconstructs between those samples can — and often does — rise above the highest sample value. That is an intersample peak. On a lossless playback chain it is mostly benign. On an AAC, Ogg Vorbis, or MP3 re-encode, the lossy decoder rebuilds the analog wave and clips against the codec’s internal ceiling. The file that measured 0.0 dBFS in Live now shows audible distortion on Spotify.

True-peak metering oversamples the signal by 4× (BS.1770-4 minimum) and reports the interpolated peak — the peak the DAC would actually produce. That is the number to clamp.

  • −1.0 dBTP — the industry-standard safety ceiling for streaming delivery
  • −2.0 dBTP — cautious when the source is already hot; Apple’s own delivery spec recommends −1.0 but some mastering houses use −2 for headroom
  • 0.0 dBFS — ceiling that will clip on at least one major platform

FabFilter Pro-L 2 enables true-peak oversampling at 4× by default; it can be pushed to 16× for paranoid masters at a small CPU cost. Youlean’s true-peak readout is the sanity check after bounce. If it reads worse than −1.0, lower the limiter ceiling and re-bounce. No exceptions.

Slide mast-04 · concept

The mastering chain — signal order, six stages

The mastering chain — signal order that survives contact with reality

Six stages, in this order, on the master bus. Deviate and you will regret it.

  1. Pre-master check — mix bounce imported on a new Live set, one stereo track, no plugins. Reference tracks on parallel tracks, Utility-gain-matched to the same integrated LUFS. This is the starting point. If the mix is broken here, mastering will not save it.
  2. Master EQ — broad strokes only. Low-shelf below 30 Hz if there is sub-mud. High-shelf at 12 kHz if the top is dull. No narrow cuts. If you need surgery, the mix is not finished.
  3. Master compression (optional) — 1.5:1 to 2:1 ratio, slow attack, auto release, 1–2 dB gain reduction max. Glue, not squash. Skip this if the mix already feels cohesive.
  4. Limiter — Pro-L 2, stock Limiter, Ozone Maximizer, bx_XL. Ceiling at −1.0 dBTP. Threshold pulled down until integrated LUFS hits the target.
  5. Metering — Youlean (LUFS + TP) and SPAN (spectrum) at the end of the chain. Measurement only, zero effect on signal.
  6. Export — 24-bit / 44.1 kHz WAV. Dither is automatic in Live on 16-bit export; skip for 24-bit.

The order matters because EQ before limiter changes what the limiter is limiting. Limiter before EQ means the EQ is re-lifting content you already clamped.

graph LR A[Mix bounce] --> B[Pre-master check + references] B --> C[Master EQ] C --> D[Master comp optional] D --> E[Limiter -1.0 dBTP] E --> F[Youlean + SPAN metering] F --> G[Export 24-bit / 44.1]
Fixed order. EQ before limiter. Metering after limiter. Export last.

Slide mast-05 · concept

A/B level-matched — close your eyes, then pick

A/B level-matched — close your eyes, then pick

Level-matching is the whole trick. A louder track always sounds “better.” Your ear is not a meter; it is a prejudice engine. Fletcher-Munson bends toward the louder source.

The protocol, tighter than slide s04.

  1. Bounce the mix. Drop it on track 1 in a fresh Live set.
  2. Drop two or three reference masters on tracks 2 and 3.
  3. Insert a Utility on each. Nudge gain on everything except the loudest until Youlean’s integrated reads the same number on all of them over a 30-second loop.
  4. Close your eyes. Solo each track in random order. Write down which one sounds best as a thing, not which one sounds loudest.
  5. If your mix wins — ship it. If a reference wins — the gap you heard is the translation you are trying to reach. Name the gap in words: “theirs has more 2 kHz snap,” “theirs has tighter sub,” etc. Then go back and address the named gap, not a vague feeling.

Three A/B passes, maximum. Each pass longer than 30 seconds is not listening, it is second-guessing.

Ear-fatigue rule: if you have been mastering for two hours, your decisions are worthless. Save. Close. Come back tomorrow. The protocol does not work when the instrument — your ear — is tired.

Slide mast-06 · concept

Translation matrix — four playback chains, four kinds of lying

Translation matrix — K371, HD600, monitors, car

The master has to work on four listening chains minimum. None of them are the chain you mastered on.

System Strengths Lies
Main monitors (NS-10, Adam, Kali, whatever) Stereo field, transient accuracy Bass is room-dependent; your room is not neutral
AKG K371 (closed) Bass translation, isolation Upper-mid slightly recessed vs Harman target
Sennheiser HD600 (open) Midrange honesty, vocals Sub below 50 Hz is absent; do not trust it
Phone speaker Real-world midrange balance Below 200 Hz is gone
Car Bass translation reality-check Cabin adds 6–8 dB of sub resonance

Protocol: mix on monitors, spot-check on K371, listen on HD600 if vocals are present, bounce to phone, take it to the car on the next trip. If the bass disappears on the phone but booms in the car, the mix has a sub-bass hole around 50–80 Hz — the fundamentals are missing and only the overtones are there. Phones reproduce overtones; cars reproduce fundamentals plus resonance.

Frequency-response measurements for the K371 and HD600 come from Crinacle and oratory1990 independent data; I could not resolve a single canonical URL at lint time, so treat the numeric specs as community-measured rather than manufacturer-published. The shape of each headphone’s bias is stable across measurers.

Each chain's bias is known. Use them in combination, not in isolation.

Slide mast-07 · caveat

Self-master vs send to engineer — the decision tree

When to master vs send to an engineer — a blunt decision tree

The honest question is not “am I good enough?” It is “is this track finished?”

Send it out when:

  • You have a label release with a distributor timeline and actual money moving
  • The mix is fully committed — bounces, arrangement, mutes, automation, done — and you want a second pair of ears to translate it
  • You have four or more tracks for the same release and need loudness consistency across an EP
  • The budget is real — $75–$300 per track for an independent engineer, $500+ for a mid-tier house, more for a name

Self-master when:

  • You are still exploring — the mix might change
  • The release is a Bandcamp private EP to one friend (this week)
  • The deadline is shorter than the turnaround of any engineer you would actually hire
  • You are twelve weeks into a practice and the learning is the point

An engineer cannot rescue an unfinished mix. A $300 pass on an unfinished track produces a louder version of the same unfinished track, shipped with your name on it.

For this week: self-master. Year Two: decide per release. The decision is situational, not skill-based.

Slide mast-08 · concept

Reading Youlean + SPAN — the five-second glance

Youlean + SPAN on the master — reading the screens

The two screens tell you two truths. Learn to read both in five seconds.

Youlean (measurement). Focus on three numbers during playback.

  • Integrated LUFS — the bottom-line delivery number. Reads across the whole track once play has looped through start to finish. This is what you match to target.
  • Short-term LUFS (3s window) — shows chorus or drop loudness. If this spikes more than 2–3 LU above integrated, the section is fighting the limiter.
  • True Peak (dBTP) — must read no worse than −1.0 on any playback. If it hits −0.9, lower the limiter ceiling.

SPAN (diagnostic). Focus on three regions.

  • 30–60 Hz — sub energy. If this is a flat shelf you probably have kick and sub stacked; if it is spiky, one element is dominating.
  • 200–400 Hz — the mud band. Too much here and the mix sounds boxy on any speaker that can reproduce it.
  • 8–12 kHz — air. Too little and the mix sounds closed; too much and cymbals ice-pick on headphones.

Set SPAN to long-time averaging (the default is short; change it). Average over a full playthrough, not four bars.

Neither tool tells you if the mix is good. That is why you also A/B against references. Meters confirm; ears decide.

Slide rel-02 · concept

SoundCloud vs DistroKid vs TuneCore — route by the job

SoundCloud vs DistroKid vs TuneCore — routing the track

Three different jobs. Pick by the job, not by the hype.

  • SoundCloud — broadcast platform, one-track ecology. Free tier accepts uploads; Pro tier removes the time cap and adds monetization. Best for: a preview, a free download, a single as promotion. Worst for: an EP. The platform does not sequence well and the download-gate friction is high. Useful as a secondary mirror, not a primary release.
  • DistroKid — subscription distributor. ~$23/year for unlimited uploads. Pushes to Spotify, Apple, Tidal, Amazon, ~150 DSPs. Allocates ISRCs automatically (per recording). You keep 100% of royalties minus per-stream micro-fees. Best for: volume releases, EP and album-length drops, artists who ship more than one record per year.
  • TuneCore — per-release distributor. ~$10–30 per single, more per album. Pushes to the same DSPs. You keep 100% of royalties. Best for: low-volume artists who release one thing a year and do not want an annual subscription.
  • AWAL / CD Baby — alternatives with different fee structures (CD Baby takes a percentage of royalties; AWAL is invite-only). Ignore for this week.

Routing logic: Bandcamp for this week’s private exercise. DistroKid when you have four tracks you actually stand behind. TuneCore if you only ever release one thing. SoundCloud if you need a public preview URL before any of the above.

ISRC agency for US artists is RIAA; DistroKid allocates under that agency automatically when you upload. Flagged in needs_bib: for a direct RIAA-ISRC URL.

Slide rel-03 · concept

File versioning — `v01_mixprint` saves you in eighteen months

File versioning — why v01_mixprint saves you in eighteen months

Future-you is a different person with worse memory than you think. Name files for that stranger.

The convention that survives.

artist_trackname_v01_mixprint.wav       # first full mix bounce
artist_trackname_v02_mixprint.wav       # revision
artist_trackname_v07_mixprint.wav       # final mix, committed
artist_trackname_v07_master.wav         # mastered from v07 mix
artist_trackname_v07_master_club.wav    # louder club variant
artist_trackname_v07_stems.zip          # stems bounced at same point

Rules that read paranoid and are not.

  • Never overwrite. Bump the version. Disk is cheap.
  • Never rename the project file. Duplicate it, bump the version, keep the old. Live’s undo history dies on close.
  • The master is tied to a mix version. If you re-bounce the mix, you re-master. Do not apply the old master chain to a new mix bounce and call it the same track.
  • Stems and masters share a version number. v07_master was made from v07_mixprint from v07.als. Breaking that chain is how tracks lose their own lineage.
  • Final is not a word. The file called trackname_final.wav is always wrong. So is final_FINAL, really_final, and final_v2. Numbers do not lie.

In eighteen months, when a label asks for the stems for a remix, you will find the session by version number and bounce from a known-good state. Without the convention you will open three projects that all look like the track and none of them are.

Metadata hygiene — composer, BPM, key, ISRC, the boring list

Metadata hygiene — composer credits, BPM, key, ISRC

The metadata fields are boring. Shipping without them is boring-and-expensive.

  • Composer credits — your legal name or a single consistent pseudonym, spelled the same way on every release. This is what the PROs (ASCAP, BMI, SOCAN, PRS) register and what pays out performance royalties. “Zak Brown” on one release and “Zak R. Brown” on another registers as two rights-holders and collects half the royalties each. Pick one form. Commit.
  • Performer credits — typically same as composer for solo-producer releases. If someone else played on a track, name them in the performer field, not in the track title.
  • BPM — integer, honest. The DAW-reported tempo if the track is a single tempo. If the track has a tempo change, pick the dominant tempo or the final tempo; do not invent an average.
  • Key — use Camelot notation (1A, 2B, etc.) if you are releasing to DJ-focused platforms; use musical notation (A minor, F# major) for Bandcamp and general catalogs. If the track is not in a single key, leave it blank.
  • ISRC — 12 characters, agency-allocated. For Bandcamp-only, you can skip it initially and add it at DSP-distributor time. DistroKid auto-allocates; TuneCore does the same. Self-allocation via the RIAA US ISRC agency is available but only worth it if you release a lot. Flagged in needs_bib:.
  • Genre — “IDM” plus “Electronic” plus one sub-tag is enough. Over-tagging does not help discovery. Under-tagging does hurt it.

The fifteen minutes you spend filling these fields accurately before release saves the three months of royalty chasing later.

Slide voice-01 · exercise

Listen to all twelve, back-to-back, once

Listen to all twelve, back-to-back, once

The exercise you will not want to do. Do it anyway.

Open a Live set. Drop every bounce from Weeks 1 through 11 onto the timeline in order, as audio clips. Utility-gain-match them to the same integrated LUFS (Youlean on the master, same protocol as slide mast-05). Set the timeline to continuous playback with no gap. Put on headphones. Play from the top.

Do not skip. Do not edit. Do not reach for the project file. Listen as if it were somebody else’s record.

You will hear four things.

  1. Tracks that sound nothing like you. These are experiments that did their job — the experiment was the deliverable.
  2. Tracks that sound like one specific reference artist. These are study tracks. Useful for technique, not for voice.
  3. Tracks that sound like the same person made them, even though they were made in different weeks with different techniques. These are the voice tracks.
  4. One or two tracks that surprise you — that you do not remember making, that sound better than you thought. File those away. The surprise is information.

The first pass is pure listening. No notes. No Live-set open on the second monitor. Pen and paper only, and only after the last track fades.

Voice is what survives the playthrough. Everything else is weather.

Slide voice-02 · exercise

Curate four, re-master as a cohesive set

Curate four, re-master as a set — the cohesion pass

You have circled four tracks from the curation exercise. Now treat them as an EP, not as four singles.

The cohesion pass is about relative loudness, tone, and space — not absolute numbers per track. Four tracks that each hit −14 LUFS on their own will still sound mismatched if one is compressed to death and one has 14 LU of dynamic range. Fix that before it ships.

Protocol for the four-track pass.

  1. Bounce the un-mastered mix of each of the four tracks. Drop all four on adjacent Live tracks.
  2. Level-match all four to the same integrated LUFS with Utility (mast-05 protocol).
  3. Play them back-to-back, 30 seconds each, in the proposed EP order. Write down what is missing on each track relative to the others — not what is wrong in isolation.
  4. Common gaps: one track has less sub-energy, one has more top-end, one has a noticeably different stereo width. These are the translation targets for the re-master.
  5. Re-master each track individually with the same master chain (mast-04) but with the cohesion gaps in mind. If track 2 sounded thin next to track 3 in the cohesion pass, track 2’s re-master adds low-shelf weight before the limiter, not after.
  6. Second A/B pass against all four re-masters. Write down whether the gaps closed.

This week you are only shipping two tracks. The four-track cohesion exercise is for Year Two. But you run the cohesion pass now so the two you pick are the ones that survive it best.

Voice is audible across the four. If it is not, pick different four.

Slide bernd-01 · teaser

Bernd says — the classical mastering mind

Bernd says — the classical mastering mind

Before the outro lands, a detour worth the minute.

Bernd Gottinger runs the mastering side of SUNY Fredonia’s Sound Recording Technology program. His day-job credits include engineering GRAMMY-nominated recordings of the Buffalo Philharmonic — Danielpour, Foss, Kodály. Orchestral sessions, not electronic music.

The reason to name him in a lesson about shipping two IDM tracks is not gear overlap. It is ethos overlap.

An orchestra plays the piece once, twice, maybe three times if the budget allows. Nobody multitracks ninety musicians and fixes things in the edit. The mastering engineer’s job is not to rescue a performance — it is to hear the performance more carefully than anyone else in the room. Bernd listens to a take and hears the oboe two rows back that sat slightly flat on bar 47. He hears the air-conditioning hum at 60 Hz that needs a notch. He hears the second-chair violinist’s chair creak.

That ethos — listen harder than anyone else, then translate — transfers directly to self-mastering your own tracks. You are both the performer and the listener. The performer is done. The listener has one job left.

This is not a pitch for SRT the program. It is a pitch for the posture.

Slide bernd-02 · teaser

Listen harder than anyone in the room

Listen harder than anyone in the room — applied to your two tracks

The orchestral-mastering frame, translated to what you are doing this week.

An orchestra recording is a single take with ninety instruments. Your track is a single bounce with forty tracks folded into two channels. The engineer’s posture is the same.

  • The recording is done. You cannot re-track the oboe. You cannot re-record the snare. Whatever is in the mix is what the master translates. If you are still reaching for the mixer, you are not mastering — you are mixing.
  • Listen for what the performer cannot hear. The producer-self heard the track a thousand times while building it. The mastering-self has to hear it for the first time, as a stranger. That is why you walk away before mastering. That is why you use level-matched A/B — so you hear the track and not the memory of it.
  • Every decision is a translation. Bernd’s EQ choices on a Buffalo Philharmonic record are not about making the orchestra louder. They are about translating the hall’s acoustics into a domestic playback chain. Your limiter is doing the same job. The goal is not “louder.” The goal is recognizable at the other end of the cable.
  • Stop before you ruin it. Classical mastering budgets time per minute of music because over-EQ’d classical records are audibly bad in a way over-compressed pop records mask. Three passes. Two plugins. Commit.

Apply this posture for the next 48 hours while you master your two tracks. Then upload. Then close the laptop.
