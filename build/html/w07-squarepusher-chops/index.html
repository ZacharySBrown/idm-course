Jenkinson does not want you watching the screen

The thing about Squarepusher is he does not like you — or anyone — watching the screen while he works. That is the whole plot.

Tom Jenkinson, to Vijith Assar in Tape Op #89 (2012): “When the graphical information is too vivid, it makes it harder to retain the information in my memory.” He sequences on a Yamaha QY700 — a 1997 hardware sequencer with a tiny LCD and numeric step entry — for exactly this reason. The screen is the enemy of the signal-path mental model.

This week we steal from that posture. Not the QY700 itself — we will simulate it inside Ableton with discipline — but the refusal. Single-pass. No undo. Chop the Amen. Commit what you get.

Slide s02 · concept

The QY700 as brain — blind numeric sequencing

The QY700 as brain

The Yamaha QY700 (1997) is a six-track, sixteen-pattern hardware step sequencer with a 320×240 monochrome LCD. Jenkinson uses it as the central brain of the Go Plastic rig.

Jenkinson’s operating principle, verbatim from Tape Op #89: “In order to really work fluently in a studio you need to have a model, or virtual image, of all those pathways in your head. If I’m running bits of data through it, I run it through the same pathway in my mental image, and then I know exactly what the results are going to be.”

Two consequences for us:

  • Program like a player. The hardware sequencer is a musical instrument, not a step-editor. You perform into it. You do not sculpt after the fact.
  • Numeric entry trumps piano-roll prettiness. Blind numeric is a constraint on second-guessing. The low-res screen is the feature.

Ableton mirror: Capture MIDI into a 16-bar clip. Do not open the piano roll to clean up. Commit.

Yamaha QY700 — the no-computer sequencer

Yamaha QY700 — the no-computer sequencer

Tom Jenkinson’s brain on Go Plastic is a thirteen-pound grey plastic box with a 320×240 monochrome LCD and a numeric keypad. That is the Yamaha QY700, released 1996.

Sixteen-part multitimbral AWM tone generator, 32-track sequencer, 480 PPQN resolution, 48-voice polyphony, step-entry-first interface. Four MIDI outs. No monitor. No mouse.

Jenkinson, Tape Op #89: “In order to really work fluently in a studio you need to have a model, or virtual image, of all those pathways in your head.” The QY700 forces that. You cannot see the signal path — you have to carry it.

The rest of this module is an annotated tour.

Slide gd-qy700-02 · gear deep dive

Annotated tour (1/2) — the step grid

Annotated tour (1/2) — the step grid

The QY700’s front panel is organized around the step sequencer grid. Each track is a row; each column is a 1/16 (or finer, up to 1/96 at 480 PPQN). Jenkinson enters notes by typing pitch + velocity + gate-time as numbers.

Four controls that matter for the technique:

  • Step grid. One line per track, up to 32 tracks. No piano-roll view. You read pitches as hex/decimal numbers in columns.
  • Velocity editor. Per-step velocity curves. This is where Jenkinson “humanized” patterns — manually, step by step, not via groove template.
  • Pattern chain. Up to 16 patterns chained into a song. Each pattern is a loop; the chain is the arrangement.
  • Part select 1–16. Sixteen AWM voices, each on its own MIDI channel, addressable from any track.

You can see why the screen was the enemy. The grid is the score.

  1. step grid: One line per track. Pitches entered numerically, not via piano roll.
  2. velocity ed: Per-step velocity curves — hand-drawn, not groove-template.
  3. pattern chain: Up to 16 patterns chained into the arrangement.
  4. part 1-16: 16 AWM voices, each on its own MIDI channel.

Slide gd-qy700-03 · gear deep dive

Annotated tour (2/2) — MIDI I/O and the chain

Annotated tour (2/2) — MIDI I/O and the chain

The back panel is why the QY700 ran the rig and not just a part of it.

  • MIDI Out A / Out B. Two independent 16-channel MIDI buses. Jenkinson used Out A to drive the internal AWM voices plus external FM synths; Out B went to samplers (S950, later S6000).
  • MIDI In. For slaving additional controllers — though Jenkinson programmed step-first, not play-in.
  • Sync Out / Tape Sync. Used to clock the DR-660 and tape transport without a DAW.
  • Stereo L/R Out. The internal AWM voices sum out here. Every external synth had its own line into the Mackie 16-channel desk.

Thirty-two sequencer tracks routed across two 16-channel MIDI buses driving up to a dozen external boxes. The QY700 is a patchbay with a clock.

  1. MIDI Out A: Ch 1-16 — internal AWM + external FM synths (TX81Z, FS1R).
  2. MIDI Out B: Ch 1-16 — sampler bus (S950, later S6000).
  3. sync out: 24 PPQ clock to DR-660 and tape transport.

How Jenkinson tracked Go Plastic on it

How Jenkinson tracked Go Plastic on it

Step-by-step, reconstructed from Tape Op #89.

  1. Patterns, not songs first. Each pattern is a 2- or 4-bar loop programmed into the step grid. Jenkinson builds ten to twenty patterns before chaining any of them.
  2. No real-time play-in. Numeric entry only. The notes go in as values, not as performance data. The blind-numeric entry is the anti-second-guess tool.
  3. Per-step velocity curves. Once pitches are in place, he walks the velocity editor and hand-draws per-step dynamics. This is the “program like a player” layer.
  4. Pattern chain = arrangement. Patterns are chained into a song. Rearrangement is a chain edit, not a piano-roll move.
  5. External voices assigned per track. TX81Z on ch 1, FS1R on ch 2, S6000 on ch 3–10, internal AWM fills the rest.

No screen. No undo history. No view of the whole song at once. The mental model is the score.

Underlying algorithm — AWM tone generation

Underlying algorithm — AWM tone generation

The QY700’s internal voice is AWM (Advanced Wave Memory): PCM sample playback followed by a per-voice envelope + filter stage. Roads 1996 covers the same architecture as generic sample-playback synthesis; Yamaha’s twist is the ROM library and the low-cost filter.

Signal flow inside one AWM voice:

PCM ROM sample → pitch shifter → LPF → VCA (envelope) → output bus

It is not why you care about the QY700 — Jenkinson routed the interesting voices (TX81Z, FS1R, S6000) externally. You care about the sequencer side:

  • 480 PPQN resolution. Every 1/16 is subdivided into 120 ticks. Per-step timing offsets are quantized to this grid — the finest groove shift available is ~1 ms at 125 BPM.
  • 32 tracks at 48-voice polyphony. Hard ceiling; dropped notes at peak load are a period signature.
  • Step entry is the native mode. Real-time record exists; nobody in Jenkinson’s camp used it.
graph LR A[PCM ROM sample] --> B[Pitch shifter] B --> C[Low-pass filter] C --> D[VCA + envelope] D --> E[Output bus]
Simplified AWM voice — sample playback, filter, envelope. Roads 1996 covers the family.

Slide gd-qy700-07 · gear deep dive

Algorithm continued — constraints as character

Algorithm continued — constraints as character

The constraints below are not bugs. They are why the QY700 sounds like a QY700 when used as a brain.

  • No undo on the chain. Pattern-level changes are live. You edit a pattern; every reference to it updates in place. This is the hardware analogue of single-pass commitment.
  • MIDI jitter at peak load. Running 32 tracks across two MIDI buses drops timing — 1–3 ms jitter on the B bus under load, documented in Yamaha service notes. The groove absorbs it.
  • No per-step probability. Anything stochastic had to live in the external gear (Orville LFOs, FS1R random matrices). The QY700 itself is deterministic.
  • No swing beyond the groove-template page. Which is why Jenkinson hand-drew velocity and micro-timing per step.

The machine forces a working style. Change the box, the style changes. That is why the Ableton translation is not “emulate the QY700” — it is “emulate the discipline.”

Parallel Max for Live — Rhythm and Seed

Parallel Max for Live — Rhythm and Seed

Ableton ships two native MIDI generators that approximate the QY700’s deterministic-but-hand-drawn character when chained:

  • Rhythm. Pattern-generating MIDI device. Seed-based, probability per step, density control. Swap the seed — swap the pattern. Deterministic given a seed, which is the replacement for blind-numeric entry.
  • Seed. Companion device for randomizing pitch/velocity within a constrained palette. Drives Rhythm’s seed input.
  • Velocity Shaper. Per-step velocity curves drawn as a ramp or freehand — literally the QY700 velocity editor reimplemented as a MIDI effect.

The chain for a QY700-style track:

Rhythm (seed=N) → Velocity Shaper (hand-drawn curve) → MIDI track → Instrument

Swap seed=N to mutate the pattern while keeping the velocity contour. This is the Ableton analogue of Jenkinson editing one pattern on the QY700 chain and letting every reference update.

graph LR A[Rhythm seed=N] --> B[Velocity Shaper curve] B --> C[MIDI track] C --> D[Instrument]
Deterministic-but-shaped MIDI chain — the Ableton analogue of blind numeric entry.

Slide gd-qy700-13 · gear deep dive

Exercise B — explore beyond

Exercise B — explore beyond

Four-instance Rhythm-generator patchwork — Jenkinson’s multitimbral posture in modern form.

  1. Four MIDI tracks. Each loads Rhythm → Velocity Shaper → Simpler (or Operator).
  2. Assign a different seed to each Rhythm instance: 17, 42, 99, 203. Do not choose them — type them blind.
  3. Each track’s Simpler gets a different sample — one kick, one snare chop, one FM-ish pluck (Operator preset), one percussion hit.
  4. Each Velocity Shaper gets a different hand-drawn curve. Track 1 ramps up; track 2 ramps down; track 3 is flat with random spikes; track 4 is two steps loud, six steps quiet.
  5. Clip length: 2 bars each, all free-running. Loop them against each other. The collisions are the composition.
  6. Bounce a 32-bar arrangement. No quantize fixes. No re-seeding.

The output should feel like four different players reading four different sheets of music in the same room. That is the QY700 posture. The seeds are the blind numeric entry.

Slide s04 · concept

Half-speed tape bass — Hard Normal Daddy's bionic swing

Half-speed tape bass

The Hard Normal Daddy (1997) bass does not sound programmed. It sounds like a player with a bionic wrist. That is a tape trick.

Jenkinson in Tape Op #89: “I’d run the tape machine at 7½ IPS and pitch everything down an octave, play it at half speed so that the tracking and timing issues would diminish once I put it back to 15 IPS. It completely interferes with human fallibility regarding timing. The whole thing just goes unnaturally tight.”

Tape chain of record: Fostex M80 → Tascam MSR16 ½” 16-track, then a move to Sonic Foundry Vegas, then Nuendo in 2001 — but Jenkinson keeps the “tape-recorder mentality” regardless.

Ableton translation

  1. Halve the project tempo. If the track is at 160 BPM, set it to 80.
  2. Record your bass part — MIDI or a real instrument — an octave down, slowly, against the 80 BPM click.
  3. Return the tempo to 160. Warp the audio Complex Pro, Formants on.
  4. The pitch snaps up an octave. The tracking tightens. The swing remains.

Half-speed tape bass — the diagram

Half-speed tape bass — diagram

The half-speed trick, visualized.

[ STAGE 1 — record at half speed ]
project tempo:    160 BPM         →  set to 80 BPM
bass part:        written in key  →  played an octave DOWN
tape/sequencer:   runs at 7½ IPS  →  (tempo halved, pitch halved)
result on disk:   slow, low bass, tracking errors DIMINISHED

[ STAGE 2 — return to speed ]
project tempo:    80 BPM          →  back to 160 BPM
warp mode:        Complex Pro + Formants ON
pitch:            snaps up one octave to original
timing:           was loose at 80, becomes TIGHT at 160

The math: timing error of ±10 ms at the tracking stage (80 BPM) becomes ±5 ms at playback (160 BPM). Human-player jitter is halved.

The “bionic swing” Jenkinson describes is this halving applied to a player who already has feel. You are not quantizing; you are compressing time around the player’s natural groove. It keeps the swing signature while killing the errors that would expose it as played-in.

Ableton translation steps — see the s04-halfspeed-bass slide for the four-step recipe.

Slide d01 · concept

The Amen break — annotated waveform

The Amen break — annotated waveform

The Amen break is the drum fill from The Winstons’ “Amen, Brother” (1969), 6 seconds, ~136 BPM, 4 bars. Its transients are what every Squarepusher chop session starts from.

The standard pad assignment — MPC-style, 16-pad layout, row-major from bottom-left:

  • Pad 1 (C1): kick 1 (bar 1 beat 1)
  • Pad 2 (C#1): snare 1 (bar 1 beat 2)
  • Pad 3 (D1): kick 2 (bar 1 beat 3)
  • Pad 4 (D#1): snare 2 (bar 1 beat 4)
  • Pad 5–8: bar 2 equivalents + hat
  • Pad 9–12: bar 3 — the famous “pickup” syncopation
  • Pad 13–16: bar 4 — the crash + trailing snare roll

The 4-bar pickup in bars 3–4 is the reason the Amen became the Amen. The first two bars are a standard breakbeat; bars 3–4 are where Gregory C. Coleman plays against the grid. That displacement is where D&B, jungle, and Squarepusher live.

In Ableton: Slice to MIDI at 1/16 → 16 pads per bar → 64 pads total → round-trip through a bitcrush.

Akai S950 + Boss DR-660 — the chop system

Akai S950 + Boss DR-660 — the chop system

The Akai S950 (1988) is a 12-bit rack sampler with a two-line LCD and a knob. The Boss DR-660 (1992) is a compact drum machine with 16 voices, 100 patterns, and a sync-out port. Jenkinson paired them for Big Loada (1997): the S950 holds the Amen; the DR-660 triggers it.

Neither box was expensive. Neither was designed for what Jenkinson did with it. That is the point.

Slide gd-s950-02 · gear deep dive

Annotated tour (1/2) — S950 sample edit

Annotated tour (1/2) — S950 sample edit

Four S950 controls that matter for the chop technique:

  • Truncate. Trim the sample start and end by ear, using the two-line LCD and the numeric wheel. No waveform view. You listen for the transient, mark it, commit.
  • Loop points. Set start and end for sustain loops. Not used for one-shot chops — but used for the held-snare rolls on Hard Normal Daddy.
  • Pitch shift (semitone + fine). Per-keygroup pitch. The S950 repitches by naive sample-rate conversion, which aliases above ±2 semitones. That alias is the D&B signature.
  • Output assign. Eight individual outs on the back. Each Amen chop can be bussed to its own channel on the Mackie, processed independently.

The knob is 0–99 for every parameter. You learn the numbers you want by muscle memory. Blind numeric again.

  1. truncate: Trim sample start/end by ear. Two-line LCD, no waveform view.
  2. loop points: Sustain loops for held-snare rolls on Hard Normal Daddy.
  3. pitch shift: Naive sample-rate conversion — aliases above ±2 semitones. That alias is the signature.
  4. 8 outs: Each chop to its own Mackie channel, parallel processing.

Annotated tour (2/2) — DR-660 pattern grid + sync

Annotated tour (2/2) — DR-660 pattern grid + sync

The DR-660 was a budget drum box that Jenkinson hotwired into a trigger sequencer for the S950.

  • Pattern programming. Step mode or real-time. Jenkinson used real-time tap-in for the feel, then re-quantized to 1/16 or 1/32.
  • Trigger Out (MIDI). Each pad sends MIDI note-on on a configurable channel. Route the DR-660’s MIDI out to the S950’s MIDI in; each pad fires a sample in a keygroup.
  • Sync Out. 24 PPQ clock — the tape-era standard. Slaves the S950’s internal LFOs and the QY700 when used alongside.
  • Accent row. A second-level velocity lane. Three velocity tiers only; Jenkinson used the accent row as a ghost/loud switch, not as nuance.

Limitation as feature: the DR-660 has no shuffle beyond a single global swing. The groove has to come from the pattern itself.

  1. pattern grid: Step mode or real-time tap-in. Jenkinson tap-in then 1/32 quantize.
  2. MIDI out: Each pad fires a note on configurable channel — routes to S950 in.
  3. sync out: 24 PPQ clock — slaves S950 LFOs and QY700 when present.
  4. accent row: Three velocity tiers. Ghost/loud switch, not nuance.

Slide gd-s950-04 · gear deep dive

How Jenkinson chopped the Amen on it — Big Loada method

How Jenkinson chopped the Amen on it — Big Loada method

Reconstructed from Tape Op #89.

  1. Sample the Amen once into the S950. Full 4-bar loop, stereo sum in mono for the S950’s mono ADC path.
  2. Slice by ear. Truncate a copy per hit — kick, snare1, snare2, hat, hat2, rim. Six to ten keygroups.
  3. Pitch-shift a subset by ±2 to ±7 semitones. Each pitched chop gets its own keygroup so you can fire it alongside its dry neighbour.
  4. Reverse half of them. The S950 reverse function is destructive — commit it and save under a new keygroup.
  5. Assign to DR-660 pads via MIDI note mapping. Each pad on the DR-660 fires one keygroup.
  6. Program the pattern. Real-time tap-in, then 1/32 quantize for the rolls.
  7. Record the run in one pass. Jenkinson’s Tape Op claim: “Nobody believes that the tracks on Big Loada were a single pass of me sequencing my Akai S950 from my DR660.”

One take. One master. Onto tape. Done.

Underlying algorithm — 12-bit companded ADC at 7.5–40 kHz

Underlying algorithm — 12-bit companded ADC at 7.5–40 kHz

People mistake the S950 for a low-sample-rate sampler because they think of the SP-1200. The S950’s grit is different and worth being precise about.

  • Sampling rate. User-selectable 7.5–40 kHz. Jenkinson typically ran at the 40 kHz ceiling for pitch headroom; the lo-fi signature lives at the lower settings. Lower rate means lower Nyquist, which is the aliasing knob.
  • Bit depth. 12-bit linear quantization with a µ-law-style (Akai/E-mu 12→8-bit proprietary) companding curve applied before the ADC — not literal ITU G.711 µ-law, but the same shape. Roads 1996 covers the general principle: companding compresses dynamic range before quantization, decompresses on playback, effectively trading linear SNR for a perceptual SNR that is quieter on transients and noisier on low-level sustained content.
  • Reconstruction filter. Fixed anti-alias filter at roughly half the sampling rate, gentle slope.
  • Pitch shift. Naive sample-rate conversion — repitching up an octave doubles playback rate. Aliasing appears on upward shifts past a threshold that’s audible by roughly +4 semitones; downward shifts produce decimation artifacts (not aliasing) that thicken the tone rather than fold it.

The “classic sampler grit” is the companding curve plus the aliasing, not the bit depth in isolation.

graph LR A[Input audio] --> B[µ-law-style companding<br/>Akai/E-mu 12→8-bit proprietary] B --> C[12-bit linear quantize<br/>at 7.5–40 kHz user-selectable] C --> D[RAM] D --> E[Pitch = sample-rate conversion] E --> F[Aliasing above ±2 semitones] F --> G[Anti-alias LPF ~24 kHz, gentle slope] G --> H[Output]
S950 signal path. Companding + naive repitch = the grit, not the bit depth alone. Roads 1996 pp. 21-23.

Parallel Max / third-party — Decimort 2, Sandman Pro, Redux

Parallel Max for Live / third-party devices

For closer period-correct processing:

What else can you do with the S950 chain

What else can you do with the S950 chain

Beyond the Amen:

  • Held-sample stretching. The S950’s pitch-shift aliases; deliberately set the loop tight and run the pitch wheel on the DR-660 to create a sustained, pitched noise bed — the Hard Normal Daddy snare rolls were generated this way.
  • Reversed+pitched layers. Reverse a kick, repitch up 5 semitones, layer under the dry kick on a slight delay. The attack becomes a swell.
  • Inter-keygroup modulation. The S950 has per-keygroup filter envelopes. Assign two keygroups to the same pad via velocity ranges — low velocity triggers one, high velocity triggers the other. Effectively a per-pad round-robin.
  • Resample the S950 output back to tape. Jenkinson did this to flatten the dynamics and print the companding artifacts into a fixed source — then re-sampled that back into the S950 for a double-companded texture. The Metallurgist era is built on this.

All of it translates to Drum Rack + Follow Actions + resample-to-clip.

Exercise B — Metallurgist / Hello Everything territory

Exercise B — Metallurgist / Hello Everything territory

Reverse half the chops + shuffle them + bitcrush. Creates the synthetic-drum terrain of The Metallurgist (2001) and Hello Everything (2006).

  1. Start from the Drum Rack built in Exercise A.
  2. For every second pad (C1, D1, F#1, A1 — any half of the rack), open the Simpler and toggle Reverse on.
  3. Insert an M4L Buffer Shuffler 2.0 before the Drum Rack. Set freeze length 1 bar, divisions 1/16, random mode ON.
  4. Shift the bitcrush harder — Redux Bit Depth 8, add a Saturator (Type: Soft Sine, Drive 6 dB) after the bitcrusher.
  5. Play the same 32-bar pattern from Exercise A through the modified rack.
  6. Capture MIDI. Freeze + Flatten the Shuffler so the re-sequence is printed.

The output should not sound like the Amen. It should sound like someone sampled an Amen, lost it, and reconstructed it from memory. That is the Metallurgist posture.

Slide s07 · concept

Single-pass — program like a player

Single-pass — program like a player

Jenkinson on Big Loada, Tape Op #89: “To this day, nobody believes that the tracks on Big Loada were a single pass of me sequencing my Akai S950 from my DR660.”

And on Hard Normal Daddy: “It’s just something I was trying to do — program like a player, make it have that swing, with a bionic aspect.”

The discipline is the point. One pass. No take-compiling. The sequencer runs; you perform; whatever happens is the record.

Why it works

  • Commit forces identity. A take that cannot be undone develops a signature.
  • Errors become features. The S950’s aliased pitch-shift is a signature because Jenkinson did not fix it.
  • The session ends on time. Single-pass is the 45-minute rabbit hole with the lid screwed on.

Ableton rule for this week

When you run the exercise at the end of this lesson, disable Edit → Undo in your head. Capture MIDI the performance in one take. Bounce. Delete the source MIDI.

If the result is ugly, the result is ugly. Ship it anyway. Week 10 teaches how to mutate ugly results. This week teaches how to make them.

Slide s08 · concept

The Metallurgist — sidechain as composition

The Metallurgist — sidechain as composition

Jenkinson, on “The Metallurgist”: “The bass drum is the sidechain source, and each time it drops, the dynamics for other instruments are being changed to permit a sense of fluidity.”

Read that again. All other instruments. The kick does not just duck the bass. It modulates the dynamics of the pads, the leads, the vocal, the noise — the entire mix becomes one rhythmic envelope.

Sidechain stops being a mix-bus trick and becomes the composition.

Ableton recipe — Metallurgist bus

  1. Route the kick to a return track named kick-trigger, pre-fader, no audio output.
  2. On every other instrument track, insert Compressor → Sidechain ON → Source: kick-trigger.
  3. Depth varies per track. Kick → sub: 6 dB ducking, 5 ms attack, 80 ms release. Kick → pad: 3 dB, 20 ms attack, 200 ms release (breathing). Kick → lead: 1 dB, 50 ms attack, 30 ms release (pumping glint).
  4. Optional: Glue Compressor on the master with sidechain from kick, 1 dB of grab — the whole mix moves together.

The result is a mix that breathes on the one. Nothing in it is mixed; everything in it is composed.

Slide s09 · caveat

Kronecker King — the 1–2ms snare, and what is unconfirmed

Kronecker King — the 1–2ms snare

On Hello Everything (2006), the “Kronecker King” snare is reportedly a Kronecker-delta impulse: a snare sample trimmed to a 1–2 ms transient-only envelope, so only the attack survives. The rest is synthesis built around that impulse.

Caveat, inline: the 1–2 ms figure is KVR and Jenkinson-site community consensus, not a direct Tape Op quote. The general technique — transient-only envelope, synthesize the body — is confirmed by Jenkinson’s broader Go Plastic-onward move to drums “synthesized inside Reaktor” rather than sampled. Flagged in needs_bib:.

Ableton translation

  1. Load a snare into Simpler (One-Shot mode).
  2. Set Fade → 1 ms, Length → 2 ms. Only the click survives.
  3. On the same rack chain, drop a Meld or Operator instance tuned to the same pitch. Trigger them from the same MIDI note.
  4. The click is the transient. The synth is the body. The “snare” is a two-device sandwich.

Why it matters: once you start synthesizing the body, the drum stops being a sample and starts being a voice. The transient stays real; everything else is yours.

Slide s10 · reference

Iambic 9 Poetry — when the Orville fights the grid

Iambic 9 Poetry — when the Orville fights the grid

Iambic 9 Poetry (2004): roughly 78 BPM, D major feel (D/Bm/G/F♯m), live fretless-style bass from Jenkinson, likely live drums for the first half. The primary processor is the Eventide Orville.

Jenkinson is self-taught in DSP via Curtis Roads’ The Computer Music Tutorial (MIT Press, 1996). The Orville patches are his own, written in VSIG, Eventide’s modular-patch editor — programmed on a PC and transferred via SysEx to the hardware.

The track matters here for two reasons:

  • Live instrumentation is not forbidden. The Squarepusher catalog is not all stuttered breaks. Fretless bass through a custom Orville patch is still a Jenkinson track. The signature is the signal path, not the tempo.
  • Your Max for Live patches are your VSIG. Next week (Autechre) we sit with that idea. For now, file it: building the instrument is part of the composition.

For us, this week: stay on the breaks. Iambic 9 Poetry is the reminder that once you can chop an Amen, you can also play over one.
