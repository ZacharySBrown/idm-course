IDM / W3 Week 3 — Simpler, Drum Rack, one-shots

Slide s01 · title

Simpler is already most of the rack

Simpler is already most of the rack

A confession before the device tour.

Nine out of ten drum racks you will build this year are sixteen instances of Simpler. Not Sampler. Not Operator. Not a boutique plug-in. Simpler — the device Ableton ships in Intro, the one with three mode buttons at the top and a waveform that barely fits.

Kieran Hebden, on Tape Notes #140, says he does not use Simpler or Sampler — “I’m all about the mouse.” Fine. He works in a lane where every sound is already a bounced edit. For the rest of us — the ones triggering hits from a pattern — Simpler is the instrument.

So this week we stop skipping past it. Three modes. Drop stemforge one-shots into a Drum Rack. Bounce a four-bar pattern.

Slide s02 · concept

Three modes, one device

Three modes, one device

Simpler has three modes. The tabs sit above the waveform. Same device, three jobs.

  • Classic — melodic play. Pitch tracks the keyboard; Warp keeps time locked at any transposition. Amp envelope, filter, LFO. This is the mode for chromatic chopping — one vocal chunk across sixteen keys.
  • One-Shot — a single sample fires start-to-end on each note. No loop, no release tail. Meant for drum hits. Fade In / Fade Out sliders, Trigger vs Gate, Snap-to-zero-crossing. This is where the kicks live.
  • Slicing — Simpler chops the sample into numbered segments. Each segment maps to its own MIDI note. Transient / Beat / Region / Manual decide where the markers fall.

You pick the mode to match the job. Loading a drum hit into Classic mode and triggering from C3 is a mistake you make exactly once.

[ref: bib:ableton_live_12_manual]

Slide sm01 · concept

Classic mode — chromatic play, warp on

Classic mode — chromatic play, warp on

Classic is Simpler’s chromatic playback mode. The sample is pitched up or down by the incoming MIDI note, interpolating with the warp engine so that timing and pitch can be decoupled.

What actually happens under the hood:

  • Incoming note number sets the ratio against the root key (C3 by default).
  • If Warp is off, Simpler does straight varispeed — pitch and time move together, like pulling tape faster. A vocal chunk an octave up is half as long.
  • If Warp is on — Beats, Tones, Texture, Re-Pitch, or Complex Pro — the warp engine time-stretches so the sample stays locked to project tempo while pitch tracks the keyboard.
  • Amp envelope, filter, LFO, voicing (Mono / Poly / Legato) — standard subtractive front-end.

Use Classic for one-shot melodic sources — vocal chops, flute stabs, pitched hits — any time you want chromatic keyboard play over a pitched fragment.

[ref: bib:ableton_live_12_manual]

Slide sm02 · concept

One-Shot mode — no envelope, just the sample

One-Shot mode — no envelope, just the sample

One-Shot is Simpler’s drum mode. The amp envelope and filter disappear from the GUI. You get start/end markers, fade in, fade out, and a Trigger/Gate switch.

What actually happens under the hood:

  • The note-on event triggers the sample from its start marker. Pitch is fixed — notes do not transpose the sample. That is the whole point. Load a kick, hit any key, get the same kick.
  • Trigger mode: the sample plays start-to-end regardless of note length. Release is ignored.
  • Gate mode: note-off cuts the sample. Rarely what you want for drums.
  • Fade In and Fade Out are linear ramps — typically 0–2 ms in, 5–15 ms out — applied independently of the sample’s envelope.
  • Snap forces start/end markers to the nearest zero crossing, which suppresses the click you’d otherwise hear when truncating mid-cycle.

If you want pitched drum layers, transpose in semitones at the device level. Don’t play them from the keyboard.

[ref: bib:ableton_live_12_manual]

Slide sm03 · concept

Slicing mode — the sample becomes a drum rack

Slicing mode — the sample becomes a drum rack

Slicing mode chops a sample into numbered segments. Each segment gets a MIDI note — by default chromatic from C1 upward. The device turns a single sample into a mini Drum Rack without the Drum Rack.

What actually happens under the hood:

  • On load, Simpler runs the Slice by detector across the sample and writes marker positions.
  • Each marker becomes a slice; notes C1, C#1, D1… trigger slices 1, 2, 3…
  • Playback: Mono (new slice chokes previous), Poly (overlap allowed), Thru (current slice plays until next trigger, ignoring note length).
  • Each slice has its own gain and can be reordered, deleted, or manually repositioned.

When to use it vs. Slice to New MIDI Track — Slicing mode keeps everything inside one Simpler; Slice to MIDI explodes the sample into a full Drum Rack with a Simpler per pad. Use Slicing mode for fast chopping where you won’t add per-slice effects. Use Slice to MIDI when you want per-slice processing chains.

[ref: bib:ableton_live_12_manual]

Slide s03 · concept

One-Shot mode — drum hits without pitch drift

One-Shot mode — drum hits without pitch drift

The parameter list, in the order you actually touch them:

  • Snap — on. Start and end markers jump to zero crossings. Kills the click you would otherwise add.
  • Fade In — 0 to 2 ms on a raw hit. Higher if the sample front-end is brittle.
  • Fade Out — 5 to 15 ms. Prevents the tail pop when a long kick is cut by the next note.
  • Trigger vs Gate — Trigger plays the full sample regardless of note length; Gate stops on note-off. One-shots are Trigger.
  • Transpose — in semitones. The pitch-tune-to-root move from Week 5 lives here.
  • Detune — cents. Use for layering the same hit against itself at ±5 cents.

Warp is available in all three modes. Leave it off for one-shots unless you are deliberately time-locking a hit to tempo.

[ref: bib:ableton_live_12_manual]

Slide d01 · concept

One-Shot envelope — what the fade sliders actually do

One-Shot envelope — what the fade sliders actually do

One-Shot has no amp envelope. What it has are two linear ramps at the ends of the sample — Fade In and Fade Out. They are not ADSR stages; they are micro-ramps to suppress transients at the truncation points.

Read the waveform above:

  • The Fade In ramp climbs from zero to full gain over its millisecond window. At 0 ms you hear the raw sample front — fine for a kick that starts on a zero crossing, bad for a sample snipped mid-cycle.
  • The Fade Out ramp drops from full gain to zero over its window before the End marker. This is the pop-suppressor for long hits that get cut short by the next note.
  • Snap pins Start and End to the nearest zero crossing. With Snap on, the fades are often unnecessary. With Snap off on a sample edited to arbitrary length, the fades do the work.

The numbers from the Reference Manual: Fade In range 0 s–4 s, Fade Out range 0 s–4 s. You will live in the 0–20 ms end of both sliders for drum work.

Slide s04 · concept

Slicing mode — Transient, Beat, Region, Manual

Slicing mode — four ways to place the markers

Slicing mode cuts a sample into segments. The Slice by dropdown decides where the knife lands.

  • Transient — detected attacks. Sensitivity controls aggression. Best for dry drum loops. Messy on wet breaks.
  • Beat — fixed grid. Choices: 1/32, 1/16, 1/8, 1/4, 1/2, 1 bar. For a warped clip, 1/16 is the default for drum loops; 1/8 if the loop is half-time.
  • Region — equal divisions of the sample, independent of tempo. One through sixty-four. For pad textures or non-rhythmic sources.
  • Manual — you place markers with Cmd+I on a selected region. Use when the detector is wrong and you know better.

Playback modes under the slice list: Mono (new slice chokes the previous), Poly (slices overlap), Thru (current slice continues until a new one is triggered). Mono is the safe default for kicks and snares.

[ref: bib:ableton_live_12_manual]

Slide d02 · concept

Slicing algorithms — how to pick the knife

Slicing algorithms — how to pick the knife

Four Slice by options. They are not ranked; they solve different problems. The decision tree above the text dispatches by source type.

  • Transient runs an onset detector over the waveform. Works well on dry, well-recorded drum loops. On anything with reverb tail, bleed, or soft onsets, it misfires — you get slices on tail peaks instead of hits.
  • Beat ignores audio entirely and places markers at fixed note divisions (1/32 through 1 bar). Requires a warped clip. Safe for any warped clip where the grid is honest — 1/16 is the default for drum loops.
  • Region divides the sample into N equal pieces, tempo-blind. Useful for pads, drones, field recordings where rhythm is irrelevant and you want consistent fragment length.
  • Manual — no algorithm. Cmd+I on a selection. The escape hatch when the detector is wrong and you’re certain where the markers should go.

Practical rule: try Beat 1/16 first. Fall back to Transient if the grid is loose. Use Manual to fix mistakes. Region is for non-rhythmic sources.

Slide s05 · concept

Slice to New MIDI Track

Slice to New MIDI Track

The hidden move that most tutorials botch.

Workflow, exact clicks:

  1. Drop an audio clip onto an audio track.
  2. Warp it. Warp mode matters — Beats for drums, Complex Pro for messy breaks. If the transients fall on the grid, Re:warp From Here is fine.
  3. Right-click the clip header → Slice to New MIDI Track.
  4. Dialog appears. Pick Slicing Preset — the default preset is a Drum Rack with Simpler in Slicing mode, one slice per pad, chromatic from C1.
  5. Pick Create one slice per — Warp Marker, Transient, or Beat Division.
  6. Click Create.

Live generates a new MIDI track, loads the Drum Rack, maps every slice to a pad, and writes a MIDI clip that re-plays the original sample one-for-one.

That last clip is your chopping source. Re-sequence it. Do not replay the original pattern.

[ref: bib:ableton_live_12_manual, bib:charnas_dilla_time]

Slide d04 · concept

Slice to New MIDI Track — the full flowchart

Slice to New MIDI Track — the full flowchart

The flowchart above traces what Ableton actually does when you click Slice to New MIDI Track. Nothing is hidden. Everything is reversible.

Under the hood:

  1. Live reads the source clip’s warp markers, transient markers, or beat grid — whichever you selected in the dialog.
  2. Live creates a new MIDI track below the source.
  3. On that track, Live instantiates the Slicing Preset — by default a Drum Rack whose pads each contain a Simpler configured in Slicing mode, one slice per pad, chromatic from C1.
  4. Live writes a MIDI clip on the new track. Each note corresponds to one slice. The notes are placed at the original slice timings, so the new MIDI clip replays the source audio position-for-position.
  5. The source clip is unmuted and unchanged. You now have two tracks making the same sound. Mute the source.

The whole point of the command: you now have a MIDI pattern you can edit. Re-sequence it. That step — not the slicing — is the compositional move.

Slide s06 · concept

Drum Rack — chains are the point

Drum Rack — chains are the point

A Drum Rack is not a sampler. It is a grid of independent device chains with MIDI notes routed to each chain.

The architecture, from the outside in:

  • 128 pads — one per MIDI note. The visible grid is 16 at a time; Page Up / Page Down scrolls octaves.
  • Per pad: a chain. A device list — Simpler, or Simpler plus EQ Eight plus Saturator, or a nested Instrument Rack. No rule says the chain must start with a sampler.
  • Send-Return chains (right column) — shared reverb, delay, parallel comp. Routed per-pad via the S knobs.
  • Choke groups — 1 through 16. Pads in a group silence each other. Closed hat chokes open hat.
  • Output routing — each pad can route to its own audio output.

[ref: bib:ableton_live_12_manual]

Slide d03 · concept

Drum Rack signal path — from MIDI to audio out

Drum Rack signal path — from MIDI to audio out

A Drum Rack is MIDI-in, audio-out, with 128 parallel chains inside. Read the diagram left-to-right.

  • MIDI enters the rack. Every chain receives every note.
  • Each chain has a Receive Note — a single MIDI note (or note range). Only notes matching the receive pass through to that chain’s devices.
  • Inside a chain: the instrument (usually Simpler), optional insert effects (EQ Eight, Saturator, a Utility), optional sends to the Return chains.
  • Return chains (the rack’s own, not the project’s) are shared across all pads — one reverb, one delay, fed by the S-knobs on each pad.
  • Choke groups operate at the MIDI layer, not the audio layer — when pad A fires, any pad in the same choke group receives a note-off. Closed-hat chokes open-hat by muting the source of its sound.
  • Audio sums to the rack’s output, or each pad can route to its own Live audio output for multi-out processing.

The rack is not a sampler. The sampler is on the pad. The rack is the router.

Slide s07 · concept

Drum Sampler — per-pad MIDI tools in Live 12

Drum Sampler — per-pad MIDI tools

Live 12 added Drum Sampler, a Drum Rack pad device with three pattern-shaping tools built into the pad itself. These run on inbound MIDI, not on audio. The sample stays clean.

  • Velocity Shaper — redraws incoming velocities against a curve you draw. Low end becomes ghost notes; peaks become accents. Per pad. Kick pads get steep curves; ghost snare pads get gentle ones.
  • Ornament — adds flams and mordents at a chosen length. 1/64 is the Aphex setting. Turn it on and every snare note becomes a short roll.
  • Time Warp — bends timing within a bar. Pulls hats late on off-beats, pushes the snare early. Per pad. Dilla time baked into the device.

The sample is untouched. All three operate on the inbound MIDI.

[ref: bib:ableton_live_12_manual, bib:charnas_dilla_time]

Slide s08 · caveat

Simpler vs Sampler — pick the warp

Simpler vs Sampler — pick the warp

The short version:

  • Simpler has Warp. Sampler does not. That single fact decides ninety percent of the choice.
  • If the sample needs to stay locked to project tempo — a looped break, a stretched pad, a sliced groove — use Simpler.
  • If the sample needs multi-sample zones, round-robin alternation, crossfade between layers, or key-tracked filter modulation depth — use Sampler.
  • Simpler’s three modes cover drum hits, slicing, and chromatic playback. Sampler covers multisample libraries and velocity-split keymaps.

Practical rule for this course: Drum Racks use Simpler on every pad unless you need round-robin. Chromatic vocal chops use Simpler in Classic mode. Sampler shows up in Week 6 when we layer and round-robin snares.

The marketing positions them as a ladder from Intro to Suite. They are different tools.

[ref: bib:ableton_live_12_manual]

Slide gd-mpc2kxl-01 · gear deep dive

Akai MPC2000XL — the Simpler predecessor

Akai MPC2000XL — the Simpler predecessor

A contradiction to open the deep-dive.

The MPC2000XL is not a Drum Rack. It is not even obviously a sampler — it is primarily a 32-track sequencer that happens to ship with 16-voice 16-bit sampling bolted on. Akai introduced it in 2000, a successor to the MPC2000 (1997), and built a machine that defined the Prefuse 73 / Madlib / Dilla-era hip-hop production aesthetic for the next decade.

Why this deep-dive belongs in Week 3: the workflow Ableton calls Drum Rack + Simpler in Slicing mode + resampling to a new track is the MPC2000XL workflow translated to software. The pads, the chop mode, the Note Repeat, the resample button — Ableton’s modern sampling stack is MPC vocabulary with a DAW under it.

Scott Herren (Prefuse 73) made two albums — Vocal Studies + Uprock Narratives (2001) and One Word Extinguisher (2003) — on exactly this machine plus a pair of turntables into Pro Tools.

[ref: bib:akai_mpc2000xl_manual, bib:prefuse_73_xlr8r]

Annotated tour (1/2) — the pad bank and its banks

Annotated tour (1/2) — the pad bank and its banks

The annotated photo above highlights the right side of the faceplate. Four things matter for the Prefuse workflow.

  • 16 velocity-sensitive pads (labeled A01–A16). Each pad holds one sample or one drum program entry. Pads are the performance surface; the Note Repeat button plus a pad holds a retriggered hit at the current TC (timing correct) value.
  • Bank A / B / C / D buttons — switch the 16 pads to four different banks, for a total of 64 assignable pads per program. Prefuse used banks A and B for vocal slices, C for drum hits, D for resampled stacks.

  • Note Variation slider — the single fader above the pads. Assignable to tune, filter, or layer crossfade per-pad. Herren used this to pitch-shift vocal fragments on the fly during finger-drumming.

Slide gd-mpc2kxl-04 · gear deep dive

The 4-step resampling ladder

The 4-step resampling ladder — sample, chop, pitch, resample

Herren’s workflow, reconstructed from XLR8R and the MPC2000XL manual. The ladder is the move.

Rung 1 — Sample. Record a 2–4 second vocal phrase from a turntable into a single pad. 44.1 kHz, 16-bit, mono. The phrase is too long to be a hit and too short to be a loop.

Rung 2 — Chop. Enter Chop Shop (Trim → F4). Slice by Regions (16 equal divisions) or by Threshold (transient-based). Assign each slice to a pad in Bank A. The vocal phrase is now 16 independent fragments addressable as MIDI notes.

Rung 3 — Pitch. Use the Note Variation slider to re-pitch selected pads ±3 to ±7 semitones. Some up, some down. Finger-drum a new rhythm across the pitched fragments. Record the performance into a MIDI track.

Rung 4 — Resample. Press Resample during playback of the MIDI track. The MPC captures its own output into a fresh sample slot. Now that resampled take is a new 2–4 second phrase — loop back to rung 1.

Two ladder cycles and the source is unrecognizable. Three, and it is no longer yours.

[ref: bib:prefuse_73_xlr8r, bib:akai_mpc2000xl_manual]

Interview receipts — Herren on limitation

Interview receipts — Herren on limitation as aesthetic

Receipts first. The ladder is not folk wisdom — it is how Herren explicitly describes his method.

On the MPC as a constraint, from XLR8R:

“I value the artistic limits imposed by the more primitive technology. I feel they help me focus on the song, instead of the production.”

On his relationship to the Miami/Schematic IDM aesthetic of the early 2000s:

“Trying to fake what they did on computers with the MPC.”

On timing — and this is the one that matters for how you program patterns:

“The way I chop samples is kind of careless; I don’t try to match bpms and shit.”

Translate: Prefuse 73 did not warp. He chopped at whatever the record happened to be at, finger-drummed over it, let the timing drift be the feel, and resampled. The carelessness is the point. A warped grid-locked recreation of a Prefuse beat will sound sterile no matter how accurate the chops are.

On the source material, from the same interview:

“I don’t care where they came from, I don’t care how rare they are — I’ll find a way to make it sound like you haven’t heard it before… If you’re going to chop your shit somehow, or find a small loop, you’re not doing anything.”

The ethic: transformation is the permission.

Underlying algorithm — 44.1kHz, 16-bit, velocity curves

Underlying algorithm — 44.1kHz, 16-bit, the velocity curve

The MPC2000XL is a 44.1 kHz, 16-bit sampler with 16 voices of polyphony. It is not a lo-fi machine in the SP-1200 sense — the specs are CD-quality. What shapes the sound is the velocity curve and the sample-playback algorithm, not the bit depth.

From the manual and from Akai’s service documentation:

  • Bit depth: 16-bit linear PCM, 44.1 kHz sample rate, no companding. Contrast with the SP-1200 at 12-bit 26.04 kHz companded — the MPC2000XL is already “clean” by hip-hop-sampler standards.
  • Playback engine: sample-and-hold interpolation by default; optional linear interpolation when pitched. Extreme re-pitch (+12 or more semitones) introduces aliasing you can hear — which is why 16-Levels chops at ±6 semitones stay musical while wider spreads get metallic.
  • Velocity curve: each pad has its own velocity-to-amp curve and velocity-to-filter curve. Default is linear; Herren and most MPC users bent the curve so that light touches produced ghost hits and hard touches produced accent hits — a three-tier dynamic map inside a single pad.
  • Timing correction: the global TC (timing correct) value quantizes incoming pad hits to the nearest 1/8, 1/16, 1/32, 1/16T, 1/32T, or Off. “Off” is the Prefuse setting. Every other setting grid-locks the performance.

Algorithm constraints — why the MPC sounds like the MPC

Algorithm constraints — why the MPC sounds like the MPC

The machine has three constraints that became aesthetic signatures. Every modern re-creation has to choose which of them to preserve and which to skip.

1. The TC swing curve. TC 1/16 with swing values 50% (straight) to 75% (maximum shuffle). Swing is applied to every other 1/16 note — the “and” counts are pushed later. The curve is not Dilla’s. It is a fixed exponential mapping; Dilla (MPC3000) had a different chip revision with a subtly different curve. Herren (MPC2000XL) usually worked at 54–58% swing.

2. Sample memory as a ceiling. The base machine ships with 2 MB of RAM — 12 seconds of mono 16-bit. Expanded to the full 32 MB via EXM-E3 SIMMs, you get about 6 minutes mono or 3 minutes stereo. This forced short samples — a 2-second vocal phrase, not a 20-second loop. The short-phrase-into-chop method is not a choice; it is what fit in RAM.

3. No time-stretch, no warp. The MPC2000XL cannot time-stretch a sample while preserving pitch. If you record a 95 BPM break and your track is at 90 BPM, you either pitch the whole sample down (which slows it), or you chop it and re-trigger at the new tempo. The “chop and re-trigger” workflow is forced by the absence of warp.

Ableton’s Simpler breaks constraint 3 via Beats warp. Students who want the Prefuse sound should deliberately turn warp off.

Parallel Max for Live devices

Parallel Max for Live devices — if you want the feel without the bridge

Three M4L devices worth installing for the MPC-like experience inside Live.

  • Buffer Shuffler 2.0 (native Ableton M4L) — drop it on an audio track, it captures the last N bars, and on each new bar it re-orders the 1/16 slices according to a probability pattern. This is the Prefuse chop-and-rearrange move automated. Useful for instant drum reshuffles over a static break.
  • K-Devices ESQ — step sequencer with per-step probability, per-step swing, and per-step retrigger. Behaves like an MPC sequence grid with the swing and probability sliders the MPC never had. Pair with a Drum Rack to program hip-hop patterns by step, not by recording.
  • Iftah’s M4L suite or Dillon Bastan’s Markov Variations — for the generate-and-audition feel that the MPC’s step-edit mode does manually. Generate a MIDI pattern, audition it against the kit, commit if it works.

Alternatively: buy a secondhand MPC2000XL. They are $500–$900 on reverb.com as of 2026. The workflow is different in ways you cannot replicate — tactile pads, the Note Repeat button as muscle memory, the forced linearity of a hardware screen. The argument for software is parity; the argument for hardware is that you will actually finish songs because there are no menus.

Creative uses beyond chopping vocals

Creative uses beyond chopping vocals

The MPC2000XL was designed for hip-hop but its architecture supports more than the Prefuse workflow. Four uses worth knowing.

  • Sample-and-hold synthesis. Record 500 ms of a field recording into a pad. Set the pad to loop across its full length. Play chromatically across 16-Levels. You now have a wavetable-adjacent pitched instrument built from one captured sound. Aphex Twin’s Druqks-era textures used this on a different Akai (S950), but the move transfers.
  • Beat-sliced drum programming with no MIDI input. Sample a 1-bar break. Chop by threshold. Drop the slices onto pads. Sequence a new 16-step pattern by entering notes in Step Edit mode — no tapping. The tracker-adjacent workflow that makes Autechre-style patterns possible on a non-computer.
  • Resampling as freeze. The MPC has no track freeze. Resampling the output of a CPU-heavy track — layered samples with filter sweeps — gives you a single flat sample you can treat as source. This is how you stack 60 pads of processing into a machine with 16 voices.
  • Live finger-drumming over DJ sets. Bank A drums, Bank B one-shots, Bank C vocals, Bank D FX. A MIDI clock in from a turntable rig keeps sequencer-based patterns locked to the set tempo. This is what Herren does on stage.

[ref: bib:akai_mpc2000xl_manual, bib:prefuse_73_xlr8r]

Parallel modern workflows — the method outlived the metal

Parallel modern workflows — who still works this way

The MPC2000XL workflow survived. It migrated. Three current examples.

  • Madlib on the SP-303 and later Akai MPC Live II. Same ladder — sample, chop, pitch, resample — on different hardware. His Shades of Blue (2003) and the later Madvillainy sessions are entirely this method. The takeaway is not get the same hardware; it is sample → chop → resample is the unit of work.
  • Knxwledge and the MPC Renaissance. The Renaissance is a hardware controller for MPC software on a computer; the workflow is identical to the 2000XL with 2024 disk space. Knxwledge’s 2010s output is MPC-native beats resampled into Ableton for arrangement.
  • The Push 3 standalone. Ableton shipped a standalone Push in 2023 that runs Live’s sampling stack on embedded hardware. It is a Drum Rack with pads. The ladder works — sample, slice to MIDI, resample to audio track, re-slice. Push 3 is the 2026 equivalent of what the MPC2000XL was in 2001: a hardware-first sampler-sequencer with a screen you are allowed to ignore.

The throughline: the method outlived the metal. If you understand the ladder, the box is interchangeable.

Exercise A — replicate the Prefuse ladder in Ableton

Exercise A — replicate the Prefuse ladder in Ableton

Deliverable. One .wav file. 8 bars. Project tempo. A single vocal source taken through two ladder cycles.

Steps:

  1. Pick one vocal phrase from the stemforge w03-express-oneshots output — ideally a 2-4 second chunk with syllabic content (not a sustained note).
  2. Drop the phrase onto an audio track. Do not warp it. This is the Prefuse constraint — timing drift is the feel.
  3. Right-click the clip → Slice to New MIDI Track. Slice by Region, 16 slices. Call the new track Chop 1.
  4. Program 4 bars of MIDI across the 16 pads. Do not re-play the original phrase; rearrange. Use unusual pad orders; repeat fragments; leave gaps.
  5. On a new audio track, set the input to Chop 1. Record-arm. Play the 4-bar pattern and resample to a fresh audio clip.
  6. Drop that resampled clip onto another new audio track. Slice to New MIDI Track again — this is ladder rung 2. Call it Chop 2.
  7. Program another 4 bars on Chop 2. Now you are chopping a chop.
  8. Bounce all 8 bars from both Chop 1 and Chop 2 sequences as the final deliverable. File: w03_<yourname>_prefuse_ladder.wav.

Time-box: sixty minutes.

[ref: bib:prefuse_73_xlr8r, bib:ableton_live_12_manual]

w03_<yourname>_prefuse_ladder.wav — 8 bars, one vocal source, two ladder cycles, no warp.

Exercise B — Buffer Shuffler + Drum Rack

Exercise B — go beyond: Buffer Shuffler + Drum Rack

Deliverable. One .wav file. 16 bars. A break that evolves — same source material, Buffer Shuffler re-ordering on a per-bar basis, plus a programmed Drum Rack layer underneath.

Steps:

  1. Pick one drum loop from stemforge output — not a one-shot, a full bar of percussion. Drop it onto an audio track. Warp it to project tempo with Beats mode.
  2. Add Buffer Shuffler 2.0 (Ableton’s native M4L device) to that track. Set the buffer to 1 bar. Set re-order density to moderate — about 40% shuffle. Let it run; now the break reshuffles itself every bar.
  3. On a second MIDI track, load a Drum Rack from the StemForgeLoader’s w03-express-oneshots output. Keep only kick, snare, hat. Program a simple 4-bar pattern.
  4. Record 16 bars of both tracks together to a new audio track. The Buffer Shuffler layer will produce a different reshuffle on each bar — 16 distinct bars from one source clip.
  5. On the bounced audio, Slice to New MIDI Track with Transient slicing. You now have a MIDI clip that re-plays the 16 reshuffled bars. Edit it — remove half the notes, double-time a section, whatever.
  6. Bounce the edited version. File: w03_<yourname>_shuffler_remix.wav.

Time-box: ninety minutes. If the Buffer Shuffler feels random, it is random — that is the device. Audition until a bar lands.

StemForgeLoader.amxd — the loader does the plumbing

StemForgeLoader.amxd — the loader does the plumbing

This is also the dedicated Max for Live lesson of the course. One device, one job.

What the loader does when you drag it onto a MIDI track and press its button:

  • Auto-creates seven template tracks — SF Source, Drums Rack, Bass Rack, Vocals Rack, Other Rack, Chromatic Simpler, Beat Chop Simpler.
  • Each Rack template arrives with its own effect chain wired — EQ, gate, compressor, a send to a shared reverb.
  • For a song manifest, the loader duplicates the Drums Rack template, renames it Drums Rack | <song>, and walks the manifest — each one-shot into its Simpler at chromatic C1 upward.
  • Templates stay untouched; duplicates are what you edit.

Screenshots deferred to Wave 5. The loader is in the stemforge repo under m4l/. For this lesson we will point it at the w03-express-oneshots output.

Exercise — four bars, three drums, one bounce

Exercise — four bars, three drums, one bounce

Deliverable. One .wav file. Four bars. Project tempo. Drums only, from the kit you just built.

Steps:

  1. Run the stemforge recipe w03-express-oneshots against grooves/Express Yourself.wav. Output lands in build/audio/stemforge-renders/w03/express_oneshots/. LarsNet classifies hits as kick / snare / hat / tom / cymbal.

  4. Program 4 bars of MIDI. Any pattern. Your pattern, not Charles Wright’s.
  5. On each of the three pads, open the Drum Sampler and draw a Velocity Shaper curve — kick steep, snare medium, hat gentle.
  6. Bounce the track. File: w03_<yourname>_4bar.wav. Mute the source chain. Ship.

Time-box: forty-five minutes.
